## A familiar scene

It's an ordinary evening. Meat and potatoes, or a burger and fries, or eggs on toast — the plate everyone eats, the plate that feels like the definition of "a proper meal." An hour later you're heavy, a little bloated, oddly sleepy, maybe reaching for something fizzy. Not sick. Just... not light. You've felt it a thousand times and filed it under "that's just eating."

Trophology — the science of food combining — asks a quieter question: what if it isn't just eating? What if the heaviness is information — your stomach telling you it was handed a job it can't do cleanly, not because the food was bad, but because of what was put *next to* what?

That's what this course is for: to give you a small, testable set of rules about which foods sit well together and which fight in the stomach — and, more importantly, to teach you to check every rule against your own body instead of taking anyone's word for it.

One honest note before anything else: **this course is not medical advice, and we are not doctors.** It's a careful introduction to a food-combining tradition — drawn mainly from Daniel Reid's *The Tao of Health, Sex and Longevity* and, through him, Herbert Shelton's *Food Combining Made Easy* and the Taoist Yin/Yang diet. We take it seriously, the way any non-mainstream idea deserves to be engaged — at its most rigorous, or not at all — and we'll be equally serious about where the evidence is thin.

## The core

### The stomach is not a blender

Picture what you were probably taught: food goes down, acid dissolves it, out come nutrients. One tank, one solvent. If that were true, combining wouldn't matter — throw anything in, it all melts the same.

But the stomach doesn't work like one tank of universal solvent. It reads what's arriving and secretes *different, nearly opposite* juices depending on the food. This is the whole hinge of trophology, so it's worth stating precisely:

- To digest **concentrated protein** — meat, fish, eggs, cheese — the stomach pours out **acid** (hydrochloric acid) and the enzyme **pepsin**, which only works in a strongly acidic bath. It needs to stay acidic for hours.
- To digest **starch** — bread, potato, rice — the work starts up in the mouth: chewing releases **ptyalin** (salivary amylase), an **alkaline** enzyme, and starch wants an *alkaline* setting to keep going.

Acid for one, alkaline for the other. In chemistry class you learned what happens when you pour acid and alkali together in equal measure: they neutralize into something weak and watery — the same reason people reach for alkaline baking soda to kill "acid" indigestion.

### Fermentation and putrefaction — the failure mode

Here's the tradition's account of what follows a cancelled-out meal. When protein isn't broken down promptly by acid and pepsin, the gut's ever-present bacteria get to it first, and protein **putrefies** (rots). When starch and sugar aren't handled in their alkaline window, bacteria **ferment** them. The by-products — gas, acids, general foulness — are, in this view, the immediate source of the heaviness, bloating, gas, heartburn and sluggishness so many people treat as the normal price of eating.

Two things matter about how we'll use these words:

- **This is a claim about *comfort and digestion*, not a disease diagnosis.** Reid and the older writers go much further — pinning serious illnesses on years of "putrefaction." We are deliberately *not* repeating those leaps as fact (more on that in a moment). In this course, "fermentation and putrefaction" name the everyday, felt failure — the bloat, not a diagnosis.
- **It's exactly the kind of thing you can feel.** You can't measure your own pepsin, but you absolutely can notice, tonight versus next week, whether a differently-combined meal leaves you lighter. That's the whole game.

### The honest stance — read this part slowly

If the "different juices" story were the whole truth, mixed meals would reliably wreck everyone, and they plainly don't — most people eat meat and potatoes their whole lives. So here's the honest note this course refuses to skip:

**Mainstream physiology does not endorse strict food combining.** A healthy digestive system produces acid and enzymes in the stomach *and* a flood of alkaline pancreatic enzymes (including plenty of amylase) in the small intestine, and it handles mixed meals routinely. The controlled trials that exist — food-combining ("Hay") diets tested against balanced, calorie-matched diets — have **not** found a combining advantage for weight loss. And the field's most dramatic historical claims (that mixed meals cause specific diseases, that pasteurized milk sterilized generations of cats and therefore of people, that grains "aren't meant for humans," that the average person carries pounds of rotting meat) are dated, contested, or simply unsupported. **We will not repeat them as established, and neither should you.**

So why teach it at all? Because underneath the polemic sits something real and testable: **many people report that simpler, better-combined meals leave them noticeably more comfortable** — less bloated, less heavy, steadier energy. That subjective comfort is not a small thing when you feel it every day, and it costs nothing to test.

Our position is the one this whole site is built on: **don't believe — test.** Treat every rule in this course as a hypothesis. Run it in your own kitchen, on your own body, and write down what actually happens — including the times nothing changes. Let your own recorded experience, not Reid's enthusiasm and not a skeptic's eye-roll, decide what food combining is worth at your table. Is it uncomfortable to build a course on a contested idea? Yes. Honestly — that's the only reason it's worth doing carefully.

### One boundary, stated once

Food combining is a discipline for the **adults' table** and for ordinary, healthy eating. A few things it is not:

- It is **not a treatment** for anything. Persistent digestive pain, blood, unexplained weight loss, trouble swallowing, or any symptom that worries you belongs to a doctor — not to a change of menu.
- It does **not** override the Health basics: real, whole, minimally-processed food, enough sleep, movement. No combining rule outranks those; they're the ground this stands on.
- For a **baby or young child**, don't impose adult combining rules. A young child eats on their own rhythm and appetite, and a pediatrician leads on infant feeding. Chapter 5 comes back to this.

## Worked example

Meet the two things you'll build across all 5 chapters — the course's spine:

1. **The digestion journal** — a paper notebook where meals and how you felt afterward get dated entries. This is your evidence, and it starts tonight.
2. **The Table Map** — a single sheet you'll post on the fridge, growing over chapters 2–4 into a plain chart of what combines with what, with your family's own go-to meals rebuilt to fit. Tonight it's a blank sheet and a magnet.

Here's what a first journal entry can look like — an ordinary dinner, observed without fixing anything yet:

> **5 July, 19:30 — dinner.** Chicken thighs, roast potatoes, a bit of salad. Ate until quite full. Mood: fine, hungry going in.
> **~21:00 (90 min after):** Heavy, a bit bloated. Sleepy earlier than usual. Slightly thirsty. Didn't want to move much.

Nothing clever, no conclusions, no rules applied — just protein-plus-starch as you already eat it, and the honest after-picture. That "heavy and sleepy at 90 minutes" is your *baseline*. In the chapters ahead you'll change one thing at a time and watch this line move.

## Practice

Tonight, away from any screen — 20–30 minutes:

1. Find a **paper** notebook you'll actually keep, and a pen that lives with it. On page one write the date and the words "Digestion journal."
2. Eat your normal dinner — change nothing yet. Note what was on the plate and roughly how full you got.
3. Set a quiet reminder for **60–90 minutes later**, and when it goes off, write two or three lines: heavy or light? bloated, gassy, or comfortable? sleepy or steady? thirsty? This is your baseline entry — the thing every later chapter is measured against.
4. Put a blank sheet on the fridge and title it "Table Map." That corner of the kitchen is now the course's home.
5. Name the cost honestly, in writing: this course asks for about 1–2 hours per chapter and the small discomfort of changing familiar habits for a few weeks. Real things have a price; we start by seeing it.

**How to know it worked:** your journal has a dated entry that describes a real meal and how your body actually felt afterward — observation, not theory, and not a single rule applied yet.
