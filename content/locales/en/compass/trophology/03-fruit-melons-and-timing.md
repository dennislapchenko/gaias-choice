## A familiar scene

The big dinner is done, everyone's full, and out comes the fruit bowl — or the cake, or the "just a little something sweet." It feels like the natural close to a meal, the reward. And for a lot of people it's also the exact moment the evening turns heavy: the fullness that tips into bloat, the burp that tastes faintly of fermentation, the sense that the meal is now just *sitting* there.

Chapter 2 handled which foods clash side by side. This chapter is about a different dimension entirely — not *what's next to what*, but *what comes when*. Because that innocent after-dinner fruit is, in trophology's account, one of the most reliably uncomfortable things you can do, and the fix isn't to give up fruit. It's to move it.

## The promise

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- explain why acids and sugars sabotage a meal they're eaten *on top of*;
- place fruit correctly in the day — before, not after — and say why;
- apply the melon rule ("alone, or leave it alone") and know what makes melons special;
- add the timing rules to your Table Map as a small, memorable set.

## The core

### The empty-stomach rule

Recall the "different juices" idea from chapter 1 — before reading on, try to remember why starch needs its alkaline window. Got it? Ptyalin, the alkaline enzyme, starts starch digestion in the mouth and needs an alkaline setting to continue.

Now the new fact: **any acid suspends ptyalin.** A teaspoon of vinegar, a squeeze of lemon, the acid of an orange — enough to shut down salivary starch digestion. And **sugars are digested neither in the mouth nor the stomach** — they're meant to pass straight through to the small intestine. Both facts point the same way: acids and sugars don't belong *in the middle of* a meal built on protein or starch. They need an empty road.

That's the empty-stomach rule, and it turns *timing* into a lever as strong as combination:

- Fast foods (fruit, melon) want an **empty stomach** so they can pass through quickly to where they're actually digested.
- Slow foods (protein, starch) hold the stomach busy for hours.
- Put a fast food *behind* a slow one and it gets trapped, waiting, and — with bacteria always present — ferments instead of passing. That's the after-dinner-fruit heaviness, mechanism and all.

### Fruit is its own meal

So where does fruit go? **Before a meal on an empty stomach, or as a meal of its own — never as dessert piled on top.** Practically:

- Fruit first thing in the morning, on an empty stomach, is close to ideal — it passes through and you eat breakfast proper 20–30 minutes later.
- Fruit as a mid-afternoon snack, a couple of hours after lunch and before dinner, when the stomach has cleared.
- A whole meal of fruit — a fruit breakfast or a fruit lunch — is a genuine, clean option.

And the rule that surprises people: **skip the after-dinner fruit and the after-dinner sweet.** Not because a bit of sugar is a sin, but because *on top of a full stomach* it's the position that reliably ferments. Reid's own concession is worth keeping: if you truly crave cake, it does far less damage eaten *alone*, as its own occasion, than crowning a big meal with it. The sweet isn't the crime; the stacking is.

This is also where a light acid rule lives: a strongly acidic dressing (lots of vinegar or lemon) works against a starch meal, and acid fruit works against both starch and protein. A little lemon on fish, a light vinaigrette on a salad beside a protein — minor. Dousing a plate of pasta in vinegar, or finishing a starch meal with an orange — that's the clash worth avoiding.

### Melons alone

Melon gets its own line in every food-combining book, and it's the easiest rule to remember: **eat melons alone, or leave them alone.**

Why the special treatment? Melon is almost entirely water and sugar and asks for essentially no stomach digestion — it wants to pass through faster than *any* other food, straight to the small intestine. Eat it on an empty stomach and it's gone in minutes, clean. Eat it after — or even with — other food, and it's stuck at the very back of the queue behind everything slower, fermenting while it waits. More than any fruit, melon punishes bad timing. So it's not a snack to graze *alongside* a meal; it's a small meal, or a first thing, on its own.

Here's the whole timing layer as one small table for the fridge:

| Food | When | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Fruit (apple, berries, etc.) | Empty stomach: morning, or between meals | Passes fast; ferments if trapped behind a meal |
| Melon | Alone, on an empty stomach — never with/after food | Digests fastest of all; worst punished by delay |
| Sweets / dessert | As their own occasion, not on top of a meal | Sugars ferment when held up in a full stomach |
| Strong acids (vinegar, lemon) | Not on a starch meal; light amounts only | Acid suspends starch's alkaline enzyme |

## Worked example

Take the classic uncomfortable evening from chapter 1's baseline family and fix it *with timing alone* — no change to the food itself:

- **Before:** big dinner, then a bowl of grapes and melon "to feel healthy," then heavy for the whole evening.
- **After:** the melon moves to *tomorrow morning*, eaten alone on an empty stomach before breakfast. The grapes become a *mid-afternoon* snack, a couple of hours after lunch. Dinner ends with nothing piled on top — maybe a herbal tea.

Then check it in the journal, holding it against the earlier heavy nights:

> **15 July, morning.** Half a small melon, alone, before anything else. Gone light within the hour, genuinely hungry for breakfast at 8:30.
> **19:30 dinner** — protein + veg, nothing sweet after.
> **~21:00:** noticeably lighter evening than the fruit-after-dinner nights. No fermenting-burp feeling.

Same fruit, same dinner, moved on the clock. If your journal agrees over a week, you've learned that *when* is a lever you own for free.

## Practice

This week, in the kitchen:

1. **Move your fruit.** For one week, eat all fruit either first thing in the morning on an empty stomach or as a between-meals snack — and none straight after a meal.
2. **Try one melon-alone morning.** Eat some melon by itself, wait, and note in the journal how fast the lightness comes compared to fruit eaten with other things.
3. **End one big dinner with nothing on top** — no fruit, no sweet — and journal the evening against a night you finished with dessert.
4. **Add a timing box to the Table Map.** Copy the small "when / why" table above onto the fridge sheet, under your protein/starch columns from chapter 2.

**How to know it worked:** your Table Map now carries a timing box as well as meals, and your journal has at least one honest side-by-side of fruit-before versus fruit-after — in your own words, whichever way it fell.
