## A familiar scene

The kit is complete. Ten labeled tubes, the index card with its prices, the journal beside it — grids filled, one repertorization grid you're quietly proud of. And then the real exam arrives, the way it always does: Tuesday, 2:14 a.m. A child crying in the dark, one cheek blazing, your own pulse drumming its solo. And here is the gap this chapter closes: you know the pieces — the grid, the portraits, the stool — but nobody has yet told you the order of operations when your hands are shaking. Thermometer first or tube first? Observe first or comfort first? And at what point is this not a home case at all?

A kit without a protocol is a box of good intentions. This chapter turns yours into a system with a fixed order — one you can run half-asleep. Because half-asleep is exactly when you will need it.

## The promise

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- run the **home protocol** in its fixed order — red flags → grid → stool → one dose of 30C → watch — without inventing steps at 2 a.m.;
- recite the formal **red-flag list** from memory and say why it stands at the front;
- place any case on its **age rung** — infant, child, adult — and name what changes on each;
- open the **situation table** for the complaints family life actually brings, from a teething night to a wasp sting.

## The core

### The red flags — now formal

Chapter 1 asked you to burn a list in and promised that chapter 6 would make it formal. First, the retrieval test — from memory, no scrolling back: how many of its items can you say out loud right now? Count them. Now here is the whole list, in its formal home — and before this chapter ends it will be copied out in your own handwriting:

A doctor — immediately, not "after we try something first" — for:

- any fever in an infant under three months;
- difficulty breathing;
- signs of dehydration;
- a stiff neck with fever;
- unusual drowsiness or unresponsiveness;
- seizures;
- severe or worsening pain;
- anything that is getting worse despite care;
- anything that frightens you in your gut.

The last item is not decoration. A parent's "something is wrong" is a sensor that the tradition and pediatrics respect equally; if it rings, that's a flag. And chapter 1's exact sentence, repeated here because it is load-bearing: **"A remedy must never buy delay."** While you observe pictures and recall portraits, the clock does not stop. A flag means the rest of this chapter does not apply: not a dose but a doctor, and not after deliberation — now.

Why does the list come before observation? Because observation is absorbing. The grid is interesting; the fork questions are interesting; and interest eats minutes. The gate exists so that the one question that outranks the whole method gets asked while your attention is still fresh: *does this night belong to a doctor?* Only a clean "no" opens the rest of the protocol.

### The home protocol

Two retrieval checks before the assembly. From chapter 3, the **rule of minimum** — before reading on, recall its clauses (one remedy; dose is frequency; stop on response; and what to do when nothing happens). From chapter 5, the **three-legged stool** — which three legs hold up a choice? If both answers came back, you are ready: the protocol adds almost nothing to what you know. It puts it in order.

1. **The gate.** The red-flag list, all of it. A flag → the doctor; end of chart.
2. **Observe.** The chapter 2 grid: local, better/worse-from, time, generals, mind, context.
3. **Match.** The stool: at least three confirming legs, the best of them a mental or a general.
4. **One dose of 30C.** One remedy. The journal open: what was given, when, against which picture.
5. **Watch.** No immediate repeat — dose is frequency, and the next dose has to be earned by the picture, not by your worry.

One boundary line, said here in passing and formally in chapter 10: anything chronic, anything constitutional, any potency above 30C — professionals' territory. The home protocol doesn't go there at all.

### The age rungs

The same five moves, but the ground under them changes with age.

**The infant — under about twelve months.** Any question about a baby this young resolves the same way: the pediatrician first. Not "in parallel," not "if it doesn't pass by morning" — first. On this rung homeopathy is comfort *alongside* the doctor, never instead of one; and remember the gate's very first item — any fever under three months is a flag all by itself, full stop. If that sounds strict: it is, and it is exactly this strictness that lets the rest of the chapter be calm.

**The toddler and child.** Self-limiting complaints only — teething nights, the ordinary cold, the bumped knee. Two ideas lead here, and neither comes from homeopathy. **Rhythm:** a young child runs on rhythm the way a house stands on a foundation — meals, sleep, the same lullaby at the same hour. A teething night is a broken rhythm, and care is what restores it: warmth, arms, quiet, the familiar order of things. The remedy is one tool on that shelf, not the shelf. **Attunement:** the crying *is* communication. Remember chapter 2's line — case-taking and attunement turn out to be the same gesture? This is where it comes home. A child who cannot say "worse toward nine, better carried fast" is saying it anyway, all night, to whoever watches closely enough — and being received that closely is itself part of what settles her.

**The adult.** Same logic, same fence. You are the easiest patient in the house, because you can report modalities in words: "worse when I stand up," "no thirst all day," "leave me alone." A case that takes an evening of watching with a toddler, an adult hands you in three sentences. (The hard part with adults is different: they minimize. "I'm fine" — you will meet the portrait that says exactly that in the table below.)

### The situation table — the kit meets real life

The table is this chapter's payload, and one sentence above it outranks every row: **these are the tradition's classic pictures — candidates for your stool, not prescriptions — and the red flags overrule the entire table.** Every "first candidate" still has to win its three legs on tonight's actual picture; the fork question speeds the match up, it does not replace it.

| Situation | First candidates | The fork question | Doctor if |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bumps & bruises | *Arnica*; *Ledum* when the spot is cold, pale and eased by cold | says "I'm fine" and the bed feels too hard — or a cold puffy spot better from cold? | pain severe or growing; a blow to the head; won't use the limb |
| Teething night | *Chamomilla* vs *Pulsatilla* | fury that hurls the offered toy — or tears that melt in gentle arms? | fever beyond the mild, unusual drowsiness — or your gut says "this isn't teeth" |
| Sudden fever, first hours | *Aconitum* vs *Belladonna* | fear and restlessness after cold dry wind or a fright — or red-hot, throbbing, glassy-eyed? | **an infant under three months — a red flag, full stop**; stiff neck, rash, seizure |
| Slow flu-ache | *Gelsemium* vs *Bryonia* | droopy, heavy-lidded, dull — or "don't move me, don't touch me"? | difficulty breathing; anything worsening despite care |
| A cold, by stage | *Aconitum* (first hours) · *Nux vomica* (chilly, irritable middle) · *Pulsatilla* (late stage: thick, bland, weepy) | which day of the cold is it — and who has the person become? | ear pain joins in; breathing labored; running past its natural course and worsening |
| Upset stomach after excess | *Nux vomica*; *Pulsatilla* after rich, fatty food | too much of everything — food, coffee, work — or one crêpe too many? | signs of dehydration; severe pain; blood |
| Stings & bites | *Apis* vs *Ledum* | a hot rosy puff worse from warmth — or a cold pale puff better from cold? | **any swelling of face, mouth or throat, any struggle to breathe — emergency, now**; known allergy |
| Earache | *Pulsatilla* vs *Chamomilla* vs *Belladonna* | weepy and clinging — furious — or red-hot and throbbing? | **ear pain sees a doctor within 24–48 h regardless** — sooner with fever or discharge; an infant goes straight to the pediatrician |
| Overexertion, "everything aches" | *Arnica* | the whole body bruised-sore, the bed "too hard," "I'm fine, leave me be"? | one spot localizing and worsening rather than a diffuse ache |
| Sleepless from overwork or excitement | *Nux vomica*; *Chamomilla* for the child sleepless in pain-fury | an overwound adult — work, coffee, screen — or a child in furious pain? | sleeplessness that is chronic: not an acute case, and not home territory |
| Anticipatory dread before an event | *Gelsemium* | limp, trembly, dull, "gone stupid" before the exam or the stage? | — (not an illness; dread that runs a life belongs with a professional) |

Two rows deserve saying out loud as well as printing. The **earache** row: ear pain is a doctor's case within 24–48 hours *every time* — the candidates above are comfort alongside that appointment, never a substitute for it, and with an infant it is the pediatrician straight away. And the **cold by stage** row hides a pattern worth pausing on: a cold is rarely a stranger. Same family, same season, the same cough finding the same child — the table answers tonight, but it quietly raises a bigger question: **why do the same colds keep coming back?** The answer doesn't live in the kit at all; it lives below the case, in the ground a complaint grows from — and that is chapter 8's territory. Carry the question.

## Worked example

Imagine a teething night — our course's toddler in the role you've watched since chapter 2 (a reminder: she and M. are hypothetical course characters; your night will be your own). 11:20 p.m., crying that saws through the wall.

**The gate, out loud, by the card.** Fever? 37.2 — and she is a year and a half, not an infant under three months. Breathing — free. Drinking — just demanded a sip. Neck — soft; she's turning her head to glare at you. Drowsy? The opposite. Seizures — no. Pain — teething-familiar, not building like an avalanche. Worsening despite care? It's the first hour; not established. And the gut check, honestly taken: tired — yes; alarmed — no. The gate opens.

**The grid, in the half-dark.** Local: gums, fist crammed in the mouth, one cheek scarlet and the other pale. Better/worse: carrying helps — but only at a fast walk; slow rocking enrages. Offered the teething ring, demands it, hurls it across the room. Time: started around eleven. Generals: hot head, takes sips. Mind: fury. Not tears — a shriek, back arching.

**The fork.** Tears or fury? *Pulsatilla* is the toddler you know from chapter 2's cold — weepy, melting in gentle arms. Tonight is nothing like that. Fury, hurling, "carry me fast or else" — that is *Chamomilla*, and count the legs: a mental (the fury), a modality (better from fast carrying), a local with a peculiar attached (one cheek red, one pale — the classic odd detail of this picture). The stool stands.

**One dose.** *Chamomilla* 30C, 11:40 p.m. A journal line written by the nightlight: what, when, against which picture.

**Watch.** And two honest endings, both of which you should be able to write before they happen:

- **It settles.** Twenty minutes and the shriek loses its engine; she goes heavy on your shoulder; asleep by midnight. Stop. No repeat — the picture is moving on its own, and repeating into a response is the beginner's classic you already know from chapter 3.
- **Nothing.** Forty minutes and the picture is unchanged. That is also data. No second dose on reflex, no second remedy chasing the first — comfort continues (arms, quiet, the cool side of the pillow), and the morning re-takes the case with a fresh grid. The dose is never escalated; the fence holds.

Both nights count as the system working. In the first, the remedy matched. In the second, an honest zero went into the journal — and what an honest zero *means*, and how to tell settling-because-remedy from settling-because-time, is exactly the next chapter's business.

## Practice

Build the **protocol card** — the kit's brain. One page, in ink, 30–45 minutes, away from the screen:

1. Take a stiff card or a sheet folded to fit the kit's lid.
2. At the top, the gate: **copy the red-flag list by hand, in full, in ink** — all nine items, no abbreviating. The copying is the memorizing.
3. Below it, the five moves with arrows: red flags → grid → stool → one dose 30C → watch.
4. Below that, copy in the three or four situation-table rows your family actually expects (teething? colds? stings?) — situation, candidates, fork question, doctor-if.
5. At the bottom, one line, chapter 1's sentence: *"A remedy must never buy delay."*
6. The card lives in the kit lid, on top of the tubes — impossible to open the box without meeting it.

**How to know it worked:** hand the card to another adult in the house and have them run an imaginary case through it cold — no coaching. If the card walks them from gate to watch by itself, it's done. Where they stumble, rewrite: a protocol card that needs its author standing next to it isn't one.
