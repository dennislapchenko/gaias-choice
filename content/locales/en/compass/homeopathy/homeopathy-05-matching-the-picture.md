## A familiar scene

The kit is complete. Ten labeled tubes, an index card with real prices, ten portraits you can recite with the card covered. And then comes the first real evening: someone in the house is properly unwell, the journal entry is written, the box is open — and you freeze. Two portraits lean toward the page at once. A third fits the mood but not the modalities. The best candidate fits everything except one underlined line. Ten familiar faces, and no way to pick one.

Chapter 4 sent you off with exactly this question — recall it before reading on: **why does knowing ten portraits still not mean knowing how to choose?** Try to answer it yourself first; you've just lived it. — Here's the answer: portraits are knowledge about *remedies*. Choosing is an operation between *two pictures* — the remedy's and the patient's — and it needs its own tools: a way to weigh symptoms against each other, a test that tells a solid match from a hunch, and a piece of paper that keeps the whole argument honest. That's this chapter.

## The promise

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- tell a **keynote** from the **totality** — and use each for what it's good at;
- test any candidate choice on the **three-legged stool**: three confirming symptoms, ideally a mental, a general, and a local with its modality;
- run a **repertorization-lite** grid: symptoms as rows, candidates as columns, the totality voting on paper;
- handle the leg that doesn't fit — weigh it, choose anyway when the choice is right, and record the doubt.

## The core

### Keynotes vs the totality

Every portrait from chapter 4 has a few lines that shine brighter than the rest — the **keynotes**: the famous signature symptoms the tradition considers almost a calling card. *Worse from the slightest motion* is *Bryonia* signing her name. *One cheek red, one pale* is *Chamomilla*'s. A keynote is precious because it's fast: one line of the journal entry, and a candidate steps forward on its own.

And a keynote is dangerous for exactly the same reason. Beginner's trap number one is **keynote-hunting**: spotting one flashy symptom, grabbing the tube, done. But one symptom — however famous — is one leg, and chapter 2 taught you why (recall the ladder before reading on: what outweighs what, and what sits at the very bottom?). The **totality** is the corrective: the whole picture votes — mind, generals, local with modalities — and the remedy has to fit the *whole person*, not win one round.

So the two work as a pair, and the pair has an order: **keynotes propose, the totality disposes.** A keynote shortlists your candidates in seconds; the totality decides between them. Skip the first and you drown in ten portraits; skip the second and you're prescribing to one symptom. And remember chapter 2's third tool: the **strange, rare and peculiar**. A peculiar symptom that *fits* a candidate is the strongest confirmation there is — better than three ordinary ones. Hold that; it cuts both ways, and you'll watch it cut the other way in the worked example.

### The three-legged stool

How much confirmation is enough? The tradition's home-practice answer is compact enough for a kitchen: **a choice is a stool, and it needs three legs to sit on.** Three symptoms from the actual journal entry that genuinely match the candidate's portrait — and ideally of different kinds: **one mental, one general, one local with its modality.** Not three restatements of the same complaint ("cough, bad cough, cough at night" is one leg, not three) — three independent lines of evidence from different floors of the ladder.

Why these three kinds? Because of the ladder — the one you drew out of chapter 2. It now gets its formal, working reading: it is the scale of **leg strength**. A mental symptom makes a nearly unbreakable leg; a general makes a strong one; a local with a clear modality holds honestly; a diagnosis name is a leg made of paper. A stool standing on three locals wobbles even when all three are true.

Two rules fall straight out of the stool. **Two legs is a wobble** — the honest move is not to force a third but to wait and observe more (a wobbly stool is information too: the picture may still be forming). And **a peculiar leg outranks its rung**: a strange symptom that matches the portrait can carry near-mental weight wherever it formally sits.

### Repertorization-lite

Professionals settle candidate arguments with a *repertory* — a book (Kent's runs to nearly a thousand pages) indexing symptoms to remedies. You don't need the book yet; you need its **gesture**, and the gesture fits on one journal page. **Repertorization-lite:** draw a grid. Rows — the symptoms from the actual entry, one per line, strongest first. Columns — your 2–3 shortlisted candidates (keynotes proposed them). Then walk the grid and mark honestly: **✓** where the candidate's portrait genuinely matches the row, **✗** where it genuinely contradicts, **—** where it's silent. No mark is a verdict by itself; the grid is the totality voting, made visible.

Three honesty rules, or the grid becomes theater. Mark from the *portrait as written*, not from hope — if you can't remember whether *Gelsemium* is thirsty, look it up in the master table, don't guess. A **✗ is data, not an inconvenience** — contradictions get written down, not shrunk. And the tally at the bottom is *advice*, not arithmetic truth: five ticks of locals can lose to three ticks that include the mind and a peculiar. The grid doesn't replace your judgment; it keeps your judgment from quietly cheating.

## Worked example

Time to pay a debt. Chapter 2 left with an open question — say it from memory before reading on: *why do two people with the same cold need two different remedies?* This chapter can finally answer it not with words but with a demonstration. Here is M.'s journal entry from chapter 2 — our course's hypothetical husband, verbatim, not a comma touched:

> **6 July, 21:40 — M.** Cold since yesterday morning, came on slowly. Local: nose blocked, throat raw, dry cough by evening. Modalities: everything worse from getting up and moving around; worse in the warm bedroom, but draughts annoy him too; better lying completely still. Time: clearly worst in the evening. Generals: almost no thirst all day (strange for him — underlined); not hungry; wants the room dark. Mind: irritable, short answers, waved away tea and sympathy — *worse from being fussed over*. Context: two short nights before this started.

**Step one — keynotes propose.** *Worse from the slightest motion, better lying dead-still* — *Bryonia* steps forward before you've finished the line. Slow onset after two short nights whispers *Nux vomica* (the overdone man) and *Gelsemium* (the creeping flu). Three candidates; the shortlist is done in a minute.

**Step two — the grid disposes:**

| M.'s journal line (ch. 2) | *Bryonia* | *Nux vomica* | *Gelsemium* |
|---|---|---|---|
| Came on slowly, after two short nights | ✓ slow onset | ✓ the overdone man | ✓ creeping flu |
| Worse from slightest motion; better lying dead-still | ✓✓ the calling card | — | — |
| Worse in the warm bedroom | ✓ | ✗ chilly, seeks warmth | — |
| Worst in the evening | — | ✗ his hour is morning | — |
| Irritable, wants everyone gone | ✓ the grim loner | ✓ snappish | ✗ too dull to snap |
| Almost no thirst all day (underlined!) | ✗ famously thirsty | — | ✓ thirstless |
| Everything dry: raw throat, dry cough | ✓ | — | — |
| **Tally** | **5✓ 1✗** | **2✓ 2✗** | **2✓ 1✗** |

**Step three — the stool test.** *Bryonia*'s legs: the mind (irritable, wants everyone gone — a mental leg, the strongest kind), the local with its modality (worse from the slightest motion — keynote-grade), a general (the all-through dryness). Three legs, three different rungs. The stool stands.

**And now the leg that doesn't fit** — because you saw the ✗, and it's not a small one. M. was *thirstless all day*, underlined as peculiar — and *Bryonia* is famously, classically thirsty. The one symptom chapter 2 taught you to prize above ordinary ones points *away* from the front-runner. So does the choice collapse? Here is what the weighing actually looks like, said slowly. The totality votes *Bryonia* by a wide margin; the stool stands on three strong legs of three kinds; no rival comes close (*Gelsemium* takes the thirst row and loses the mind and the motion — the two heaviest lines on the page). One contradicting general, even a peculiar one, does not outvote that — **but it doesn't get erased either.** The honest entry reads: *"Picture points to Bryonia — mind, motion, dryness. Doesn't fit: thirstless (Bryonia thirsty). Choosing Bryonia; doubt recorded."* If this case is ever dosed and returns nothing, that underlined line is where the re-take begins.

This discomfort is not a flaw in your technique — it *is* the technique. **Textbook cases live only in textbooks; real cases arrive with a leg that doesn't fit.** A beginner hides the mismatched leg to make the choice feel clean. A practitioner writes it down and chooses anyway — or waits — with open eyes. The journal exists for exactly this sentence.

And the toddler from the same chapter-2 week? Two sentences close the demonstration. Her picture — weepy, carried-calm, window open — is *Pulsatilla* on every mental and general leg, with *her* misfit line: the constant sips where the portrait says thirstless; same weighing, same honest margin note. One family, one virus, one week — and the grids come out *Bryonia* and *Pulsatilla*. That is chapter 2's question answered in full: the remedy follows the picture, and the pictures were never the same.

One boundary before the practice, so nothing blurs: this chapter ends at the **choice**. When to actually give what you chose, to whom, at what age, and behind which safety gate — that is a protocol, and the protocol is chapter 6's whole job. The chapter 1 fence hasn't moved: self-limiting complaints only, red flags to the doctor, a remedy never buys delay.

## Practice

Your first real repertorization — paper and pen, away from the screen, 20–40 minutes.

1. Open your journal and pick one entry: a real complaint from the past weeks if you have one, otherwise a healthy-day baseline entry (yes, the grid runs fine on those — the vote just comes out weak and scattered, which is itself worth seeing).
2. Read the entry once, then shortlist **2–3 candidates** from the ten portraits — keynotes propose. Master table allowed; books unnecessary.
3. Draw the grid by hand across a spread: rows — the entry's symptoms, strongest first (ladder order: mind at the top); columns — your candidates.
4. Fill it honestly: ✓ match, ✗ contradiction, — silence. Every ✗ stays. Check the portrait when memory blurs; don't tick from hope.
5. Tally, then run the stool test on the leader: three legs? Of which rungs? Write the verdict as one sentence — *"X sits on three legs: … / Doesn't fit: …"* — **or**, just as valid: *"none of them sits."*

**How to know it worked:** the spread holds a finished grid whose winning remedy stands on **at least three ticks including a mental or a general** — or holds the honest written verdict *"none of them sits,"* dated and unashamed. Both outcomes are a pass; only an unmarked contradiction is a fail. (What to *do* when none of them sits — chapter 10 will take that up properly.)

## Now you can

- Use keynotes to shortlist candidates in seconds — and refuse to let one flashy symptom make the whole choice.
- Test any candidate on the three-legged stool and name which rung each leg stands on.
- Repertorize a real journal entry on one hand-drawn grid, contradictions included.
- Choose with a leg that doesn't fit — recording the doubt instead of erasing it — and recognize an honest "none of them sits."
