## A familiar scene

The third cold since autumn. Same toddler, same streaming nose, same tears that only settle when she's carried — and you read the picture faster than the kettle boils: journal open, portraits familiar, the fork decided in a minute. The system works. Which is exactly why a different question starts ringing — not "what do we give?" but "why *again*?" The kit answers the night. It has no answer at all for the third cold of the season.

Chapter 6 left you precisely this question — *why do the same colds keep coming back?* — and promised a chapter-length answer. Here it is, and it's honest the way this course is honest: the answer isn't in the kit. It's underneath — in the terrain.

## The promise

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- name the **terrain** — the ground recurring complaints grow from — and show that the tradition itself says to start there;

## The core

### The terrain

A complaint that keeps returning is not a series of separate cases. It's a harvest. And harvests grow from ground: from sleep, food, the day's rhythm, air and light. In a garden this is obvious to everyone — if the same bed produces sickly seedlings three years running, nobody upgrades the watering can; they look at the soil. At home, somehow, we keep upgrading the watering can.

And hear this clearly: terrain-first is not our amendment to the tradition — it is the tradition's own voice. Hahnemann, in the *Organon*, instructs the practitioner to identify and remove the **obstacles to cure** — the way of living that holds an illness in place — before expecting anything from a remedy. The discipline we agreed to take seriously puts the ground ahead of the vial itself. So here is this chapter's honest thesis; carry it through the rest of the course: **for recurring complaints, the terrain does more than any remedy — and the tradition is the first to sign that statement.**

Remember from chapter 7 what we called **the response**, and its three paths after a dose? Before reading on, recall all three. A mended terrain responds in a different key: not as a picture shifting within an evening, but as **pages you never had to write** — the same autumn, one cold instead of three. In a journal, that is the quietest handwriting there is, and the most valuable.

### The four doctors

For how the ground is put together, the map we use is Paul Chek's, with his names kept as he wrote them: health has four doctors — **Dr. Quiet, Dr. Diet, Dr. Movement, Dr. Happiness**. Chek's insistence is the point: a health decision is weighed across all four at once, never optimized on one axis.

- **Dr. Quiet** — sleep and rest. The top stratum, and the most depleted one in any house with a baby. The question is not "how much is recommended" but how much *actually accumulated* — as an honest number, for the adults and for the child.
- **Dr. Diet** — real, whole food: vegetables, grains, good meat, honest fat. Our baseline is whole food, meat included; this course sells no dietary ideology. And it promises no "detox" — the word is loud, and terrain mends boringly, by plate and by schedule.
- **Dr. Movement** — the body needs a day that contained movement: walking, ground, stairs, floor play. Not a gym membership — a day actually lived in a body.
- **Dr. Happiness** — the stress that lives in a home is a stratum of the terrain too, and the child stands on it with you. Naming it out loud is already the beginning of the repair.

Notice what the list does not contain: a single vial. The four doctors are not "support for the treatment." They are what does the healing; the remedy merely stands on top.

### Remove before adding

The second lens is Dave Asprey's: before adding anything clever, **remove what poisons**. His suspect list — fragrances, plastics, rancid oils, stale air — gets subtracted from the home before one new jar enters it. Recognize the move? Our family's whole plastic-free, fragrance-free line is exactly this gesture.

Practically: a small dwelling — a bedroom, a camper, a tent — is the air of one small room, and airing it costs nothing. Removing the scented candle is cheaper than buying an air purifier. Subtracting the evening screen is cheaper than adding a "sleep remedy." And once more, so no fog remains: this is not a cleanse and not a detox program — no promises of either. Just stop adding what interferes, and let the journal show you what changes.

### Season and rhythm

Reread your cold pages: they follow a calendar. Terrain has a season — autumn asks about sleep and light, winter about the air of an unopened room, spring about how much of the day happened outdoors. The [almanac](/) in this site's sidebar tracks the moon and the seasons for a reason: a family already living in some rhythm notices sooner that complaints have orbits too. Rhythm here is not self-coercion — it's the cheapest tool a stratum has: the same things at the same hours, tuned to the season, without strain.

Where to start when a complaint has returned — the crib sheet:

| It keeps returning | The first question — to the terrain, not the kit |
|---|---|
| Cold after cold | How much sleep honestly accumulates — and what air does the room breathe at night? |
| The stomach, again and again | Do meals have a rhythm — and what actually gets eaten between the entries? |
| Endless "teething nights" | How is the whole day built around the child — the rhythm, not just the evening? |
| Sleep unraveling in cycles | What happens with light and screens in the day's last two hours? |
| Everything at once, every month | What stressor lives in the house — and has it been named aloud even once? |

## Worked example

Imagine a journal three months old. An evening, half an hour, a pencil. You reread it not as a nurse but as a surveyor: not "what we gave" but "what was around."

Remember chapter 2's **observation grid** — and the "Context" zone we gave its own corner of the spread? Before reading on, recall why that zone earned a place of its own. It is about to earn it in full.

At the third cold, your eye catches the context line: *"after a short night."* You leaf back. Before the first cold: "after a short night." Before the second: "two short nights, the move." Before the third — the same herald line, word for word. The remedy was the same each time — *Pulsatilla* — and the question "did it work?" suddenly turns out not to be the main one. The main one is that the journal you started in chapter 1 for choosing remedies has spent three months quietly answering a bigger question: it was showing you the stratum that sags. Dr. Quiet in this family is running on fumes — and all three "separate cases" grew from one bed of soil.

The cost of the discovery: half an hour and a pencil. No new purchase, no new remedy — your own entries, read at a different depth.

## Practice

The terrain page — 30–45 minutes, away from the screen, journal and pencil:

1. **Reread the journal from the start** — only the context lines and the generals. Underline the repeats: "after a short night," "after the move," "indoors all week"…
2. **One spread, four strata.** Title it "Terrain," date it. Four lines, an honest number in each: *Sleep* (how many hours really — yours and the child's); *Food* (does it have a rhythm — times, not ingredients); *Outdoors* (how many hours the day spent outside); *Stressor* (one, named by name).
3. **Choose one fix.** One, not three — three fixes is how none get made. One sentence, doable this week: "lights out at 22:30 all week," "lunch at the same hour," "an hour outdoors before noon." Date it.
4. **Write down how you'll know it moved:** not "worked faster" but "written less." A line like: "count the cold pages between now and the end of the season."

**How to know it worked:** the fix is one sentence, doable this week, written down and dated. If it came out as three sentences, or as "sort out sleep in general" — rewrite until it's one and concrete.
