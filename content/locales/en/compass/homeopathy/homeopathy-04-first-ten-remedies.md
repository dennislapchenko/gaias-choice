## A familiar scene

Three in the morning — the hour this course opened with. A child crying, teeth probably, and this time you're not empty-handed: you have a journal, a grid, one tube of *Arnica* and a head full of chapter 2's grammar. So you open a reference book to find the right remedy — and the book defends itself. "CHAMOMILLA: irritability, toothache, colic, fever, sleeplessness, diarrhea…" The next entry lists half the same words. The one after that, too. Somewhere between the third list and the crying, you understand the real problem: **lists don't work at 3 a.m.**

What works at 3 a.m. is what works at your own front door. You don't identify the people you live with by running a checklist of features — you recognize them, by footsteps, by how they close a door. This chapter turns ten lists into ten people. By its end, the tradition's great home remedies won't be entries you look up; they'll be characters you recognize.

## The promise

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- tell all ten kit remedies apart as **portraits** — mood first, modalities second, classic home situations third;
- place each remedy in its cluster and give its keynote from memory;
- use the master table below as your kit's paper reference card;
- finish the kit itself: ten labeled 30C tubes, arranged by cluster, with a completed index card — prices included.

## The core

### The remedy portrait

A quick retrieval from chapter 1, before anything new — from memory: where does the tradition's knowledge of a remedy come from in the first place? Ten seconds. — Right: from provings, the like-cures-like principle at work. A substance taken by healthy people produces a recorded picture of symptoms; the remedy is then matched to the sick person showing a *similar* picture. Two hundred years of such records mean thousands of logged symptoms per remedy. True — and unusable at a bedside.

So the tradition compresses, and the compressed form is the **remedy portrait**: the list folded into a *person*. Not "fever, irritability, thirst" but *someone* — how they behave when ill, what they want from the people around them, what makes them better and worse, which hour is their worst. A portrait is built from exactly the grammar you learned in chapter 2 — recall it before reading on: of the mind, the generals, and the local complaint, which weighs most, and why? — and it keeps that hierarchy: **mood first, modalities second, situations third.** A portrait is a totality with a face.

Two boundary notes, so the fence from chapter 1 stays visible. First: a portrait describes the tradition's *classic picture* — "the tradition's first look for a sudden red-hot fever" — never "this treats that." Portraits give you candidates; your journal stays the referee. Second: ten remedies is a deliberate ceiling, not a budget compromise. Ten known deeply beat fifty half-known — less, but better, is this whole site's spine. And every tube you'll buy says **30C**: chapter 3's workhorse, one less variable in your journal.

The ten arrange themselves into four small families. Meet them cluster by cluster.

### Injuries: Arnica, Ledum, Apis

The injury triad sorts itself by one fork: *what kind of hurt is it — and does it want cold or warmth?*

***Arnica montana*** — the bruise and the shock. The mood is the giveaway: someone who just took a real knock insisting **"I'm fine"** while visibly not fine, waving help away. Everything feels bruised and sore; famously, **the bed feels too hard**, and every touch or jolt is unwelcome. The classic home situations: falls, bumps, blows, and the day after genuine overexertion — the tradition's first look after any blunt knock. Already in your kit since chapter 3.

***Ledum palustre*** — the cold puncture. Quieter than *Arnica*, and the wound tells the story: **puncture wounds, bites and stings** that turn **cold, pale and puffy** — yet, against all intuition, feel **better from cold applications** and worse from warmth. The tradition's classic follow-up to *Arnica* for a bruise that settles into a black eye. A wound that wants ice and feels cold itself: that contradiction is the portrait.

***Apis mellifica*** — the sting. Everything a bee would sign: **rosy, hot, puffy swelling** with **stinging-burning** pains, worse from warmth and touch, better from cold — and, peculiar for someone hot and swollen, **thirstless**. The classic picture around stings and sting-like swellings: puffy, pink, indignant.

Hold the triad as one fork: blunt bruise (*Arnica*) · cold puncture, better cold (*Ledum*) · hot rosy puff, worse warmth (*Apis*).

### Fevers & flu: Aconitum, Belladonna, Gelsemium, Bryonia

This family sorts by **speed**: two arrive like weather, two seep in like fog.

***Aconitum napellus*** — sudden, and afraid. The onset is violent and out of a clear sky — classically **after cold dry wind, or a fright**. Restless fear is the center of the portrait: the child (or adult) is not just hot but *frightened*, tossing, with dry burning heat and real thirst. This is the tradition's **first-hours remedy** — its classic window opens at onset and closes fast. Classic scene: fine at bedtime, awake before midnight burning and scared, after a windy day.

***Belladonna*** — red, hot, throbbing. Also sudden, but the note is heat, not fear: **flushed face, radiating heat** you feel from across the blanket, **glassy eyes**, and pains that **throb**. Little thirst, despite the furnace. Worse from light, noise and jarring — the child who cries when the bed is bumped. The classic picture in sudden red-hot fevers and throbbing complaints.

***Gelsemium*** — the droopy one. The slow counterpart: **droopy, drowsy, dizzy, dull**. Heavy eyelids, aching weakness, **chills running along the spine**, thirstless. The flu that seeps in over days rather than striking at midnight. And a second face worth knowing now, because it returns in chapter 6: **anticipatory dread** — the exam, the stage, the appointment — the knees-go-weak, please-not-me kind.

***Bryonia*** — the grump who must not move. Slow onset, and then one law governs everything: **worse from the slightest motion, better lying dead-still** with firm pressure. Everything is dry — lips, cough, mood — and the thirst is classic: **long cold drinks at long intervals**. Grim, monosyllabic, wants to be left alone; being fussed over makes it worse. Chapter 2 promised you'd meet the fuss-aggravated picture by name — here he is.

Hold the fevers as a fork of three: sudden **with fear** (*Aconitum*) · sudden **red-hot-throbbing** (*Belladonna*) · slow and **droopy** (*Gelsemium*) — with *Bryonia* standing slightly apart as the slow, dry, don't-move flu.

### Excess: Nux vomica

***Nux vomica*** — the overdone person. One remedy gets its own shelf because its cause isn't a virus or a wasp but **too much**: too much food, coffee, work, screen, night. The portrait: **irritable, chilly, oversensitive** to noise, light and smells; digestion that **"wants to and can't"**; everything **worst in the morning**. The classic home picture after excess of any kind — the festive dinner, the deadline week.

Worth one deliberate contrast, because beginners mix these two grumps: ***Bryonia*** is grim and wants **stillness and solitude** — motion is the enemy; ***Nux vomica*** is snappish from **overload** — the enemy is every sound, smell and demand of the morning after. Both irritable; different engines.

### The two children: Pulsatilla, Chamomilla

The pair every parent learns first — opposite answers to one question: *what does the crying want?*

***Pulsatilla*** — weepy, clingy, changeable. Tears sit close to the surface; the child (this picture is often a child) **wants gentle arms, company and consolation — and melts when held**. Wants open air, the window, the walk. Classically **thirstless**; discharges — a running nose in the late stage of a cold — **bland, thick, yellowish**. The tradition's classic picture in **lingering children's colds and ear complaints**. Chapter 2's toddler — soothed the moment she's carried, reaching for the open window — was this silhouette, and chapter 2 promised her by name: *Pulsatilla*.

***Chamomilla*** — the teething fury. The anti-*Pulsatilla*. Not weepy — **furious**: shrieking, arching, **demanding a thing and hurling it away**; nothing is right and consolation *offends*. The classic odd sign: **one cheek red, one pale**. Pains feel unbearable — out of proportion to everything. The one relief: **fast carrying**, motion with urgency in it. The classic picture of the teething night that turns a house upside down.

The fork: tears that melt at consolation (*Pulsatilla*) — fury that consolation offends (*Chamomilla*). At 3 a.m., that one question sorts the two children faster than any book.

## Worked example

The kit is easiest to learn the way you'd learn ten new neighbors: not by files, but by asking *"who does this remind me of?"* So — an evening meeting. Imagine it's the night after your pharmacy trip (the practice below). The ten tubes stand on the table, the journal is open, and you walk the portraits against moments your own memory offers up.

*Someone banged a shin on the van step last month and growled "I'm fine" at every offer of help?* — you've met *Arnica*; that exact stubborn sentence is in the books. *A wasp at the picnic, and the sting puffed up pink and hot, and only the cold spoon helped?* — that's *Apis* introducing itself; if the puffiness had been pale and cold and still begging for ice, it would have been *Ledum*. *A deadline week that ended in a snappish morning, coffee on top of coffee, everything too loud?* — *Nux vomica*, and no illness required to recognize the silhouette.

Then the toddler from chapter 2 — our course's little hypothetical girl with her cold. Weepy the moment she's put down, calm the instant she's carried, reaching for the open window: walk her past the ten portraits and one of them turns around. *Pulsatilla* — the clingy, air-loving, consolation-melted child, the classic lingering-cold picture. It clicks. Almost. Because chapter 2's entry recorded one more thing, underlined: she **asked for sips constantly**, and the portrait you just read says *Pulsatilla* is classically thirstless. One leg doesn't fit.

Don't resolve it tonight. Note it — literally, in the journal margin — and hold it. What to do when a portrait fits *mostly* is precisely the craft this course hasn't taught yet; it waits one chapter over. What matters now is what already happened in this exercise: you didn't look anything up. You *recognized*. That's the skill this chapter existed to build.

## Practice

The pharmacy trip — the kit's completion run. Away from the screen; 40–60 minutes with the errand.

1. In the journal, copy out the shopping list by hand: the nine missing remedies, each with "30C" beside it (*Ledum, Apis, Aconitum, Belladonna, Gelsemium, Bryonia, Nux vomica, Pulsatilla, Chamomilla* — *Arnica* is already home). Add one keynote next to each name, from memory first, master table second.
2. Go buy them. A single pharmacy may not stock all nine — two trips or an order for the stragglers is normal; note in the journal what was missing. Read labels like chapter 3 taught you: source, scale, steps. Everything you accept should say 30C.
3. At home, arrange the tubes in the kit box **by cluster** — injuries together, fevers together, *Nux* on its shelf, the two children side by side. If a pharmacy label is cryptic, relabel it plainly.
4. Finish the **kit index card** begun in chapter 3: ten lines — name, cluster, one keynote, price paid. Write the total at the bottom. Real things have prices; the kit's is now a fact in ink, not a guess.
5. Close with a journal entry: the date, the total, which remedy was hardest to find, and one real observation from the errand itself.

**How to know it worked:** cover the index card with your hand and name all ten from memory — cluster and one keynote each. Stumble on more than two, and the portraits section above is worth one slow re-read tomorrow; recite again before bed. Ten out of ten uncovered means the kit now lives in your head as well as in the box.
