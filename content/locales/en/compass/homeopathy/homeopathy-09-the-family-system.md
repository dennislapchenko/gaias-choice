## A familiar scene

Imagine: three in the morning, the toddler is teething — and you are not home. You're three hundred kilometers away, the phone is dead at grandma's, and the other adult is alone with the child. Eight chapters of this course sit at their disposal: a kit with ten labeled tubes, a journal full of grids, the card in the lid, a terrain page. And they stand over all of it the way you'd stand over a stranger's workbench: the tools gleam, but where to start is a thing only *you* know.

Chapter 8 ended on exactly this question: *how does everything you've built become a system that lives without your effort?* This chapter is the answer, and it fits on one page. Literally — the page *is* the answer.

## The core

### The living loop

A system is not a shed of tools; it's a loop the tools drive each other around: **observe → journal → match → dose → response → observe again**. Remember chapter 7's three response paths? Recall where each one leads — and notice that all three return to the same station: observing. A zero means re-take the case, which means observe afresh. A shift means write it down, which means the journal, which makes the next observation richer. The loop has no "done" position; it turns for as long as the family lives.

Except for one exit, which stays open always. The **red flags** — the list from chapter 1, formalized in chapter 6 — stand not inside the loop but above it: any flag breaks the loop at any station, and the road is one — to the doctor, now. A system without that exit isn't a system; it's overconfidence.

What sits at the loop's center? The protocol card from chapter 6. So far it has walked one person through one night. This chapter needs more from it: to walk *any* adult through *any* night this family has. For that, the card grows into the next concept.

### The one-page protocol

The **one-page family protocol** is chapter 6's card plus everything the course has learned about *your* family. Not a textbook — a weekend cheat sheet for an adult who never read the chapters. What lives on it:

| Section | What goes there | Built in |
|---|---|---|
| Red flags | the full list, in ink, as the first line | ch. 1 → 6 |
| Family rows | who is prone to what, and which portraits recur in this house | ch. 2, 4, 5 |
| The night protocol | flag gate → grid → stool → one dose 30C → watch | ch. 6 |
| The response | the three paths and what each dictates | ch. 7 |
| Terrain | which stratum sags in this family + the dated fix | ch. 8 |
| Housekeeping | where the kit lives, the restock rule, the monthly half-hour | ch. 9 |

The flags are the first line for a reason: the page, like the night, opens with the question "is this a doctor's case?" Copy the list by hand once more — yes, a third time: the hand remembers what the eyes only saw.

The family rows are the page's heart. The tradition holds thousands of portraits, but in *your* home, two or three recur in a year. Writing them down turns a general course into a family instrument: the next night starts not from ten portraits but from a line that says "here, it usually looks like this."

And then the terrain line. Remember from chapter 8 what handwriting a mended **terrain** answers in? Recall it before you write: not "it worked faster" but pages that never had to be written. That is exactly why the terrain earns a line on the one-pager: the sagging stratum, the fix, the date — so that a season from now, that quiet handwriting has something to measure itself against. A remedy reports on a night; the terrain reports on a season. The page needs both reports.

### The system's rhythm

Who restocks the tube that ran out in February? Whose conscience carries the terrain page into spring? "Mine" is the one-person engine again. The system's answer is different: **rhythm instead of willpower**. Steiner taught rhythm as the load-bearing support of a child's life — chapter 6 already built on that; now the same principle carries the system itself. Not "when I remember," but a **monthly half-hour**: reread the month's entries, update the family rows, check the kit's empty slots, glance at the terrain page.

Half an hour won't survive on "sometime around month's end" — tie it to a mark the family already sees. Every page of this site carries the [almanac](/): the full moon is a ready-made monthly notch that doesn't need remembering; it arrives on its own. The first of the month works too — as long as the date is *named*, not implied. The system's entire price is these thirty minutes a month. Cheaper doesn't exist; more expensive is heroics again, and heroics are exactly what this chapter retires.

## Worked example

Let's assemble the one-pager for the course's family — the hypothetical one from chapter 2. Here are its family rows, filled in:

> **Red flags — see the list below; it outranks every remedy.**
> **M.** Colds unfold slowly; turns grim, wants everyone gone, worse from the slightest motion, better lying dead-still. Usual candidate — *Bryonia*; check the thirst: last time there was an atypical thirstlessness, recorded with doubt (p. 14).
> **The toddler.** Teething nights. The fork: tears, clinging, better carried — *Pulsatilla*; fury, arching, demands then hurls — *Chamomilla*. Watch the cheeks, and watch what she does with whatever is offered.
> **Terrain.** Sleep is the sagging stratum (every cold — "after a short night"). Fix dated 8 July: lights out at 22:30. Counting cold pages until December.
> **Housekeeping.** Kit — the shelf above the journal, lid closed. Empty tube: enter it in the index and buy within the week. System half-hour — every full moon, evening.

Count how many chapters are compressed into those ten lines: chapter 4's portraits, chapter 5's stool-legged doubt, chapter 6's fork, chapter 8's terrain. And notice what the lines do *not* contain — certainty. "Usual candidate," "check the thirst," "recorded with doubt": the page is exactly as honest as the journal it grew from. The one-pager is not a verdict on the family; it's today's summary of it — and the monthly half-hour exists precisely so the page ages *with* the entries, not instead of them.

## Practice

Assembling your one-pager — 30–60 minutes, on paper, with the journal and the protocol card at hand:

1. **One sheet.** Sturdy enough to live in the kit lid next to chapter 6's card — or in its place, having absorbed it.
2. **Red flags first.** By hand, the full list, no abbreviations. Yes, a third time. The hand remembers.
3. **Family rows — from the journal, not from memory.** One line per person: what recurs for them, which portrait usually fits, what to double-check (your "recorded with doubt" notes go here too). No entries yet? Leave the line empty — an empty line is more honest than an invented one.
4. **The terrain line:** the sagging stratum and chapter 8's dated fix.
5. **Housekeeping:** where the kit lives; the restock rule ("ran out — enter it and buy within the week"); the monthly half-hour's day — tied to the almanac's full moon or to a date, but *named*. Write it into the real family calendar, and the first date into the journal.
6. **The exam.** Hand the page to the other adult with the words: "imagine the child is teething tonight and I'm away." Have them narrate their first three steps from the page. If they stumble — fix the page, not the adult.

**How to know it worked:** the one-pager exists, lives in the kit lid — and the journal holds the first scheduled date of the monthly half-hour.
