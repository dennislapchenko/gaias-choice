## The core

### The partial match — a stool on two legs

Remember the **three-legged stool** from chapter 5? Before reading on, recall it: why exactly three legs, and which kind of leg counted as the best one? (If you answered "the strange, rare and peculiar" from chapter 2 — the journal has trained you well.)

So here is the honest answer to chapter 5's question: when the stool stands on two legs, **you don't sit on it**. Not "give the closest thing," not "it'll probably do." Three moves, all of them legitimate: wait a few hours and look again (pictures ripen); retake the picture aiming at the peculiar — the missing leg hides there more often than anywhere; or choose comfort without a remedy — warmth, arms, quiet, water. Giving nothing is not inaction. It is a decision, and it goes into the journal with the same date and the same handwriting as any dose.

### The layered case

Here is chapter 9's answer. A layered case is one picture lying on top of another: an acute on tired **terrain** (chapter 8 taught you to see it), or two events at once — a cold, and teeth underneath it. The totality from chapter 2 honestly collects *everything* — which is precisely why a blend of two pictures resembles no portrait: you are repertorizing a stew.

The tradition's rule is simple: **peel the top layer first.** Not the loudest layer — the *newest* one. You've had the tool since chapter 2 — the same grid, but its first question is now singular: *what changed last?* What appeared last night that wasn't there the night before? The fresh layer is its own picture; it gets its own stool. The old layer isn't going anywhere; it will wait its turn, if it ever speaks up again.

### Retaking the picture

Remember **the response** and **the honest zero** from chapter 7? Recall, without scrolling back: how many zeros in a row meant "wrong remedy" — and what were you told to do instead of escalating? Right: retake the case. The Summit adds one severity to that: **yesterday's picture is not today's.** Before *every* repeat — not "remember how it was" but look again: the same six zones, fresh eyes, as if for the first time.

### Both hands: working with doctors

Next, a thing that has to be said plainly, with no soft edges. Home homeopathy and the doctor are not rivals, and not secrets from each other. The both-hands rule: **the doctor treats the disease — you keep attending the picture and the comfort.** Neither hand replaces the other, and neither hides behind the back.

Three consequences follow, all in ink. A remedy runs *alongside* prescribed treatment — never *instead of* it, and never as a secret: at the appointment you name everything you've been giving, pellets included. **Red flags** (chapter 6 wrote them onto the first line of your protocol card) bypass every stool and every layer — they go to the doctor past this entire chapter. And the sentence from chapter 1, verbatim, because it weighs more at the Summit than it did at the Gate: **a remedy must never buy delay.** For babies under a year the order is not negotiable: the pediatrician first, always; a pellet only as comfort *alongside*, never instead.

### The hard boundary

And last — the thing this chapter was written for. Home practice has an edge, and it is not blurry. Anything chronic — whatever lives for months. Constitutional prescribing — a remedy chosen for the whole person, not for an acute picture. Complaints that keep returning even though the terrain is tended. Any potency above 30C. Any question about an infant beyond comfort alongside the pediatrician. All of that is a trained professional's territory, and crossing it out of enthusiasm means playing specialist on someone else's field.

How do you recognize a serious one? With the same instinct chapter 3 used to tell a pharmacy from a lottery. A serious practitioner takes a full case history and asks about your whole life, not your diagnosis; promises nothing; and *never* talks you out of medical care. Whoever sells certainty and cancels medicine is not a practitioner but a salesman.

## Worked example

Imagine one long week — you've known this hypothetical family since chapter 2.

**Monday.** The toddler has a cold: weepy, clingy, the discharge thick, yellowish and bland, she reaches for the open window, melts in gentle arms. The stool? Mind — tears and "carry me"; general — wants air; local — thick, mild discharge. Three legs, the silhouette of *Pulsatilla* — you've known it since chapter 4. One dose of 30C in the evening, journal open.

**Tuesday morning.** Nothing. Same mood, same nose. Into the journal goes an **honest zero**, no cosmetics. In the evening, by the rule of minimum — one reasonable repeat. **Wednesday morning:** nothing again. A second zero. By chapter 7 that's a verdict on the choice, not the dose: retake, don't escalate.

**Wednesday evening — the retake.** The first question is new: *what changed last?* And here the grid catches what the eyes had seen but the head hadn't weighed: since last night — fury. Not tears — shrieking; she demands the rabbit and hurls it away; one cheek burning, the other pale; drool in streams, fists in her mouth; only fast carrying around the room helps, and only for a minute. This is not "the cold getting worse." This is a **fresh layer**: teeth, lying on top of the cold. And on the fresh layer the stool stands firm: fury and nothing-is-right — the mind; better only from fast motion — the modality; the cheek and the gums — the local. The tradition's classic picture for a teething night — *Chamomilla*, the anti-*Pulsatilla* from chapter 4.

**That same night — the card's first line.** Breathing free, drinking, wakes easily, fever moderate, no rash. No flags — written down. And still, in the morning, the parents drive to the pediatrician: two zeros in a row had rattled them, and a gut that won't settle is itself a line on the red list. The doctor looks: teething plus a mild viral cold, nothing to treat, keep watching. The parents name everything they've given — pellets included; the doctor nods.

Count what happened here. The journal caught two zeros and refused to let them be painted over. The grid peeled a layer the eyes had missed. The card ran the red list. The doctor got the full truth and confirmed the picture. This is not a system failing and being rescued — this is **the system working**, including the visit that "wasn't strictly necessary." It wasn't. But both hands means both.

## Practice

The messy-case drill — 30–60 minutes, on paper, away from the screen:

1. Find the most uncomfortable case in your journal — the one where the choice creaked or ended in a zero. If there isn't one, build a training case: take two portraits from the ten (say, *Bryonia* on top of tired terrain, or teeth on top of a cold) and blend their symptoms on a scratch page.
2. Retake it cold, as if it were a stranger's: a fresh grid, first question — "what changed last?".
3. Write the layered reading: which layer is fresh, which picture sits on it, how many legs the stool has. Honestly, from the record, not from memory.
4. Decide the move by this chapter's table — wait, retake, comfort without a remedy, a dose on the fresh layer, or a professional — and write the decision in one dated line.
5. Take the protocol card out of the kit lid and add its last line in ink: **"When we call a professional: anything chronic; returns despite tended terrain; above 30C; no match twice; an infant; and anything that rings alarm inside."**

**How to know it worked:** the card in the kit lid carries the professional line, written in ink — and any adult in the house can read it aloud and say where it points.
