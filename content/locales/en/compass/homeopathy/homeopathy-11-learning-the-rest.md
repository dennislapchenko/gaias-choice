## A familiar scene

The last chapter. The journal sits on its corner of the shelf, worn exactly as much as it was honestly used. And somewhere between its pages waits the question chapter 7 planted and promised not to forget: what if an honest journal — not one entry but months of them — says "this isn't working"? Was it all for nothing?

No. And that "no" is not consolation — it is the most serious lesson in the course. This chapter is about what to do with any verdict the journal returns, which books to grow with, how to hold an adult position in an old argument — and about the judgment: the artifact against chapter 1's promise. No new fundamentals, as agreed. Just a farewell walk through everything you built.

## The core

### When the journal says "not working"

Remember **the honest zero** from chapter 7? Before reading on, recall: why did we insist that "nothing happened" be recorded as first-class data rather than an embarrassment? Now that zero pays its debt.

A journal full of honest zeros is not a failure. It is a **result**. Months of disciplined observation bought you something no borrowed authority can: the right to your own conclusion. Whoever took things on faith is still holding someone else's opinion; you are holding your own knowledge. And there are three exits from that knowledge, all honorable, none of them a retreat:

- **Narrow the practice** to where your journal saw signal: let the kit live for bruises and teething nights, if that is where it earned its keep.
- **Hand the question to a professional** — chapter 10's boundary is already in ink — and keep the observation discipline for yourself: a doctor who is handed a grid with modalities is being given a gift.
- **Close the kit — and keep the journal.** Because the course's real asset was never the pellets. It is the eyes that now see a picture instead of a diagnosis.

Whichever exit you take, the discipline of honest observation stays with you. No verdict can take it away — it was the real cargo this course carried under the guise of pellets.

### The shelf of sources

The course gave you ten portraits. The tradition holds hundreds — and here is the door the rest come through. In the spirit we started with: we don't enter non-serious rooms, and we enter serious ones only seriously.

| Kent, *Repertory* + *Lectures* | the deep end: symptom → lists of remedies; lecture-portraits that breathe | when repertorization-lite stops holding your cases | feed it *your* recorded symptoms, never the book's; read the Lectures like meeting people |

Reading a Boericke entry is simple: the leading traits first, set large (you'll recognize them — they are stool legs), then the modalities ("worse," "better" — old friends from chapter 2), comparisons with neighboring remedies at the end. The whole grammar of this course works there too — the print is just smaller.

And how do you choose a living teacher or a course? By the same test as the practitioner in chapter 10 — technique, sources, precision. A serious school teaches method and shows *where* it knows from; the pop version sells certainty and outcomes. The moment you hear "guaranteed" or "you won't need medicine anymore" — close the door. Real further study leads exactly where we drew the boundary: the chronic, the constitutional, potencies above 30C — years of training with people, not an evening with a book. The book only shows you how large the territory is.

### The evidence debate, between adults

At the Gate we laid an honest note on the table and promised to come back to it. We're back — without flinching.

Science's position has not softened because you took a course: large meta-analyses cannot distinguish homeopathy's effects from placebo, and past the molecular cliff (~12C — chapter 3) chemistry expects not one molecule of the source in the vial. The tradition's position is no caricature either: it objects that protocol trials — "remedy X against disease Y" — break the method itself, which matches by totality, and it asks to be judged on individualized cases. The argument is older than either of us, and no chapter will close it.

The adult position is to refuse the false exit: pretended certainty in *either* direction. Remember **like cures like** from chapter 1 — and how, from the very first evening, the course kept answering not "is this true" but "how do you test it in your own home without risking anyone"? The course's answer was never a verdict. It was a **method**: the fence — the journal — the honesty. The red flags stand where they stood, a remedy still never buys delay, and no line of this chapter moves them. Which is why the believer who honestly recorded zeros and the skeptic who honestly recorded shifts both did this course right. Their conclusions parted; their discipline didn't.

## Worked example

Imagine our hypothetical family one year on. The journal holds thirty entries. Nine involve remedies. Four are honest zeros, recorded without cosmetics (two of them from that layered week in chapter 10). One terrain page where "after a short night" stands next to every single cold — a discovery that did more for the household's health than any vial: they fixed the evening, not the nose. And one entry shorter than all the rest: "fever, can't rouse her properly — went straight in," — the night the card's first line sent them to the doctor early, and the doctor said "right call, coming at once."

Read that journal as the course's report card. Not "nine wins and four losses" — thirty cases in which this family never once guessed blind: every time there was a method, a record, and an honest outcome. That is what "understand it for ourselves" looks like on paper. Some readers' notebooks will be zeros end to end — and that is also a report card with honors, if the entries are honest: see the three exits above.

## Practice

The verdict — 40–60 minutes, in the evening, away from the screen. This is the course's last practice, and it is all reading plus one page of writing:

1. Reread the journal from the first page to the last. Not selectively — all of it. That *is* the task: to see half a year of your own eyes.
2. As you go, tally on a scratch page: how many cases, how many remedies, how many zeros, how many doctor visits — and the one line that surprised you most on rereading.
3. Now one page, headed "Verdict" and dated: what *your own recorded experience* — not our course, not anyone's argument — says this method is worth in *your* home. In your words.
4. Next to it, the price, closing the account opened at the Gate: the money from the index card (add up the column) and an honest estimate of the hours. Real things have real prices; we end the way we began — by seeing them.
5. Sign the page. The signature is not a formality: it is the difference between an opinion and testimony.

**How to know it worked:** the page exists, dated and signed — and it contains at least one sentence you would read aloud to a believer and to a skeptic alike, without correcting a word.
