## A familiar scene

The dose is given. 11:40 p.m., the granules have dissolved, the journal is open on a fresh line — and now comes the most honest part of the whole method: the next forty minutes. You sit in the dark and catch your own eyes red-handed, seeing what they want to see. She's quieter, isn't she? Isn't she? Or do I just need her to be quieter? Choosing the remedy was the mind's work. The waiting after the dose is honesty's work, and it is harder.

Chapter 3 left a question with you and promised this chapter would take it on: how do you know the remedy worked, rather than time simply passing? Tonight the IOU comes due.

## The promise

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- recognize a **response** — the whole picture shifting, mood and energy first — inside the complaint's natural timescale;
- meet a **brief aggravation** without panic and without a second dose;
- record the **honest zero** as first-class data and know what it dictates;
- hold the placebo question like an adult: knowing what your journal can show and what it cannot prove.

## The core

### The response

First, the chapter 3 debt, paid in full. Recall — before reading on — what the rule of minimum told you to do when in doubt. (Don't repeat; write down what you saw.) That was the conservative placeholder. Here is the whole answer, in three parts.

A response is not "the symptom went away." It is **a shift of the picture as a whole**, and it has a signature: mood and energy move first, the local complaint follows later. The cheek is still red — but the arching fury is gone; she asked for water, she pointed at the book. The adult is still coughing — but he got up, made a joke, asked what's for dinner. The tradition reads it this way: the person comes back first; the complaint leaves second. One local symptom improving while the person stays gray is not a response yet — it's a data point.

The second part is the **natural timescale**. Every self-limiting complaint has its own gait: a teething night is measured in minutes and hours, a cold in days, a bruise in a week. A shift matters when it is *earlier and steeper than the complaint's own walk* — a clear turn inside the timescale. "Feeling better" on day five of a cold is, most likely, day five of a cold.

The third part is the tool that keeps the first two honest, and it costs one line of ink: **write the expected natural course down before you dose.** "Teething night; usually burns out by 1–2 a.m. on its own." Now the night has a control line drawn in advance — and "it worked" has something real to be compared against, instead of being compared against your hope.

| Looks like a response | Looks like noise |
|---|---|
| the whole picture shifts: mood, energy, then locals | one symptom moves; the rest stands still |
| inside the natural timescale — earlier and steeper than expected | right when the complaint fades on its own anyway |
| two adults see it independently | one hopeful adult sees it — the one who gave the dose |
| holds for hours and onward | flickers for ten minutes and dies |
| the expected course was written before the dose — and got beaten | the comparison line is drawn after the fact |

### The brief aggravation

The tradition maps a second path: a short worsening, then an easing that goes further than where things stood before. It has a name in the old books — the aggravation — and the home literature of the tradition treats it as a known road, not an accident. At home, at 30C, on self-limiting complaints, if it happens at all it is brief and shallow: minutes, rarely an hour of "louder," then the unwinding.

Two rules, and they are absolute. Note it — a timed line in the journal. And **never dose into an aggravation**: a second dose "because it got worse" is exactly the wrong reflex; the path is already moving.

And the honest boundary, because this concept can be abused as an excuse: an "aggravation" that runs long, runs deep, or worsens the whole picture is not an aggravation. It is worsening — and "anything worsening despite care" you already know by name. That is a red flag, and flags skip every chart in this course.

### The honest zero

Remember chapter 2's discipline — observing without interpreting — and why it made you write "nothing peculiar today" as a real entry? Same muscle, higher stakes. Forty minutes, two hours, the sensible window for the complaint — and the picture has not moved. That is not an embarrassment to smooth over. That is **the honest zero**, and it is first-class data: your journal doing the exact job you built it for.

Not a reflexive second dose; not a second remedy chasing the first into the same hour. One sensible repeat may be defensible within the complaint's timescale — and a zero after *that* means the choice was wrong: back to the grid, re-take the case, or step aside and let a self-limiting illness be self-limiting. Comfort never stops; escalation never starts.

And one question this course will not smooth over, because zeros accumulate: what if the journal starts filling with them? When an honest journal one day says plainly "this isn't working" — what then? That question gets an adult answer and a chapter of its own: **chapter 11.** Carry it; it is the question that keeps this practice honest.

### The placebo question, head-on

Let's say the thing careful books tiptoe around. At home, on self-limiting complaints, your journal **cannot** cleanly separate three explanations: the remedy, the placebo effect, and time. Not because your journal is bad — because nothing at a bedside can: no control group, no blinding, and the complaints are, by the fence's own design, ones that pass by themselves.

You have exactly three honest tools, and you own all three already: recorded failures (chapter 1 demanded them from day one); healthy-day baselines (chapter 2's grid on ordinary days, so you know what "normal" looks like in your house); and the expected course written down before the dose. None of them turn your bedroom into a laboratory. What they do is keep the pen honest.

And the adult conclusion — the reason this section exists: the stakes at home are low **by design**. The fence admits home practice only where illness resolves on its own and the question is comfort — which is precisely why the fence matters more than the debate. The journal is a discipline, not a laboratory: it will never prove the remedy to a skeptic or to an enthusiast — what it grows is an observer whose records can be trusted. This is the position the whole course stands on: record, don't believe. In both directions — enthusiasm is a belief, and so is a scoff. The written page outlives both.

## Worked example

Chapter 6's teething night, now from the journal's side. The entry before the dose is the same in all three worlds:

> **11:38 p.m. — the little one.** Teething night: fury, hurls the ring, better only carried fast; one cheek scarlet, one pale. Given: *Chamomilla* 30C, 11:40. Expected course without anything: usually burns out by 1–2 a.m.

Three endings, written the way they would actually land on the page:

**Ending one — a response.**

> 11:55 — the shriek broke mid-crest; went heavy on the shoulder. 12:10 — asleep. The picture moved as a whole: the fury left before the cheek cooled. Verdict: response. No repeat.

**Ending two — a brief aggravation, then ease.**

> 11:50 — ten minutes louder than before; we hold her, give nothing. 12:05 — unwound, yawned. 12:25 — asleep, deeper than these nights usually give. Verdict: brief aggravation, then ease. Noted; there was no second dose.

**Ending three — the honest zero.**

> 12:20 — forty minutes: picture unchanged, fury unchanged. Verdict: zero. Written. Comfort continues; the morning re-takes the case with the grid. The pre-dose line matters now: if she settles toward 1–2 a.m., that's the night's own timetable, not the remedy.

What each dictates next. The response dictates restraint: stop, let it act — the next dose is earned by a returning picture, not by habit. The aggravation dictates the same restraint plus a note: the tradition knows this road, and now your journal knows it too. The zero dictates the grid: re-take the case in the morning, or let the complaint walk its own course with comfort alongside — and either way the zero stays on the page, uncorrected, because in chapter 11 those lines will turn out to be the most valuable ones you own.

## Practice

Give the journal its second recurring page type — the **response log**. 20–30 minutes, in ink, away from the screen:

1. Rule the next spread into columns: *given (remedy, potency) · against what picture (one line) · when · expected course without anything · +30 min · +2 h · next morning · verdict*.
2. Back-fill one real past case from memory, marking it honestly as "from memory" — or, if no case has happened yet, dry-run a healthy-day baseline (chapter 2's habit): no dose, but the columns filled at the same clock points. The format has to exist before the night that needs it.
3. The verdict column takes exactly one of this chapter's three words: *response / aggravation / zero*. No other vocabulary lives there — "sort of helped, I think" is not a verdict, it's a hope with commas.

**How to know it worked:** somewhere on that spread, "nothing happened" stands written plainly — no softening, no excuses appended. A journal in which a zero can be written without embarrassment is a journal whose wins can be believed.
