## The core

### What potentization is

Hahnemann prepared remedies by **serial dilution with succussion** — vigorous rhythmic shaking at every step. Take one part of source substance (a plant tincture, a mineral trituration), dilute it in 99 parts of water-alcohol, succuss: that is 1C. Take one part of *that* and repeat: 2C. Thirty rounds of this gives 30C. The D (or X) scale is the same game at 1:10 instead of 1:100 — so D6 means six tenfold steps.

The arithmetic has a famous cliff in it. Around 12C (or D24), the dilution passes Avogadro's number — beyond that point, chemistry expects **not one molecule** of the original substance in the vial. The tradition knew the arithmetic and kept going anyway, claiming that the succussion step transmits something dilution alone does not, and that pattern, not substance, is what acts; this is precisely the claim mainstream science finds untestable-at-best. We flagged this fault line honestly in chapter 1 and won't re-litigate it here — remember the position you signed up for: **your journal is the referee.** What matters practically today is that you can now read the shelf: the number is the count of dilution steps, the letter is the ratio per step. That's the whole code.

### The rule of minimum

Here is the tradition's dosing logic, and it is nothing like a paracetamol schedule:

- **One remedy at a time.** Mixing three remedies means learning nothing from your journal — you can't attribute the outcome (any experimenter recognizes this: change one variable).
- **Dose is frequency, not quantity.** Five granules or three is a non-question in this system — a "dose" is an *event*. Intensity is regulated by how often you repeat, not how much you swallow.
- **Stop on response.** The moment the picture clearly shifts — calmer child, sleep arriving, pain easing — you stop. Repeating into an active response is the classic beginner's error the tradition warns about most.
- **Nothing happening after a few sensible repetitions means the choice was wrong** — not that the dose was too small. The answer is to re-take the case (back to your chapter 2 grid), never to escalate.

### Handling and storage — the tradition's etiquette

Granules are medicated on their surface, so the tradition handles them with care that looks fussy and costs nothing to respect:

- **Don't touch them with your hands** — tip granules into the vial's cap, then under the tongue, where they dissolve.
- **Away from food and strong tastes** — classically 15–30 minutes clear of eating, coffee, toothpaste.
- **Storage:** cool, dark, dry; away from direct sun, heat, phones and routers (the tradition's rule, stated as such), and *especially* away from strong aromatics — camphor, menthol, essential oils. A remedy kit does not share a shelf with the eucalyptus rub.
- **Label everything** and keep the kit closed and out of children's reach — granules look and taste like candy, which is exactly why your kit box has a lid and a high shelf. (At 30C this is a safety *habit* more than a toxicity risk, but the habit is non-negotiable in a house with children.)

Whether each rule is physics or liturgy we won't adjudicate; they are the tradition's handling conditions, they're free to follow, and following them removes a whole family of "maybe the remedy was spoiled" excuses from your journal. Clean experiments need clean protocol.

## Worked example

Let's put a real label under the magnifier. Suppose tonight's pharmacy trip yields a tube that reads:

> **Arnica montana 30C** — granules, ~4 g. Ingredients: sucrose/lactose granules medicated with Arnica montana 30C. 

Decoded with this chapter: *Arnica montana* — the source, a mountain daisy, the tradition's first-reach remedy picture for bruises, knocks and overexertion (its full portrait is chapter 4's business). *30C* — thirty serial 1:100 dilution-succussion steps; well past the molecular cliff, squarely in the home-workhorse band; exactly what this course stocks. *Granules* — the standard carrier; cap-to-tongue handling applies.

Now the kit ritual, the part that makes it real: the tube goes into your chapter 1 box — first occupant. On an index card that will live in the box lid, the first line of your **kit index**: *"Arnica 30C — bought 6 July, ¤___."* And in the journal, a dated entry: where you bought it, what it cost, and one sentence of observation from the errand itself (you now notice things; that's chapter 2 working). The box has stopped being a box. It's a kit with one remedy and an index — and a visible row of empty slots, which is precisely the point. Those gaps are chapter 4 pulling you forward.

## Practice

This one happens entirely away from the screen — 30–45 minutes:

1. Go to a real pharmacy if you have one nearby that stocks homeopathy (the walk counts as part of the practice; online only as fallback). Find the homeopathy shelf and **read five labels before buying anything** — for each, silently decode: source name, scale letter, step count, band (low / workhorse / high).
2. Buy **one** tube: **Arnica montana 30C** — the course kit's cornerstone and the tradition's most famous home remedy. Just the one; the rest of the kit is chapter 4's shopping list, and buying ahead of understanding is how drawers fill with strangers.
3. At home: set up storage properly per the etiquette above — cool, dark, closed box, away from the essential-oil shelf, out of the child's reach. Start the **kit index card** with Arnica's line, price included.
4. Journal entry: date, purchase, cost (write the number — real things have prices), plus your five-label decoding practice: which was the highest potency on the shelf? Was anything above 30C sold over the counter? No conclusions. Just observations.

**How to know it worked:** hand the Arnica tube to another adult in the house and have them quiz you — "what does every word on this label mean, and when would you *not* use anything from this box?" If you answer both halves without hesitating, the chapter landed.
