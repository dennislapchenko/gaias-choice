## What you're actually building

Gaia's Choice is a **niche affiliate content site**. The business model, stripped
of romance:

1. You publish **honest, first-hand reviews and guides** about natural,
   plastic-free gear for traveling with a baby.
2. People searching for exactly that find you (search, Pinterest, word of mouth).
3. Some of them buy through your links, and the shop pays you a small commission —
   at no cost to the buyer.

That's it. There are only two assets in this business, and everything you do
either builds them or wastes time:

- **Trust** — readers believe you because you actually used the gear and say so,
  including what disappointed you.
- **Traffic** — enough of the right people find the site.

Trust compounds slowly and dies instantly. Traffic follows trust with a 6–12
month lag. There is no shortcut where either arrives first.

That pair is also the spine of this course: chapter 2 builds the trust asset,
chapter 3 brings the traffic, chapter 4 turns them into income without
corrupting either, and chapter 5 is the gate you walk through before calling
any of it launched.

## The uncomfortable truth about the current content

The reviews and images on this site today are **AI-generated placeholder
text**. The family in the About story is real — you really did build a camper
and hit the road with your baby — but the first draft of that story was written
*for* you, with invented details filled in where the writer didn't know the
facts. Placeholder text reads fine. It is also exactly the content Google's
review-system updates are built to bury, and exactly what a real parent smells
from a paragraph away.

The rule from here — call it **the literal-truth rule**: nothing stays on this
site that isn't literally true.
Every claim of experience must be yours. Every photo must be yours. Every product
reviewed must be one you bought (ideally with your own money) and used. The
placeholder content's only job was to make the layout real — its replacement is
the actual work.

## The site you planned — and the site you got

An honest note from mid-course, because the site has already outgrown the
plan above. Gaia's Choice began as one thing: a place for one half of the
family to publish reviews and the occasional guide. Then the other half —
the technical one — kept sneaking in mini-course ideas, and the sneaking
worked: the [Compass](/compass) is now a whole wing of the site, free
multi-chapter courses standing next to the reviews. So the honest
description of today's shape: **the reviews are the core** — they are the
business model at the top of this page, the thing this course teaches —
and **the courses are the powerhouse**: the deepest pages here, the
biggest reason to come back, link, or send the site to a friend. The
Compass builds both assets and converts neither; the money still walks in
through the reviews (chapter 4).

The courses also forced the literal-truth rule to grow a clause. Some are
written from lived notes. Others — this founder course among them — are
**openly drafted with an LLM**: you gave the model a lot of context, your
influences and your personality, so that a topic you care about could be
laid out in the most streamlined, teachable order — frankly, better than
you'd have laid it out yourselves. That is not a violation of the
literal-truth rule; hiding it would be. Call the clause **the provenance
rule**: every page on this site says what it is. A review claims lived use
and must prove it — that's chapter 2's whole job. A course claims a
well-built curriculum — and must say openly how it was built.

## Order of operations

Do these in order. Each one is pointless without the one before it.

1. **Own your story.** The About page now states your real facts — the next step
   is retelling it in your own words and deciding the public identity: names?
   faces? how much of the homestead dream? An honest beginner beats a polished
   ghost-writer.
2. **Own 5–10 products for real.** Buy the gear you'd genuinely research for your
   own kid. Use each item for at least a couple of weeks. Take photos with your
   own hands in the frame.
3. **Write the first 10 real reviews** using the process in
   [chapter 2: Writing Reviews That Earn Trust](/compass/writing-reviews-that-earn-trust).
   Delete or rewrite every placeholder review as you go.
4. **Launch properly** — domain, real email, disclosure and privacy pages,
   analytics, Search Console. The full list is in
   [chapter 5: The Launch Checklist](/compass/launch-checklist).
5. **Publish on a rhythm and build traffic** — see
   [chapter 3: Getting Traffic](/compass/getting-traffic-seo-and-pinterest). One
   good piece a week beats ten in January and silence until June.
6. **Apply for affiliate programs only once there's real content** — see
   [chapter 4: How This Site Will Make Money](/compass/how-this-site-will-make-money).
   Applying with placeholder content gets you rejected or banned.

## The weekly rhythm

A content site is a habit, not a project. A sustainable starting rhythm:

- **One review or guide per week**, properly done: you used the product, took the
  photos, wrote the verdict you'd give a friend.
- **One older piece improved** (better photos, an update note, a fixed claim).
- **A few pins** to Pinterest from the week's content.
- **Fifteen minutes in Search Console** looking at what queries you're showing
  up for — that's next month's content list.

## What failure looks like (so you can recognize it)

- Publishing reviews of products you haven't touched, because it's faster.
- Chasing commission rates instead of reader questions.
- Redesigning the site for the third time instead of writing review #7.
- Quitting at month four because traffic is flat — **traffic is always flat at
  month four.** You'll stare those flat months in the face in chapter 3. The
  sites that win are the ones still publishing at month ten.

## Practice

Twenty minutes, away from the screen:

1. Write, on paper, the one-sentence promise of the site you're building (yours:
   honest, first-hand reviews of natural, plastic-free gear for traveling with
   a baby).
2. Put the weekly rhythm in the calendar — a real hour on a real day,
   recurring, starting this week.
3. Start the raw-notes file for the first product you already own and would
   review for real.

**How to know it worked:** the calendar event exists, and the notes file has
its first dated line.
