## A familiar scene

Somewhere online, someone is dabbing undiluted lavender straight from the bottle onto their wrist and calling it natural, gentle, fine. It usually is fine — for a while. Then one day the same person, or their child, or a friend they passed the bottle to, gets a red, itchy patch that won't settle, and can't understand it: *it's just a plant, it's natural, how could it hurt?*

Here is the sentence this whole chapter turns on: **"natural" says nothing about "safe" — the dose does.** A living oil is the plant's chemistry concentrated dozens of times over. A field of lavender is gentle; a bottle of its distilled oil is that whole field pressed into a few millilitres, and your skin was never meant to meet the plant at that strength. This is the first chapter with real safety content, and it all reduces to one idea you can carry for the rest of your life with oils.

## The core

### The skin route

Smell was the nose route; the skin is the other way in. Skin is a barrier by design, but the small, fat-loving molecules of essential oils cross it — they dissolve into the skin's own oils and pass through, especially where skin is thin, warm, or broken. That's the whole appeal of a topical application: a scented oil rubbed into the wrists or the back of the neck both *reaches the nose* (the volatile part rising to be inhaled) and *sits on the skin* working locally. Two routes at once.

But that same crossing is why you never put an essential oil on neat. Straight from the bottle, the concentration at the skin is enormous, and three things can go wrong: **irritation** (a burn-like redness), **sensitisation** (your immune system learns to react to that oil, sometimes permanently — one careless week can cost you an oil for life), and, for the citruses, **phototoxicity** (a reaction with sunlight — its own topic in chapters 5 and 8). The fix for all three is the same, and it's the next concept.

A **carrier oil** is a plain, fatty vegetable oil — jojoba, sweet almond, fractionated coconut — that you mix the essential oil into. It "carries" the tiny amount of essential oil across a large, safe area of skin, slows its evaporation, and dilutes the concentration to something skin can meet. It is not filler; it's half the preparation.

| Carrier | Feel on skin | Keeps | Good for |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jojoba | light, dry, sinks in fast | very long (a liquid wax) | rollers, faces, anything you want stable |
| Sweet almond | silky, a touch richer | months (keep cool) | massage, larger areas |
| Fractionated coconut | very light, no smell, non-greasy | very long | rollers, blends where you want no carrier scent |
| Apricot kernel | soft, gentle | months (keep cool) | sensitive or mature skin |

### Dose decides

Here is the heart of the chapter, and the safety architecture of the whole course: **the dose decides.** The same lavender that soothes at a low concentration will irritate at a high one. Nothing about the plant changes — only how much of it meets you. So you never think "lavender: safe." You think "lavender *at what percent*, on *whose* skin, *where*." Get that habit now and most of aromatherapy's dangers simply close off.

The math is simpler than it looks. **Percent = how many drops of essential oil per amount of carrier.** A workable kitchen rule:

- **1% ≈ 1 drop of essential oil per 5 ml of carrier** (a teaspoon).
- So **2% ≈ 2 drops per 5 ml**, **3% ≈ 3 drops per 5 ml**, and so on.

And the target percent depends entirely on *who* and *where*:

- **2–3%** — a healthy adult, short-term, on a small area (a pulse-point roller, a spot rub).
- **1%** — a larger area, sensitive skin, the face, daily use, or older adults.
- **0.5%** — the gentle end: young children, very sensitive or compromised skin, anywhere near the delicate.
- **Neat (undiluted)** — the red line. Not a strength on the scale; a thing you don't do.

And now an earned cross-link worth pausing on, because it prevents a real mistake. If you've read this site's **herbalism** course, hold its lesson next to this one: there, a plant was food — you steeped a whole tablespoon of chamomile and drank it, because the whole plant at kitchen strength is closer to a vegetable than a drug. An essential oil is the *opposite* animal. It is that plant concentrated far past food — the volatile fraction alone, at dozens of times its natural strength. A tablespoon of dried chamomile is tea; a tablespoon of chamomile essential oil is not a stronger tea, it's a hazard. Same plant, opposite rules — because aromatherapy works at *trace* doses through smell and skin, while herbalism ingests the whole plant. Never let a food-grade instinct wander into an essential-oil bottle.

### The red lines, seeded

*Dose decides* has a hard edge, and it's where the softest bodies live. Babies, pregnant people, and pets are not small adults — they process these concentrated molecules differently, some oils are wrong for them at *any* dilution, and the stakes of a mistake are higher. This course gives that its own name — the **red lines** — and its own full chapter, because it deserves more than a footnote. For now, three things stand:

- **Babies get the pediatrician first, always.** No exceptions. Whatever else this course teaches, an infant's skin and breath belong to a doctor before they belong to any oil. This is the one place the trust ladder's even-handedness does not apply — the caution wins, full stop.
- **Pregnancy and pets have their own rules.** Some oils that are ordinary for an adult are not for them. Until you know which, keep them out of it.
- **The rule of thumb until chapter 8:** the younger, smaller, or more fragile the body, the lower the dose — and for the truly vulnerable, the honest default is *not yet*, not *a little*.

You'll write the full red-line card in chapter 8. Tonight, just plant the flag: this is where casual ends.

## Worked example

Let's build the artifact — your **first correctly diluted roller** — with the math shown, start to finish.

**The difficulty first:** the hard part isn't mixing, it's *resisting the round number*. Ten drops feels generous, potent, like you're really doing something. Four drops feels like too little. That instinct is exactly the one to overrule — in aromatherapy, restraint *is* competence, and the roller that respects the dose is the one that still helps you in a year instead of sensitising you out of the oil.

A standard **roller bottle is 10 ml**. We want a **2% lavender roller** for adult pulse-points — an unwinding scent for the wrists before bed. The math:

- 1% = 1 drop per 5 ml. Our bottle is 10 ml → 1% = 2 drops.
- We want 2% → **2 × 2 = 4 drops** of lavender essential oil.
- The rest is carrier: fill the 10 ml bottle with jojoba, leaving room for the roller ball.

So: **4 drops lavender, topped up with jojoba, in a 10 ml roller.** Not ten drops. Four. Add the lavender first, top with carrier, cap it, and roll it gently between your palms to mix — don't shake a roller hard. Then, the step people skip: **label it.** Oil, percent, carrier, date. An unlabelled bottle is a future mistake with your name on it.

> **Journal, 14 July — first roller.** Lavender 2% in jojoba, 10 ml = 4 drops. Rolled on both wrists at bedtime, breathed it in from the wrist. **Help:** same unwinding as the strip in ch.1, but longer — it stays with you because the carrier holds it on the skin. **Buff:** —. **Nothing felt:** no. Note: 4 drops genuinely enough; the urge to add more was just the round-number itch.

## Practice

Away from the screen — about 30 minutes:

1. Get a **10 ml roller bottle** and a small **carrier oil** (jojoba or fractionated coconut are the easy starts). Note both prices in the journal.
2. Do the math *out loud* for a **2% roller**: 1% = 2 drops in 10 ml, so 2% = **4 drops**. Say it before you pour — the habit matters more than this one bottle.
3. Add **4 drops of lavender**, top up with carrier leaving room for the ball, cap, and roll between your palms.
4. **Label it:** oil, 2%, carrier, date. Every preparation, every time.
5. Use it once tonight — wrists, breathe it in — and log it in the **three columns**, blank result included if that's the honest one.

**How to know it worked:** you have a labelled 10 ml roller with exactly 4 drops of lavender in it (not a round-number guess), and a journal entry noting how the skin route held the scent longer than a bare strip did.
