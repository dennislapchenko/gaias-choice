What this chapter asks is harder than any dilution math: to reread your own journal without flattering yourself, to take bottles *off* a shelf you filled with your own hands, and to grade the whole thing against a promise made ten chapters ago. ## A familiar scene

Imagine the evening the course ends — which is this evening. You stand in front of your scent shelf. It's real now: ten labeled bottles, a calm blend, a focus blend, the family-safe kit behind its red-line card, and the signature blend you composed last chapter, drying into itself. Somewhere in the row there's an oil you were sure about in the first week and haven't reached for since. And the smell journal on the shelf's edge is thick enough to say things you don't remember writing — including a whole column, the honest one, of evenings where you felt *nothing* and wrote that down anyway.

Here's what happens to most home practices, and it's worth saying plainly: they die not from ignorance but from drift. The course ends, the structure ends, and by winter the shelf is decoration and the journal is a diary that stopped. This course was built against that ending from its first page — but the mechanism was never willpower. The mechanism is what this chapter hands over: a way to *learn from yourself* once there's nobody assigning chapters.

## The promise

By the end of this chapter — the end of the course — you will be able to:

## The core

### The journal review

A retrieval first — reach all the way back to chapter 1: why did we open the journal with three columns, and why did the third one exist just to record *"felt nothing — that's normal"*? Try to answer before reading on.

Because of tonight. Tonight those entries stop being notes and become **data**. The review is simple and takes one honest hour: reread the whole journal, first page to last, and let the three columns do the sorting:

- **Help** (помощь) — the maladies where an oil reliably steadied you: the evening lavender dropped your shoulders, the stuffed head eucalyptus opened. Which effects showed up *three or more separate times*, in separate entries not copied from each other?
- **Buff** (усиление) — the focus, energy, and mood lifts that repeated. Same test: not once, but again and again.
- **Felt nothing — that's normal** — the honesty column, and tonight it's worth almost as much as the other two. It's the list of things you read, believed, waited for, and your own nose never delivered. From tonight, each of those goes back to being *someone else's claim* — carried lightly, not believed, until your own pages say otherwise.

The repeated ✓ across the first two columns is your real materia — the handful of oils that actually work *for you*, which may be five, not ten, and honestly yours rather than the internet's. And the honesty column is the quiet triumph of the whole method: most beginners have no such column, so they never find out which of their beliefs were empty. You have it. That is the difference between a practice and a habit of buying bottles.

This is the discipline the martial artist and consciousness teacher Peter Ralston spent a lifetime on: never mistake hearsay, concepts, or expectation for knowing — and be exact about which one you're holding. Your journal has quietly trained you in it since day one. The three columns just make it visible.

### Grading sources, for life

This course taught the vital core — ten oils, the nose and skin routes, dose, safety, honesty. The rest lives in books and shops and studies, and it's a mixed field: real knowledge growing right next to a sales script. You don't need a reading list so much as a **reflex** — the one you named in chapter 9. Recall it before reading on: what did we call the habit of asking *who profits if I believe this?*

**Follow the money.** It was a tool for buying an honest bottle; tonight it becomes a way of moving through the world. Point it at every rung of the ladder, for the rest of your practice:

- at the **seller** who calls a coffee-priced rose "pure";
- at the **study** — read who funded it before you read what it found; a company selling capsules and an institution defending its turf both commission the science that flatters them;
- at the **tradition** — honored, weighed, never swallowed whole; and at the **teacher**, including the ones this course leans on. Buhner gave this course its soul — the oil as the plant speaking in volatile chemistry — and his *Plant Intelligence* gave it the spine to doubt a lab coat as readily as a linen apron. Read him. And read him the way his own best student would: his claims about direct perception are invitations to test in your own nose-hours, not facts to swallow. Run them through the same journal as everything else. The nose stays the judge, even of the teachers. Especially of the teachers.

### Ten oils truly known

Your ten were chosen the way this whole site chooses — *less, but better.* The temptation now, with a working practice, is to sprawl: forty oils, a wall of them, a collection. Resist it, and not out of thrift. Mooji is the author's touchstone here: be nobody, own nothing, and see if you lose anything but illusions. A shelf of forty strangers is illusions with price tags. Ten oils you know the way you know people — how each smells, what each does in *your* body, which one your child accepts — outperform forty you merely own. Depth was the method on the first page, and it's the method on the last. Your next oil earns its place only when a real, repeated need in your journal asks for it by name.

## Worked example

The final inspection — the walk to the shelf this whole course has pointed at. Do it exactly like this, journal open.

**Reread the promise.** Literally — open [chapter 1](/compass/aromatherapy/01-what-living-scent-is) and read what it said the course would be: a serious practice with living scent, the bright line drawn hard against synthetic fragrance, safety senior to everything, your own nose as the judge. That was the contract. Tonight you're the inspector.

**Walk the shelf against the journal, bottle by bottle.** The lavender that started everything — a repeated ✓, or a hope you never confirmed? The peppermint, the tea tree, the frankincense, the signature blend from chapter 10. For each, one question with a written answer: *what does the journal actually show this earned?*

**Find the dead weight — and take it off.** There will be some; there always is. The oil bought in enthusiasm and never journaled. The one that sat in the honesty column every time. Take it off the shelf, without drama and without guilt: gift it, use it in a cleaning spray, let it go. This is not failure management — it's the same non-attachment that runs through this whole site. You are not your bottles. A shelf is a working tool, not a museum of intentions, and *less, but better* applies to it exactly as it applied on the first page.

## Practice

The closing session — about an hour, away from the screen, shelf and journal within reach:

1. **Run the journal review:** the whole journal, first entry to last, sorted into the three columns — help, buff, felt-nothing. No skimming; this is the harvest of two months of writing.
2. **Write your own-oils page:** one page, your handwriting. Each repeated ✓ oil gets one line — what it reliably does *for you*, in your words, not a website's.
3. **Write the honesty list** on the facing page, titled plainly: *expected, never felt.* No excuses attached.
4. **Edit the shelf:** every bottle defends itself against the journal, or comes off. Count what remains and write the number down.
5. **Write the verdict:** 5–10 dated lines against chapter 1's promise, mixed and honest.
6. **Keep the habit alive:** name the next claim you'll grade — a seller, a study, a friend's certainty — and the rung you already suspect it's standing on.

**How to know it worked:** an own-oils page in your own hand; a shelf that either got smaller or defended every bottle; a dated verdict in the journal — and one source, named tonight, waiting to be graded the next time it crosses your path.

## Now you can

- Turn a raw journal into an evidence base with three columns — and say precisely which of your scent knowledge is *knowledge*.
- Keep a short, personal, true list of the oils that actually work for you.

- Meet a new oil only when a repeated need asks for it, and take one off the shelf without guilt.
- Judge your own work in writing against a promise — the one skill that keeps every other one honest.
