## A familiar scene

This is the chapter where the shopping happens, and where most aromatherapy beginners quietly go wrong. They walk into a shop or a webpage, feel the pull of *more*, and come home with a basket of twenty half-known bottles — a lavender they'll actually use, and nineteen strangers that will sit on a shelf slowly oxidizing until they're thrown out unopened. Money spent, nothing learned. It's the exact mistake this whole course is built against.

We're doing the opposite. Ten oils — a fixed cast, chosen once and not added to — each one known the way chapter 1 promised you'd know them: by nose, by effect, by its one honest danger. Ten oils truly known beat forty strangers every time, and the ten below are chosen so that the safety lessons of the entire field are baked right into the set. Meet them properly here, and you'll never need the basket of twenty.

## The promise

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- read any oil through a six-field **portrait** — family, smell, effect, safety flag, dose, ladder rating;
- recognize all ten oils of the starter set and say what each is *for* and what each demands *care* about;
- give any single oil an honest four-rung rating, including leaving the felt-it-myself rung blank when it's blank;
- stand ten labeled bottles on a real shelf — the working core of your kit.

## The core

### Reading an oil portrait

In the [herbalism course, every plant gets a portrait](/compass/herbalism/04-the-kitchen-ten) — one small grid that reads it whole so you're never guessing which fact matters. Aromatherapy borrows the tool exactly. Call it the **oil portrait**: six fields that, together, tell you everything you need before you reach for a bottle.

- **Family** — from chapter 4, the molecule family, so you already half-know the character.
- **Nose** — how it actually smells, in plain words.
- **Effect** — what it's reached for, its direction: **help** (a state eased) or **buff** (a state lifted).
- **Safety flag** — its one headline danger, the thing this oil specifically demands.
- **Dose** — because **dose decides**: every oil below is used diluted; the flag says how carefully.
- **Ladder rating** — where the claims about it stand across the four rungs, your journal included.

One grid, six fields, any oil. Learn to fill it and no bottle can arrive unread.

### The ten, on one shelf

Here is the whole cast at a glance. Read it once across; then the prose below walks the groups.

| Oil | Family | Nose | Effect | Safety flag | Ladder rating |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Lavender** | esters | soft, herb-floral | help — the calm anchor | the gentlest here; dilute anyway | trad strong · study strong · felt: yours |
| **Sweet orange** | monoterpene | bright, sweet, sunny | buff — mood, kid-friendly | can oxidize; keep capped, cool | trad strong · study some · felt: yours |
| **Peppermint** | alcohol (menthol) | sharp, cold, minty | buff — focus, energy | **not near under-6s**; dilute low | trad strong · study some · felt: yours |
| **Tea tree** | alcohol | medicinal, fresh-green | help — antimicrobial | **toxic to cats & dogs**; skin-test | trad newer · study decent · felt: yours |
| **Eucalyptus** | oxide | camphor-cool, clearing | help — breath | **under-6 + pet caution**; dilute low | trad strong · study some · felt: yours |
| **Roman chamomile** | esters | sweet, apple-hay | help — gentle calm, children | very gentle; still dilute for a child | trad strong · study thin · felt: yours |
| **Lemon** | monoterpene | clean, zesty, bright | buff — uplift, cleansing | **phototoxic** — no sun on treated skin 12h | trad strong · study some · felt: yours |
| **Frankincense** | monoterpene | warm, dry, resin | help — grounding, breath | gentle; a little goes far | trad ancient · study early · felt: yours |
| **Rosemary** | oxide / camphor | herbaceous, sharp | buff — focus | **avoid in pregnancy**; dilute low | trad strong · study some · felt: yours |
| **Geranium** | alcohols | rosy, green-sweet | help — balance, skin | can sensitize; skin-test first | trad decent · study thin · felt: yours |

One field carries the whole safety spine, so hold it before the groups. Remember **dose decides** from chapter 3 — that concentration, not the oil, is most of the danger? Before reading on, recall why. Every one of the ten below is used *diluted*; the safety flag only tells you how low to go. Even the gentlest oil here is a hazard neat and a friend at 1%.

Now the groups, because ten portraits are easier held as four families than as a list.

**The calm anchors — lavender, Roman chamomile, frankincense.** Esters and resinous monoterpenes: soft, forgiving, the oils you reach for to *settle*. Lavender is the safest single oil on the shelf and the one everyone should meet first; Roman chamomile is its child-gentle cousin; frankincense grounds and slows the breath. All three are gentle — and all three still obey **dose decides**: gentle is not the same as neat. Even lavender goes on skin diluted.

**The lifters — sweet orange, lemon.** Both monoterpene citruses, both bright, both **buff**-direction: they lift mood and clear a room. Two cautions live here. Citrus oils **oxidize** — keep them capped, cool, and don't hoard them for years. And **lemon is phototoxic**: applied to skin, it makes that skin burn far faster in sunlight for about twelve hours. This is safety non-negotiable number six, and it's simple to obey — keep citrus on skin off any part that will see the sun, or use it only where the sun won't reach. Dose decides here too: a low dilution, kept out of the light.

**The clearers and the sharp focus — peppermint, eucalyptus, rosemary.** The strongest characters in the set, and the ones carrying the biggest flags — which is exactly why they're here: the cast teaches its own safety. **Peppermint** (menthol) is bracing focus, but its cooling menthol is **not for children under six** — near a small child's face it can affect breathing. **Eucalyptus** (the oxide 1,8-cineole) opens the breath but carries the **same under-6 caution, plus care around pets**. **Rosemary** is sharp focus with camphor in it — and camphor is why it's **avoided in pregnancy**. None of these is a beginner's first oil; all three are used at low dilution, and their flags are not fine print — they're the reason these oils earn their portraits early.

**The specialists — tea tree, geranium.** **Tea tree** (terpinen-4-ol) is the antimicrobial workhorse — genuinely useful, but **toxic to cats and dogs**, so a home with animals treats it with real care. **Geranium** is the alcohols' representative — rosy, balancing, kind to skin for most people, but a known **sensitizer**, so it earns a skin-test before any regular use. Both, again: diluted, always.

Notice what just happened. Read as families, the ten stopped being a list to memorize and became four small groups with four clear personalities — which is the [molecule family](/compass/aromatherapy/04-molecule-families) doing exactly the job chapter 4 promised. **Before reading on, try to name the family of each oil from memory** — if you can, the portrait grid has already started living in your nose rather than on this page.

### Rating an oil on the ladder

Look again at that last column: every oil's ladder rating ends the same way — *felt: yours.* That is not a placeholder we forgot to fill. It's the point. The **trust ladder** has four rungs — felt it myself, long tradition, studies support it, marketing myth — and no seller, no study, and no course, this one included, can fill the felt-it-myself rung for you. It stays honestly blank until your own nose, logged in your own journal, puts something there.

So a full rating reads like this: tradition and studies are inputs you can copy from a good source; marketing myth is the claim you cross out (lavender "cures anxiety" — no; lavender may *help you settle*, tested — yes); and the felt rung is the one you earn. An oil with a blank felt rung isn't unproven-to-you — it's simply *not yet tested by you*, and saying so plainly is a trust signal, never a gap to paper over. The empty rung is the most honest mark on the whole ladder.

## Worked example

Let's rate one oil fully — **lavender**, the anchor everyone starts with — across all four rungs, and write it down.

- **Long tradition:** strong. Centuries of use as a calmer, across many cultures — a heavy, well-earned input.
- **Studies support it:** present, and to be *weighed, not worshipped*. There are real trials associating lavender with easier sleep and lower agitation; there are also small, industry-adjacent ones. Copy the signal, note the caveat, don't treat "studies show" as a trump card.
- **Marketing myth:** the biggest one is "lavender cures anxiety." Cross it out. Curing is a medical claim, and this course makes none. *Helps some people settle some evenings* is the honest, testable version.
- **Felt it myself:** blank — for now. This is the rung tonight fills.

Then you test the blank rung, honestly, tonight: lavender on a strip by the bed (**dose decides** — a strip, not neat on the pillow), and one journal line about the evening that followed. Maybe it settled you. Maybe nothing — and "nothing" goes in the honesty column and counts. Do that three separate evenings and the felt rung stops being blank and starts being *yours*. That's the whole difference between an oil you've read about and an oil you know.

## Practice

At the shelf, away from the screen — about 45 minutes, and it can be a separate sitting from the reading:

1. Gather whichever of the ten you have (you do **not** need all ten today — buy them slowly; the shelf grows honestly). Label each bottle clearly: name, and the date you opened it (citrus especially).
2. For three oils you own, fill a full **portrait** in the journal — all six fields, family and nose from your own sniff, safety flag from the grid above.
3. For one of those three, write its **four-rung rating** — tradition, studies, myth crossed out, felt rung left blank if it's blank. No cheating the blank rung full.
4. Stand the labeled bottles on a real shelf — reachable, visible, the start of your kit. One bottle is a shelf; ten is the set.
5. Set tonight's test: one oil, one strip or diluted drop, one journal line afterward. Begin filling one felt rung for real.

**How to know it worked:** three finished portraits in the journal, at least one honest four-rung rating with the felt rung left truthfully blank — and a shelf with labeled, dated bottles you can name by family with your eyes shut.
