## The core

### Synergy: the whole above the parts

Retrieval first — reach back to chapter 4 and the idea we named **molecule family**: what did it say a smell could *predict*? Ten seconds before the new idea.

Because synergy is that idea composed. A single oil is already a whole family of molecules working together — chapter 1's first lesson, aimed at the nose. A blend is *families meeting families*: the linalool in lavender softening the sharp edge of rosemary's cineole, the limonene in orange lifting the heavy resin of frankincense so you can actually smell it. Molecules from different families buffer, round, and carry each other — a well-chosen pair reaches a place neither goes alone. That is synergy, and it is not mystical. It is chemistry keeping company.

Buhner would put it another way, and this course honors both: the oil is the plant speaking in volatile chemistry, and a blend is a *conversation* between plants. Hold that as an invitation, not a doctrine — *try it and see* whether two plants you chose sound better together than apart. Because synergy has an evil twin: **the wrong pair fights.** Two loud top notes cancel into noise; a heavy base smothers a delicate heart. More oils is not more power — your shouting little bottle was five oils with no architecture. The nose is the judge of whether a pairing sang or brawled, and the journal writes the verdict.

### Top, heart, base

Here is the architecture, and it's the whole chapter in one triangle. Oils evaporate at different speeds, and that speed *is* the structure of a scent over time:

- **Top notes** greet you and leave first — the bright, volatile citrus. From your ten: lemon, sweet orange. They're the opening; they fade in minutes.
- **Heart notes** are the body, the scent that carries the middle of the experience — lavender, geranium, Roman chamomile, rosemary. Most of your ten live here. This is where the blend actually *lives*.
- **Base notes** are slow, heavy, and stay longest — they hold everything up and linger after the rest is gone. Among your ten, frankincense is the true base.

A complete blend has all three layers — a greeter, a carrier, something that stays — which is why your all-citrus attempt smelled thin and vanished: all top, no arc. A rough starting ratio that rarely fails: a little top, more heart, a touch of base. And here the oldest safety rule of this course walks back in — say it from memory: **dose decides.** A blend of ten drops still has to sit under the same dilution ceiling from chapter 3 as a single oil. Layering three oils does not buy you three oils' worth of strength; the *total* drops in the carrier are what the skin meets. A blend is composed, then diluted — never diluted less because it's "just a blend."

### Blending to the person, not the recipe

Now the question chapter 9 planted: how to build your own rather than follow a stranger's formula. The answer is a small discipline, and it's the whole point of the summit — you don't blend for "calm," you blend for *this person, tonight.*

Start from the person and their direction — one of the two you named in chapter 1, **help or buff**: is this for winding down (help) or lifting up (buff)? Pick the **lead** from that person's own portraits — the oil *they* reliably answer to in the journal, which may not be the one a website loves. Give the lead the largest share; it sits in the heart. Then build the arc around it: a top note to greet, a base note to hold, each chosen because it agrees with the lead rather than fighting it. Adjust one drop at a time, smelling between each — the nose route (chapter 2) is your instrument, and it works on chords as well as single notes.

And when the person in front of you is a child, or someone pregnant, or the room holds a pet — the red lines come first, before any art. Recall them from chapter 8 before reading on. A family blend is **chamomile-forward and gentle**: no peppermint, no eucalyptus for the smallest — those are the under-6 oils. Rosemary steps out of any blend a pregnant person will share. Tea tree steps out where the cat sleeps. This is the one place the composing stops being play: the red lines are not a layer you balance against beauty. They are the veto that runs first, and here — with children — the ladder's even hand does not apply. The blend serves the person; the person never bends to the blend.

## Worked example

Your signature blend — walked honestly, with the shouting first draft left in, because the wrongness is the lesson.

**Name the person and the direction.** The person is you; the direction is *help* — a wind-down for this particular over-lit evening. (Chapter 1's discipline: not "make something nice," but a named state for a named person.)

**Choose the lead by portrait, not by fashion.** Your journal says lavender reliably drops your shoulders — it's a ✓, not a hope. So lavender leads, in the heart, the largest share: **3 parts.** Intent in one sentence: quiet an over-stimulated evening.

**Build the arc.** The heart wants a companion that agrees: **geranium, 1 part** — a soft floral that rounds lavender instead of arguing. A greeter on top: **sweet orange, 1 part** — a kind, kid-safe citrus that lifts the opening without shouting (lemon would have shouted; you learned that tonight). And something that stays: **frankincense, 1 part** — the base that holds the whole thing up and lingers past the citrus.

**First smell — the messy middle.** You blend it and it's *wrong*: the orange is loud and cheap-smelling over the top, nothing settling. This is the honest first draft, and it's data, not failure. One variable: drop the orange to a whisper — you were heavy-handed — and give the frankincense a beat to come up. Smell again: now the citrus greets and steps back, the lavender-geranium heart carries, the frankincense stays under it like a floor. That's an arc.

**Dilute — the rule that outranks the art.** Six drops of blend do not go on skin neat. Into the carrier at your adult dilution (chapter 3), the same ceiling as any single oil — dose decides, blend or not.

Now the family variant, because the same wind-down for a small child is a *different* blend, not a smaller dose of this one:

| Layer | Signature calm blend (adult) | Family-safe variant |
|---|---|---|
| **Top** — greets, fades | sweet orange, 1 part | sweet orange, a touch |
| **Heart** — carries | lavender 3, geranium 1 | Roman chamomile, leading |
| **Base** — stays | frankincense, 1 part | — (kept simple, no heavy base) |
| **Dilution** (chapter 3) | adult, 2–3% | child, 0.5–1%, chamomile-forward |
| **Red-line check** | rosemary out if shared in pregnancy | no peppermint / eucalyptus; pregnancy → no rosemary; pet in room → no tea tree |

> **Journal, 12 July — first signature blend, v1 → v2.** Wind-down for me, lavender-led (✓ in my pages). v1: lav 3 / geranium 1 / orange 1 / frank 1. Smelled it neat on the strip — orange shouting, cheap, nothing settles. v2: orange down to a whisper, one change only. Better: bright hello, lavender-geranium body, frankincense floor that stays. Diluted to adult %, not neat. Child variant written (chamomile-led, no citrus base) — untested, waiting for a calm day.

That's what your journal catches: the shouting v1 written without embarrassment, the single change, the reason. A blend isn't composed in one clever stroke. It's *grown*, one honest correction at a time.

## Practice

Your first signature blend — 40–60 minutes at the shelf, blotter strips and journal in front of you, then a week of quiet use:

1. **Name a person and a direction** — start with yourself, help or buff, a real recurring state (the over-lit evening, the flat afternoon). Not "something nice"; a named state.
2. **Choose the lead by portrait** — the oil your own journal marks ✓ for that state, not a website's favorite. Write its one-sentence job.
3. **Build the arc:** one top to greet, the lead in the heart, one base to stay. Three oils is a full blend; if you reach for a fourth, you probably need a better lead.
4. **Blend dry, in parts, on a strip first** — never on skin. Smell it. Write *expected vs sensed* — the chapter 2 discipline works on chords.
5. **Change exactly one variable,** relabel v2, and write why. One change, so the journal can still see.
6. **Dilute to your dose** (chapter 3) before anything touches skin — the ceiling holds for blends. If the blend is for the family, run every red line *before* the nose test: chamomile-forward, no under-6 oils, pregnancy and pet checks.
7. **Label the jar** with three lines: name, formula in parts, date. It takes its place on the shelf as your signature piece.

**How to know it worked:** there's a labeled jar whose formula is written in parts, at least two journal entries about it — including one honest "wrong" first draft and the single change that fixed it — and, if it's a family blend, every red line checked in writing before you ever smelled it.
