## A familiar scene

Imagine you finally go looking to replace the lavender you've been rationing since chapter 1. You open two tabs. One "essential oil" of rose costs about what a coffee costs. On the next tab a bottle a quarter the size costs more than a dinner out — and it's the same word, *rose*, on both. Scroll down and it gets louder, not clearer: one seller swears their oil is "100% natural, therapeutic grade, safe to ingest — a drop in your water every morning." Another site, in the same breath of authority, warns that essential oils are "unregulated poisons you must never, ever put in your body." Same plant. Opposite commandments. Both very sure.

Here is the trouble in one sentence: **you have been handed two sermons and no way to tell either from the truth.** By the end of this chapter you'll have that way — not a rule to memorize but a question to ask, one that works on the seller, the skeptic, the study, and the tradition alike. And the cheap rose? You'll know exactly what's in it, and why the honest bottle costs what it costs.

## The core

### Follow the money

Retrieval first — reach back to chapter 7, which left you a question: *why does one bottle cost like coffee and another like perfume?* Try to answer from what you already know about plants before reading on.

Because the plant mass is not the same. It takes a few oranges' worth of peel to fill a bottle of sweet orange — the oil is right there in the rind, squeezed out cold. It takes an entire field of roses, tens of thousands of hand-picked petals, distilled at dawn before the scent flees, to fill a *quarter* of a bottle. The price gap isn't a scam; it's honest botany. A rose oil priced like orange oil is lying about one of two things: either it's synthetic, or it's a few real drops swimming in an odorless carrier and sold at full strength.

So here is the named tool this chapter gives you, the one that will outlast the course — **follow the money.** On any claim about an oil, ask: *who profits if I believe this, and does the price make botanical sense?* A seller calling a coffee-priced rose "pure" profits from your not knowing how many petals a real bottle costs. It is the same question we've been asking all along, only aimed at the wallet — and, crucially, it points **both** ways. The seller who inflates a claim to sell you a bottle and the influencer who inflates a fear to sell you *their* "safe" brand are running the same play. Follow the money past both of them.

### Reading the bottle

An honest bottle tells on itself, and a dishonest one gives itself away by what it won't say. You don't need a lab; you need to read the label like it's under oath.

| What to read | Honest bottle | Warning sign |
|---|---|---|
| The name | Latin binomial — *Lavandula angustifolia*, *Rosa damascena* | just "Lavender," or worse, "Lavender **fragrance**" |
| What it calls itself | "essential oil" / "absolute" | "fragrance oil," "aroma oil," "perfume oil" — these are synthetic by definition |
| Price vs. plant | citrus cheap, rose/melissa/chamomile dear — the price tracks the petals | a costly-plant oil priced cheap: cut, extended, or fake (follow the money) |
| The glass | dark amber or cobalt, glass not plastic — light and plastic degrade the oil | clear glass, plastic dropper, a bottle built to look pretty, not to protect |
| Batch & testing | a batch number; a GC/MS report offered or on request | no batch, no test, "proprietary — trade secret" |
| Origin & method | country of growth + steam-distilled or cold-pressed, stated plainly | vague, absent, or a flag-and-a-feeling instead of a fact |

The line under all of it is the one this course drew on its very first page. Try to say it from memory before you read the next sentence: **living scent is not synthetic fragrance.** A "fragrance oil" is a lab's imitation — the exact class of synthetic the whole site is built to keep out of your air. When a seller paints that word "natural" over a fragrance oil, that isn't marketing spin; it's adulteration, the bright line crossed and hidden. Reading the bottle is how you catch it.

### The ingestion question, honestly

Now the question chapter 8 planted, the one that gets people shouting: *can a clean, organic oil be taken internally?* You were promised an honest answer, not a comfortable one, so here it is graded on the ladder — every rung, no rung as a trump card.

First, the frame that doesn't move: **inhalation is the default.** This is *aroma*-therapy — the nose route you learned in chapter 2, then diluted-topical, the skin route from chapter 3. Ninety-something percent of everything worth doing with these oils happens without a drop ever entering your mouth. That is the practice this course teaches.

But the honest answer to the actual question is not "never." Grade it:

- **Long tradition** — some real ones did use certain oils internally, in specific, careful, culturally-embedded ways. Not universal, not casual, but not invented either. The rung is not empty.
- **Studies support it** — thin, and funding-shaped in *both* directions: the company that sells ingestion capsules commissions studies that flatter it; the institution defending its turf commissions studies that condemn it. Weigh it; do not kneel to it.
- **Marketing myth** — this is where both sides land, and it cuts both ways. "A drop in your water every morning" is an MLM selling volume — dose ignored, *the whole safety story of chapter 3 ignored*. And "never, ever, it's poison" is often institutional dogma defending a monopoly on who's allowed to say what goes in a body. Both are myths with a price tag. Follow the money past both.
- **Felt it myself** — the arbiter, and here it must stay disciplined. Dave Asprey's n=1 is a self-experiment on *one's own adult body*, one variable, logged — and even he never blanket-endorses a product line over your own observation. Your journal can hold an honest n=1. It cannot hold anyone else's.

And the red lines do not soften here — recall them from chapter 8 before reading on: who never gets casual treatment? **Children, pregnancy, pets.** For them the ladder's even-handedness stops. Internal use is not a thing you experiment toward with a child, a pregnant body, or an animal — full stop, the pediatrician and the vet come first, exactly as the red-line card says. Strong oils get the same extra care regardless of who's drinking.

So the honest position, which this course will not pretend is simpler than it is: internal use is **discussed, not instructed.** No doses live in this chapter, no protocols, no "take this for that." What lives here is the tool to think about it clearly instead of obeying whichever sermon shouted loudest. Buhner's warning in *Plant Intelligence* is the ground under this whole section — a claim wearing a lab coat deserves exactly as much scrutiny as a claim wearing a linen apron, and often more, because the lab coat is trusted more and audited less.

## Worked example

Two claims, walked honestly, journal open — because grading a claim is the skill, not memorizing an answer.

**The rose bottle.** You put the two tabs side by side. Coffee-priced rose, dinner-priced rose. Follow the money: a real *Rosa damascena* oil is tens of thousands of petals in a tiny vial — the dear one's price makes botanical sense; the cheap one's does not. Read its label: "Rose **fragrance** oil, 100% pure." The word *fragrance* is the confession. Journal it — not "rose is expensive," but *why*: the cheap one is a synthetic wearing the word "natural," the bright line crossed. You didn't need a lab. You needed to read.

**The morning-water claim.** "A drop of lemon in your water every day detoxes you." Grade it on the rungs, out loud. *Felt it myself* — empty; you haven't, and you won't take it on someone else's say-so. *Tradition* — thin; this is a modern sales script, not an old practice. *Studies* — the ones offered come from the company selling the capsules; follow the money. *Marketing myth* — squarely here: dose ignored (chapter 3's whole lesson), and lemon's phototoxicity (chapter 5, chapter 8) waved away entirely. Verdict in the journal: *not proven, sold hard, and it ignores my own safety rules — filed as someone else's claim until my own pages say otherwise.* Notice you didn't have to decide whether ingestion is "good" or "bad." You had to grade one specific claim, and the ladder did it in four lines.

> **Journal, 12 July — the honesty audit.** Two rose bottles: cheap one labeled "fragrance oil" = synthetic, struck. Dear one: Latin name, batch, GC/MS offered — real, saved to buy. Graded the "lemon-in-water daily" claim: felt-myself empty, tradition thin, studies bought, marketing myth — ignores dose + phototoxicity. Filed as a seller's claim, not mine. Follow the money worked on both.

That's the whole chapter in your own handwriting: not a list of good brands to trust, but a habit of grading that trusts no brand and needs none.

## Practice

The honesty audit of your own shelf — about 40 minutes, oils and journal in front of you, screen only to look up a Latin name:

1. **Take down all ten bottles.** For each, read the label against the table above: Latin name present? "Essential oil," not "fragrance"? Dark glass? Batch number? Note each on its own line.
2. **Rank them by honesty,** not by how much you like the smell. Which bottles told the truth about themselves, which stayed vague, which — if any — used the word "fragrance"? A vague bottle isn't automatically fake; it's a bottle to replace with an honest one next time.
3. **Follow the money on one oil** you own. Does its price make botanical sense for the plant? Write the reasoning, not just the verdict.
4. **Grade one ingestion or "detox" claim** you've actually seen — from a friend, a seller, a video. Four rungs, four honest lines, in the journal. End with where it's filed.
5. **Write the "you can stop here" line in your own words** — what your practice can already do. This is the honesty column pointed at yourself.

**How to know it worked:** your journal has ten labeled honesty-lines, one price-reasoning, and one claim graded on all four rungs — and you can name, without looking, the one word on a label that means "put it back."
