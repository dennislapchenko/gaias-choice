## A familiar scene

The week arrives with its ordinary weather: a head cold going around, a scraped knee at the playground, a heavy stomach after a rich dinner, a shoulder knotted from carrying a baby all day. You have ten oils now, two directions, and blends you trust. The natural next thought — *can these gently help with the small stuff?* — is a good one. But right behind it is the thought this whole course has been getting you ready for, the one chapter 3 planted and refused to answer: **which oils must never go near the baby, and why?**

This is the chapter that pays that debt in full and draws every remaining boundary. Not to frighten you off — the small everyday help is real — but because the difference between a home apothecary and a hazard is a set of lines you can recite in your sleep. By the end you'll have written them onto a card that lives on the shelf next to the bottles, and you'll know the one place where this course stops being even-handed.

## The promise

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- offer everyday support — colds, scrapes, tension, a heavy stomach — while keeping it **support, never treatment**;
- dose correctly for an adult versus a child, and say why the number is the whole safety story;
- name the four **red lines** — babies, pregnancy, cats, dogs — with the reason and the action for each;
- apply the **phototoxicity** rule to citrus without hesitating;
- write your own **red-line card** and assemble a family-safe kit — the eighth thing on your shelf.

## The core

### Care that stays care

The gentle end first, because it's real. A drop of eucalyptus in a bowl of steam can ease a stuffy adult evening; lavender on a cleaned scrape is a mild comfort; peppermint inhaled can settle the queasy after-feeling of too rich a meal; a diluted geranium or lavender roller eases a tense shoulder. This is the **help** direction from chapter 6, pointed at an ordinary week — small, breathed or well-diluted, and honest about its size.

But there's a boundary drawn through the middle of it, and it's the same one herbalism draws with its three steps: **support is not treatment, and an oil never buys delay.** Aromatherapy lives at the *food-and-comfort* end — easing, soothing, making an ordinary complaint more bearable while the body does its own work. It does not treat infections, it does not manage anything chronic, severe, or worsening, and it does not touch a fever that climbs or a breath that struggles. Those go to a doctor, immediately, oils left on the shelf. A scent that "handles" something that needed real care isn't help — it's a delay wearing help's clothes.

### Dose is the whole safety story — again

Recall the concept the whole course rests its safety on — before reading on, name it and the rule it carries. *(**Доза решает / dose decides** — the same oil is a friend at 1% and a hazard neat; concentration, not the oil's identity, is what makes it safe or not.)* Everyday care is where that rule stops being abstract, because the right number changes with the body in front of you. An adult tolerates 2–3%. A child needs far less — 0.5–1% — because thinner skin and smaller airways mean the **путь через кожу / skin route** (chapter 3, again — recall what a carrier oil is *for*) delivers proportionally more of the same dose. The younger the person, the lower the number; below the youngest lines, the number goes to zero.

### The red lines — and where the ladder stops bending

Now the four boundaries that don't move, and a word about *why they don't move like everything else.* All course long, the **лестница доверия / trust ladder** has kept both sides open: weigh tradition against studies against your own logged nose, and let experience arbitrate. Here — and only here — that even-handedness stops. For **children**, the ladder does not get its usual "both sides." No amount of "it's traditional," "a study suggests," or "it was fine on me" overrides the caution. Children-domain care outranks the ladder outright, on purpose. When the subject is a small child or a baby, you do not weigh and test — you hold the line and you ask a professional. Say it to yourself plainly, because the rest of the course will keep inviting you to experiment and this is the exception: **with children, caution wins by default.** And for a baby specifically, the pediatrician comes first, always, before any oil at all.

The four red lines, each with its reason and its action, are the **красные линии** — the named boundary this chapter exists to formalize:

| Who / what | Why | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| **Babies & infants** | Skin is thin, airways tiny, systems immature — even "gentle" oils are strong for a body that small | Pediatrician first, always. No oils on or near a baby without professional guidance — not even the mild ones |
| **Pregnancy** | Body chemistry is changed and some oils (rosemary, and the strong ones) are not advised | Avoid rosemary and strong oils; favour inhalation at low dose; ask a qualified professional before topical use |
| **Cats** | Cats can't metabolise certain compounds — tea tree especially — and even diffused oils reach them | Keep tea tree and strong oils away entirely; never apply to a cat; diffuse only in a room they can leave |
| **Dogs** | More sensitive than adults; some oils are toxic and they can't tell you it's too much | Dilute far more, never neat, never forced; let them opt out of the room; tea tree caution again |
| **Citrus + sun** (phototoxicity) | Lemon and other expressed citrus on skin, then UV, causes burns and lasting dark marks | No citrus on skin before sun; diffuse or inhale instead, or keep the skin covered ~12 hours |
| **Strong oils near young kids** | Peppermint and eucalyptus — menthol and 1,8-cineole — can trouble small airways | Not for the under-6 near the face; **chamomile is the child's path** (recall chapter 6) |

The phototoxicity line deserves its own beat, because it's the one that surprises careful people: lemon in a diffuser is fine, lemon on your wrists before a sunny walk is a burn waiting for daylight. It's a property of expressed citrus on skin plus UV — recall the lemon portrait's safety flag from chapter 5, now made formal. Diffuse the citrus, or keep the skin out of the sun after it.

## Worked example

The difficulty first, honestly: assembling a family-safe kit means taking things *out*, not adding. The instinct is to keep every oil "just in case," but a kit that a tired parent grabs at 2am has to be safe by default — which means the oils that carry the sharpest cautions don't live in it. Here's the kit worked through, and the card that governs it.

**The family-safe core** — the oils gentle enough to reach for around the household, at the right dilution: lavender, sweet orange, Roman chamomile, and frankincense. These are your first reaches. **Handle-with-care, adults only, kept separate:** peppermint, eucalyptus, rosemary, tea tree, lemon, geranium — not banished, but not in the grab-at-2am tray, each with its own flag from its chapter-5 portrait.

Then the card. Write it by hand, in ink, and stand it on the shelf where the bottles are — not in a drawer, not on a phone. A card you can read in the dark of a bad moment is worth more than a perfect one you have to go find.

> **RED LINES — shelf card.**
> Babies → pediatrician first, always. No oils without guidance.
> Pregnancy → no rosemary, no strong oils; inhale low, ask first.
> Cats → no tea tree; diffuse only where they can leave.
> Dogs → dilute far more, never neat, let them leave.
> Citrus on skin → never before sun (12h).
> Kids' calm → chamomile, never peppermint/eucalyptus.
> Support, not treatment — an oil never buys delay.

That last line is the whole safety architecture in seven words, and it's the one to write largest. Everything else on the card tells you how to help gently; that line tells you when to stop and call someone.

## Practice

Away from the screen, about 40 minutes at the shelf, with all ten bottles and their portraits in front of you:

1. **Sort the ten** into two trays: the family-safe core (lavender, orange, chamomile, frankincense) and handle-with-care (the other six). Physically separate them — a different tray or shelf edge.
2. **Write the red-line card** by hand, in ink, in your own words — the six lines above plus the "support, not treatment" line, largest. No copying from a screen; writing it is how it sticks.
3. **Stand the card on the shelf**, visible, next to the bottles. Not a drawer.
4. **Walk one real situation** from your week — the cold, the scrape, the tense shoulder — and decide, out loud: is this support (which oil, which dilution) or is it a doctor's? Log the reasoning in the journal.
5. **Check every blend you've made** against the card — is anything in your calm or focus roller a problem for a child or pet in your home? Adjust or re-label if so.

**How to know it worked:** a hand-written red-line card stands on your shelf, your ten oils are sorted into safe-core and handle-with-care, and your journal holds one worked decision where you placed a real situation on the right side of the support-versus-doctor line.

## Next

You've drawn the boundaries around the air and the skin — inhale, dilute, keep the strong oils in check. But there's a route this course has deliberately kept off the table, and somewhere you've already heard the claim: that a clean, organic, "pure" oil can be *taken internally* — a drop of lemon in your water, oregano for a cold, whole protocols sold by people who swear by them. Is that true, or is it dangerous nonsense? The answer is neither a flat "never" nor an easy "sure" — it's the honesty of the bottle, weighed on the ladder both ways. That's chapter 9.
