## A familiar scene

A windy week, and a small child comes home with chapped red cheeks. You reach for the tube of "gentle baby cream" — and, out of new habit, read the back of it. Fifteen ingredients; you can pronounce four. The word *parfum* sits in the middle of the list like a stranger at a family table. And on the kitchen shelf, two steps away, stands everything chapter 5 taught you to make: a tincture maturing in the dark, a bright vinegar, a thyme oxymel — real preparations, made by your own hands, from plants you've actually met.

So the question chapter 5 left you with stops being theoretical tonight: **which of these jars can be trusted to a child's skin?** The honest answer has two halves, and both matter: skin has its own doorway and its own preparations — and several things from your shelf must *never* go through it, especially on the smallest people in the house. This chapter gives you the four skin preparations, the short never-list, and the gentlest artifact of the whole course: a jar of calendula oil that starts tonight.

## The promise

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- answer chapter 5's question properly: say which preparations belong on a child's skin, which need care, and which never touch it;
- make an **infused oil** two ways — the slow solar jar and the same-day warm bath;
- turn that oil into a **salve** with nothing but beeswax and a scale;
- run a **warm compress** and an **herbal bath** — and use touch itself as part of the care;
- patch-test anything new the disciplined way, and name where the doctor's step begins for skin.

## The core

### Which jars a child's skin can trust

First, the answer you were promised. The skin-facing pair from your shelf is **water and oil**: infusions used outside the cup — as compresses and baths — and infused oils with their salves. Both are gentle, both are food-grade at heart, both suit children. And the plant that rules this whole territory is one you jarred in chapter 4 — quick retrieval, from memory: what did calendula's portrait call her, and what was the one caution attached to her family? Ten seconds. — **Calendula, the queen of child skin**: the classic European answer to chapped cheeks, dry patches and everyday irritation, gentle enough that the tradition hands it to babies. (The caution you were recalling: she's Asteraceae, ragweed's family, alongside chamomile and yarrow — which is exactly why the patch test below is a rule and not a suggestion.)

Now the other half — the never-list, stated plainly, because a child's skin is thin, absorbent, and attached to a person who puts their hands in their mouth:

- **Raw garlic never touches skin — anyone's, and a child's least of all.** It doesn't just tingle; it chemically burns, and small skin blisters fast. Garlic's place is food (and chapter 7's interactions list). There are folk recipes that tape garlic to skin; this course's verdict on them is one word — no.
- **No mint or menthol near a baby's or toddler's face or chest.** In the very young, menthol near the nose can trigger a reflex spasm of the airway. However cozy "a mild rub for the sniffles" sounds — not for the littlest. Mint stays in the grown-ups' cups.
- **Alcohol preparations are not a child's skin care.** Chapter 5 already gave you the house line for tinctures and children; on skin, alcohol stings, dries, and crosses easily. An adult tool, inside and out.
- **Honey stays away from babies — in every direction.** You know the under-1 rule from chapter 5: never by mouth, because of infant botulism. Add the practical corollary: no honey preparations on the skin of a baby who is one long hand-to-mouth experiment.

And one rule that covers everything new, for every family member: **the patch test.** A pea-sized amount of the new oil or brew on the inner forearm (yours first, then the child's), and a day of patience before any bigger use. Redness, itching, bumps — that preparation is not for that person, whatever any book says. One more boundary, and it's step three of the staircase from chapter 1: **broken, infected, weeping or spreading skin is the doctor's territory** — a salve on top of an infection is delay dressed as care, and a plant never buys delay. For a baby, any skin condition starts at the pediatrician; your jars come after, if at all.

### Infused oil, two speeds

Chapter 5 taught you four solvents. Here is the fifth, the one the skin drinks: **oil.** The method is the folk maceration you already know — plant, jar, solvent, patience — with one new enemy and two speeds.

The enemy first: **water.** Water in an oil jar is how mold wins, so skin oils are made from **dried plant material only** — dried calendula flowers, not fresh ones, however lovely fresh petals look in photos. Dry jar, dry spoon, dry flowers.

- **The slow solar route:** fill a clean, bone-dry jar loosely with dried calendula, cover with a simple stable oil — olive is the honest default — by two fingers above the flowers, lid on, and set it on a windowsill for **3–4 weeks**, giving it a turn when you pass, the same small ritual as shaking chapter 5's tincture. Sun and time do the extraction.
- **The same-day warm-bath route:** the same jar sits up to its shoulders in a pot of warm water on the lowest heat — warm to the hand, never frying, never bubbling — for a few patient hours. You lose the poetry, you gain the speed; the tradition uses this route all winter.

Both routes end identically: strain through cloth, squeeze the flowers, bottle the golden oil — and label it, because the chapter 3 discipline only tightens here. Retrieval nudge: why did the label habit start back when it was trivial? Because your shelf now holds jars that mature for weeks, and from tonight it holds two that look identical and were made a month apart. Plant, oil, date, route — four words of ink.

### The salve: oil that learned to stay

An oil is care that runs; a **salve** is the same care that stays put — on cheeks in the wind, on a dry patch under trousers, in a pocket on the road. The whole recipe is a ratio: **one part beeswax to 7–8 parts infused oil, by weight.** Melt the wax into the oil in that same gentle water bath, stir once, and before pouring run the tester: drop half a teaspoon on a cold plate (a minute in the freezer helps), let it set, and press. Too soft — a shaving more wax; too stiff — a splash more oil. Then pour into small jars, let it set open, lid, label.

The honest costs, so nothing surprises you: beeswax is real money for a small block (buy from a beekeeper if you can — chapter 8 will have more to say about knowing your sources); the first batch almost always sets wrong on the first pour, which is why the plate test exists; and a salve is only ever as good as the oil inside it — beeswax preserves, it doesn't improve.

### Warmth, water, touch

The last two preparations need no new chemistry at all — they are chapter 3's infusions, aimed at the skin — and they carry the part of this chapter no jar can hold.

- **The warm compress:** a strong chamomile infusion (chapter 3's strong-cup setting), a clean cloth soaked and wrung out, tested against your own inner wrist for temperature, then laid on — an irritated patch of skin, a tense little back, a tired forehead — and held. Re-soak as it cools. Ten minutes is a long, generous compress.
- **The herbal bath:** a good handful of chamomile or calendula (or both) tied in a cotton cloth bag or a clean sock, hung under the tap or dropped straight in the warm water, squeezed like a sponge. No loose-leaf soup, no scrubbing agenda — just warm, faintly golden water. For a baby, this is *the* herbal preparation, the one the tradition actually built for them: weak, warm, and mostly made of attention. (Same rules as everything: patch test first, pediatrician first for any actual skin condition.)

Now the part worth slowing down for. Rudolf Steiner's line on early childhood was that **warmth and honest materials are not comforts but nourishment** — a young child absorbs the whole environment, and what touches their skin is part of the curriculum. Teal Swan's work adds the second half: **touch is attunement** — a child needs to *feel felt*, and skin is where that lands first. Put those together over a bathtub and the point of this chapter comes into focus: a compress is also a hand resting on a body; a bath is also ten unhurried minutes in which the child has your whole attention and the water smells faintly of a flower you can name. We won't dress this up as doctrine, and you shouldn't take it as one — take it as an invitation with a built-in test. Run the bath evening this week, put your phone in another room, and let the journal say afterward what the difference was. Yours is the only verdict this course ever asks for.

| Preparation | What it is | Where it shines | Child note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Infused oil** | dried herb macerated in oil — weeks on a sill or hours in a warm bath | dry skin, chapped cheeks, gentle massage; the base for salve | calendula is the gentlest of the four — patch-test first, always |
| **Salve** | infused oil set with beeswax, ~1:7–8 by weight | wind, weather, pockets and travel — care that stays put | intact skin only; broken, weeping or spreading → doctor first |
| **Compress** | cloth wrung out of a strong warm infusion, held on | one place, ten minutes — heat, herb and a hand together | test warmth on your own wrist before it touches a child |
| **Herbal bath** | a handful of flowers in a cloth bag in warm water | evening rhythm, whole-body gentleness, the smallest people | the one preparation tradition built *for* babies — weak and warm |

## Worked example

Let's build this chapter's shelf pieces end to end — imagine a Friday evening, forty unhurried minutes.

**The slow jar first, because it's the artifact.** A clean jar from the morning's dishwasher, checked bone-dry. Dried calendula flowers in, loosely, to the shoulders — the packet cost about as much as one tube of that baby cream. Olive oil over, two fingers above the flowers; chase the air bubbles out with a chopstick, lid on. The label, per chapter 3, plus one new line that makes the future easy: *calendula, olive oil, 6 July, solar — strain 2 August.* Windowsill. Done: the shelf now holds a jar that works for a month without you.

**The fast route for the weekend's salve.** A second, smaller jar — a handful of the same flowers, oil to cover — sits up to its shoulders in a pot of warm water, lowest flame, while the evening happens around it. Three hours later: strain through a clean cloth, squeeze hard, and you're holding a small cup of warm golden oil that smells like the flower's quieter cousin. Weigh it: say 80 g. Beeswax at one to eight: 10 g. Melt together in the same pot, plate test — first try too soft, as promised, so one more shaving of wax — pour into a little jar. By bedtime it's set: your first salve, made from a flower you can name, with two ingredients you can eat.

**Then the bath.** A handful of chamomile in a cotton bag under the warm tap, the bag squeezed until the water goes faintly gold. The child in, the phone elsewhere, ten minutes of nothing but warm water, a familiar smell and your hands. That's the whole protocol. The journal afterward, honestly:

> **Journal, 12 July, evening — calendula day.** Slow jar set (solar, strain 2 Aug). Fast batch: 80 g oil + 10 g wax, second pour right. Salve on my own wrists tonight — patch test on both of us before any cheeks. Bath: chamomile bag, 10 min. What I didn't expect: how much of the bath's effect was on *me*.

One thing to carry out of this evening with you: tonight everyone was well, and choosing was easy. But the shelf's real test is the week when nobody is — one person burning and dry, another pale and shivering, the same cold wearing different masks. Which jar then, for whom? Hold that question; **chapter 7** was built for it.

## Practice

This week, kitchen and bathroom, no screen — about 45 minutes plus one bath evening:

1. **Start the slow calendula jar:** bone-dry jar, dried flowers loosely to the shoulders, olive oil two fingers over, lid. Label with plant, oil, date, route — and the strain date 3–4 weeks out, so the jar carries its own instructions.
2. **Patch-test day:** run the warm-bath route on a small batch (or use a strong chamomile infusion if you'd rather wait for the oil), and patch-test it — inner forearm, pea-sized, 24 hours — on yourself first, then the child. Journal both results, including "nothing happened," which is the result you want.
3. **One warm compress on yourself:** strong chamomile infusion, cloth, wrist-test, ten minutes on — so the first time you do this for someone else, your hands already know the temperature and the rhythm.
4. **The bath evening:** a handful of chamomile or calendula in a cloth bag, warm water, the child, and your undivided attention. No new soap, no agenda, phone in another room.
5. **Journal the evening** — one honest line about the child, and one about you.

**How to know it worked:** the shelf holds a labeled calendula jar with a strain date on it; two patch tests are written in the journal; and the bath entry contains at least one observation you didn't expect.
