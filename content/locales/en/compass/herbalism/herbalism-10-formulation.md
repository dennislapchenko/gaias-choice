## A familiar scene

Imagine a raw November evening. The cold arrived all at once, the way family colds do: heat behind the eyes, a dry scratchy throat, the first bark of a cough — and dinner sitting like a stone, because being sick is work too. You stand in front of your shelf. It's a good shelf now: labels face forward, the safety card is posted, chapter 9 taught it to breathe. You reach for elderflower, because the state is plainly hot and dry and you know how to match that.

And it's right — and it's not enough. Elderflower answers the feverish heat; the throat keeps scratching; nothing in that one jar has an opinion about the stone in the stomach. So you brew a second cup of thyme, then consider a third of chamomile, and by ten o'clock the kitchen counter looks like a tasting bar, the sick person has three half-drunk mugs cooling in front of them, and wants none of them.

Chapter 4 left you a question: *how do ten single herbs become one smart blend?* Chapter 9 sharpened it: *what do you do when one herb is not enough?* Both debts come due tonight. The answer is not "more herbs." The answer is **anatomy**.

## The promise

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- decide when a blend is even justified — and when a single herb still wins;
- name the three roles of **blend anatomy** — lead, support, corrigent — and assign each one deliberately;
- compose a 3–4-herb formula from the kitchen ten, matched by energetics to the actual person in front of you, with a written recipe in parts;
- adjust a blend one variable at a time, journal-tracked, without losing the thread;
- run every ingredient through chapter 7's safety checks — because checks don't merge when herbs do.

## The core

### When one herb is not enough

Quick retrieval before the new idea — from memory: what did *plant, not pill* (chapter 1) say about where a plant's action lives? Ten seconds. Because a blend takes that principle one step further: if a single plant is already a whole chemistry, a blend is a *composed* chemistry — several whole ranges, chosen to overlap on purpose. It is the least pill-like thing this course makes.

Most days, the single herb still wins. One plant, one named job, one clean journal entry — that's *less, but better* applied to the mug, and it's why nine chapters ran on single herbs. A blend earns its place only when the **state has more than one front**: the fever-heat *and* the scratchy cough *and* the stone in the stomach. One herb covers one front well. Stretch it across three and it covers none of them well.

But hear the opposite failure too, because it's the more common one: the beginner's kitchen-sink tea, seven herbs deep, brewed on the logic that more allies can't hurt. They can. Seven herbs is not depth, it's noise — the aromatics fight, the bitters bury the delicate things, no single intent survives, and when it *does* help you'll never know which herb to thank. If you can't say in one sentence why a herb is in the jar, it isn't in the jar.

### Blend anatomy: lead, support, corrigent

A working blend has a skeleton, and it's small enough to memorize tonight:

- **The lead** carries the intent. It's chosen exactly the way chapter 7 taught you to choose a single herb: name the state (recall the two axes from chapter 2 — try to say them before reading on: warming/cooling, drying/moistening), then match the direction. The lead gets the largest share of the formula, and the blend is *named* by its lead in your head: this is an elderflower blend, that is a chamomile blend.
- **The support** deepens or broadens. Two legal jobs: push the same direction harder (a second diaphoretic beside the first), or cover the second front the lead can't reach (the stomach while the lead handles the heat). One support. Pick which job it's doing and write the job down.
- **The corrigent** fixes what the blend gets wrong. Taste, most often — the herb that makes a child actually drink the cup; sometimes texture or edge — something soft against an astringent's rasp, something cooling against a warmer's push. The corrigent is the only role that answers to the *drinker* rather than the state — and it's optional. A two-herb blend with nothing to correct is complete.

Three roles, and notice what the anatomy quietly enforces: 3–4 herbs is a *full* blend. There is no fourth role waiting to be filled. When you feel the urge to add a fifth herb, what you actually need is a better lead.

### The rules of the small formula

Anatomy tells you who's in the jar; these four rules tell you how the jar behaves over time.

**Start from one lead and one support.** Compose the smallest blend that covers the fronts you actually named — you can widen later, from evidence. It is much easier to hear what a third herb adds to a duo than to guess what to remove from a septet.

**Write the formula in parts.** "Some elderflower, a bit of yarrow" cannot be repeated, fixed, or trusted. Parts by volume — spoons — are precise enough for kitchen work: 2 parts elderflower, 1 part yarrow, 1 part mint is a formula; a handful of this and that is a mood. The label on a blend jar carries one line more than chapter 3's standard: name, **formula in parts**, date.

**Change one variable at a time.** This is the biohacker's n=1 discipline wearing an apron, and your herb journal — running since the first page of chapter 1 — is the lab notebook. Brew the blend several times *as written* before touching anything; then make exactly one change — swap the corrigent, shift one part, move a strength setting from chapter 3's dial — and label it v2. Change two things at once and the journal goes blind: whatever happens, you won't know why.

**Taste the blend before anyone needs it.** A blend composed at a sick person's bedside is a guess under pressure. Compose on a calm day, brew a cup at everyday strength, and run it through your own mouth first — the daily cup is your test bench, and taste-as-information (chapter 2) works on chords as well as notes: you will *hear* the yarrow shouldering everything else aside long before a sick child tells you the tea is awful.

### Safety: the checks don't blend

Here is the one place where blending adds nothing and must add nothing: **every single ingredient passes chapter 7's checks separately, for every person who might drink the cup.** Three herbs means three passes. The list doesn't average out — it vetoes.

- **Pregnancy:** yarrow is off the list for anyone pregnant — which strikes it from any blend the whole family shares. Pregnant, nursing, or on regular medication → the standing rule: herbs only after checking with a professional.
- **Medications:** garlic, ginger and yarrow all sit on the anticoagulant caution list — a blend can quietly stack three mild interactions into one cup. Herbs and medicines from the same household don't mix silently; the doctor hears about both.
- **Allergy:** chamomile, calendula and yarrow are all Asteraceae — a ragweed-family allergy vetoes three of your ten herbs at once, and a blend is exactly where that surprise likes to hide.
- **Children:** child variants run chamomile-forward at everyday strength; no strong mint for the smallest — menthol has no business near an infant's or toddler's face or chest; and honey, if the blend meets it, never under one year, no exceptions.
- **Babies:** no blends at all. For infants the pediatrician comes first, always — the staircase from chapter 1 stands over every formula in this chapter.

And the red flags outrank everything. A blend is judged against the posted list exactly like a single herb: high or persistent fever, difficulty breathing, worsening after 48 hours — the staircase goes up, and no formula, however elegant, buys delay.

## Worked example

Back to the November evening — walked honestly this time, with the messy middle left in.

**Name the state.** Hot and dry: heat behind the eyes, dry scratchy throat, restless. (Chapter 7's discipline — the state, not the diagnosis.) Check the posted list first: no red-flag fever, breathing fine, nobody an infant. This is step two of the staircase — a self-limiting cold evening, the exact territory support was made for.

**Choose the lead.** The state is hot-dry, so the lead cools and opens: **elderflower, 2 parts** — the traditional cold-evening herb of half of Europe, and its portrait (chapter 4) fits the front. Intent in one sentence: ease the hot, dry, restless evening. Flowers only, of course — you've known since chapter 4 what elder's berries demand (cooked, always) and that leaves and bark are simply not ingredients.

**Choose the support.** Second front: the whole-body ache-and-shiver of a cold taking hold. **Yarrow, 1 part** — the classic partner here; it pushes the same sweat-opening direction harder and brings its bitter, tightening edge. Job named: deepen the lead. (And the checks fire immediately: nobody pregnant tonight, nobody on blood thinners — say it out loud, per cup, per person.)

**First taste, calm day.** Everyday strength, your own mug — and the honest journal line: it *works*, and it tastes like a fence. Yarrow's bitterness elbows the elderflower aside. This is the messy middle: the first draft of a blend is nearly always wrong in the mouth, and that's not failure, that's data.

**Add the corrigent.** **Mint, 1 part** — brightens the whole cup, and its cooling direction agrees with the intent instead of fighting it. Formula v2, relabeled: elderflower 2 · yarrow 1 · mint 1. One variable changed; the journal can still see.

**Now adjust for the actual person — the whole point of the Summit.** The same cold in a different body is a different blend:

- **The child** (old enough for herbal tea at all): chamomile-forward, always — **chamomile 2 parts, elderflower 1 part**, everyday strength, and no strong mint for the smallest. Notice the corrigent slot standing honestly empty: at everyday strength, with chamomile leading, there is nothing to correct. Two herbs, complete blend. And if a baby is sick — no blend exists for that; pediatrician, tonight if the list says so.
- **The cold-shivery variant:** the same virus in the pale, chilled, blanket-seeking body is a *cold-wet* state — and the lead flips. Elderflower steps down; **ginger leads** (warming from the center — you've felt exactly this since chapter 3's decoction lane), thyme supports the chest, and honey, for anyone past their first birthday, carries it as the medium. Same anatomy, opposite compass corner. The formula follows the person, never the other way round.

| Role | The family cold blend (adults) | The child variant |
|---|---|---|
| **Lead** — carries the intent | elderflower, 2 parts (cooling, opening) | chamomile, 2 parts (gentle, accepted) |
| **Support** — deepens or broadens | yarrow, 1 part (same direction, deeper) | elderflower, 1 part |
| **Corrigent** — fixes the edge | mint, 1 part (taste, agrees with intent) | none — everyday strength needs no correction |
| Strength (chapter 3's dial) | strong cup | everyday cup |
| Checks before brewing | pregnancy → yarrow out · anticoagulants → yarrow, ginger flagged · Asteraceae allergy vetoes chamomile *and* yarrow | Asteraceae check · no strong mint for the smallest · honey never under 1 · infants: pediatrician, not blends |

And one more for the shelf, because it shows the roles are *roles*, not fixed ranks: the **after-dinner blend** — chamomile 2, ginger 1, mint 1. Chamomile leads with its late bitterness (the one you met on the very first page of this course), ginger supports by warming a stalled stomach, mint corrects the taste into something the whole table will drink. Now shift the state: the heavy-and-cold stomach, the sluggish after-a-rich-meal-in-winter kind — and ginger takes the lead with chamomile supporting. Same three jars off your shelf, two different formulas, because the *state* is the employer and it writes the job titles.

> **Journal, 6 July — first deliberate blend, v1 → v2.** Cold blend: elder 2 / yarrow 1. Everyday strength, my mug, calm day. Works as intended, tastes like a fence — yarrow shouting. v2: + mint 1 part, one change only. Better: bright first, bitter behind, drinkable. Child variant written but untested — waiting for a real (mild!) occasion. Checks noted per person on the label.

That's what your journal should catch: the wrongness of v1 written down without embarrassment, the single change, the reason. A blend is not composed — it's *grown*, one honest correction at a time.

## Practice

Your first deliberate blend — 40–60 minutes at the kitchen table, then a week of quiet tracking:

1. Pick a **real, recurring situation** in your home — the restless evening, the heavy dinner, the cold-season sniffle. Not an imaginary emergency; a thing that actually happens, so the blend will actually get tested.
2. **Name the state in the journal** — the energetic verdict, chapter 7 style: hot or cold? dry or damp? Which fronts — one or two?
3. **Choose the lead** from the kitchen ten by portrait. Write its job in one sentence. If you can't — different lead.
4. **Choose one support.** Write which of the two legal jobs it's doing: deeper, or broader.
5. **Run the checks** — every herb, every person who might drink it: pregnancy, medications, Asteraceae, child rules, honey-under-1. Anything doubtful → that herb doesn't board.
6. **Mix a small dry batch in parts** (a spoon is a part). Label with all three lines: name, formula in parts, date. The jar takes its place on the shelf.
7. **Taste the same day** at everyday strength. Journal: *expected vs sensed* — the chapter 2 discipline works on chords too.
8. **Over the week,** brew it when the real situation shows up. After two or three honest cups, make **exactly one change** — one part shifted, one corrigent swapped, one strength step — relabel v2, and write why.

**How to know it worked:** there's a labeled jar on the shelf whose formula is written in parts, at least two dated journal entries about it — and exactly one deliberate adjustment, with its reason, in writing. Not a perfect blend; a *governed* one.

## Now you can

- Say when a blend is justified — and defend the single herb when it isn't.
- Assign lead, support and corrigent so that every herb in the jar has a one-sentence job.
- Write a formula in parts that can be repeated, corrected, and handed to someone else.
- Flip a lead when the same complaint shows up in a different body — hot-dry versus cold-shivery — and downshift any blend into its child variant.
- Keep three herbs honest with one journal and one-variable changes.
