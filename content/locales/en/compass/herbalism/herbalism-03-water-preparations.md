## A familiar scene

Someone tells you nettle is "incredibly nourishing — practically a multivitamin you can grow." So you buy a box, brew a teabag's worth for three minutes, and get… faintly grassy water. ## The core

### Infusion vs decoction — the part decides

Water extracts, but plant tissues surrender differently. The rule the whole tradition agrees on:

- **Infusion** (pour hot water over, steep, covered): for the **delicate parts** — leaves, flowers, aromatic seeds. Their cell walls are thin and their treasures often volatile; boiling them outright drives off exactly the aromatic fraction you learned to taste in chapter 2. Chamomile, mint, nettle leaf — all infusions.
- **Decoction** (simmer gently in water, lidded, 15–30 minutes): for the **tough parts** — roots, barks, dried berries, seeds with hard coats. Ginger root, cinnamon bark, rosehips. These hold their compounds behind woody walls that steeping barely dents; they need sustained heat to open.

One plant can need both manners in different parts — but the working question is always the same: *am I holding something delicate or something tough?* Delicate steeps. Tough simmers. That single sentence correctly handles most of the plants you will ever touch — and when a plant seems to give water almost nothing at all, note the suspicion; chapter 5 owes you that story.

| | Infusion | Decoction |
|---|---|---|
| For | delicate parts: leaves, flowers, aromatic seeds | tough parts: roots, barks, dried berries, hard seeds |
| How | pour just-boiled water over, **cover**, steep | gentle simmer, **lid on** |
| Time | 10–20 minutes | 15–30 minutes |
| From this course | chamomile, mint, nettle leaf | ginger root, cinnamon bark, rosehips |

### The power of ratios

Here is where the nettle disappointment gets solved. Water preparation isn't one thing — it's a *dial*, and the tradition uses at least three settings:

- **The everyday cup:** 1–2 teaspoons of herb per mug, covered, 10–15 minutes. Pleasant strength; the register of daily life, of taste, of a child's first acquaintance with a plant.
- **The strong cup:** a full tablespoon, 15–20 minutes. Chapter 1's chamomile — you've already brewed at this setting; it's the register of *support* on the three steps.
- **The nourishing infusion:** the herbalist's quart jar — roughly **30 g of dried herb, a liter of just-boiled water, lid on, 4 hours to overnight**, then strain and drink through the next day, plain or however you like it. This is the setting for the great **food-grade** herbs — nettle above all — where the point is minerals and substance, and a long, patient steep is what actually pulls them. This is nettle "like a broth": calcium, magnesium, iron don't leap into water in three minutes.

Same jar on the shelf, three different drinks — and now the chapter 1 principle has numbers: *the preparation is the medicine.* A note for the compass, too: long and strong pulls more of everything, including tannins — your own tongue (astringency, chapter 2) will tell you when you've dialed past pleasant.

The three-steps rule from chapter 1 keeps its authority here: the nourishing infusion is **food** — and it stays on the food step. It doesn't treat anything, and the Health rule of this whole site stands: no jar outruns real meals and real sleep.

### The daily cup

Habits beat intentions, so the shelf gets an anchor: **one deliberate cup a day** — any strength, any plant you've properly met, but brewed *properly* (covered, timed, noticed) and logged in one line. Not a supplement schedule; a standing appointment with the craft. Ten seconds of journal per day («mint, everyday strength, after lunch — belly quiet») keeps the observer's muscle warm between chapters, builds your own reference data plant by plant — and quietly guarantees the shelf never becomes decoration. The plants you'll meet in chapter 4 will each pass through the daily cup on arrival; that's how strangers become the acquaintances chapter 2 promised.

### Storage, fridge life, labels

Water preparations are fresh food, and they spoil like it:

- **Room temperature:** a brewed infusion is same-day.
- **Fridge:** 24–48 hours in a closed jar — smell before drinking; sour or off means out, no negotiating.
- **Vessels:** glass, with real lids. A dedicated quart jar for nourishing infusions, a small strainer, and a funnel are the whole equipment list.
- **Labels — on everything, always:** plant, strength, date and time brewed. Masking tape and a marker are perfect. The label habit starts today, while it's trivial, because from chapter 5 onward (tinctures maturing for weeks) unlabeled jars stop being a joke and start being a genuine safety problem in a house with children.

## Worked example

Tonight's build is a **side-by-side**: one delicate plant, one tough one, both methods running at once — plus the shelf getting its station.

- **Infusion lane:** chamomile, strong-cup setting — 1 tbsp, big mug, covered, 15 minutes. You've done this in chapter 1; tonight it's the *control* arm.
- **Decoction lane:** fresh ginger root — 4–5 thin slices per two cups of water, gentle simmer, **lid on**, 15 minutes. Watch what simmering does that steeping couldn't: the liquor turns golden, the kitchen fills with the pungent-warm smell — and remember which taste family that is and which energetic direction it announces (chapter 2 answers both).
- **Taste them side by side, slowly.** Same journal grid as the plant hour, one column per lane: smell, taste families, energetic verdict, body notes. Chamomile: aromatic-bitter, mildly cooling. Ginger: pungent, warming from the center — the *opposite corner* of the compass, made by the *opposite method*. One evening, and you've now personally produced and felt both water methods and both temperature directions. That's the compass and the craft shaking hands.
- **Then set the third lane running for tomorrow:** the quart jar, 30 g nettle leaf, boiling water to the top, lid, overnight on the counter. Morning: strain, taste, journal — against the predictions you made at the top of this chapter.

## Practice

Kitchen, away from the screen — about 45 minutes tonight plus 10 tomorrow morning:

1. Run the full **side-by-side** as worked above: chamomile infusion lane, ginger decoction lane, slow comparative tasting, journal grid with an energetic verdict per lane.
2. Set the **nourishing infusion**: 30 g nettle (pharmacy loose-leaf is fine), quart jar, boiling water, lid, overnight. Write your prediction in the journal *before* bed — tomorrow you check yourself, not us.
3. Build the **infusion station** on the green shelf: the quart jar's permanent spot, strainer, funnel, a roll of masking tape and a marker for labels — and label everything currently brewed or stored, including the chamomile jar from chapter 1 (plant, strength, date).
4. Choose your **daily cup** slot — a real time anchored to a real habit (after breakfast, after the child's bedtime) — and write it on a label stuck to the station. Day one is tomorrow, with the strained nettle.

**How to know it worked:** tomorrow morning you strain a jar of genuinely dark, broth-like nettle, compare it against your own written prediction — and the shelf now has a working station where yesterday it had one lonely jar.
