## A familiar scene

Imagine the evening this course ends — which is this evening. You stand in front of the green shelf. It's real now: the infusion station, the ten labeled jars, a tincture that finished maturing weeks ago, the calendula oil, your own dried harvest, the safety card in ink, a blend with a formula in parts. Here is what happens to most home apothecaries, and it's worth saying plainly: they die not from ignorance but from drift. The course ends, the structure ends, and by spring the shelf is decoration and the journal is a diary that stopped. This course was built against that ending from its first page — but the mechanism was never willpower. ## The core

### The journal review protocol

A retrieval first — reach back to the very first page of the course: why did chapter 1 insist that "effect: none noticed" be written down, entry after entry? Try to answer before reading on.

Because of tonight. Tonight those entries stop being notes and become **data**. The protocol is simple and takes one honest hour: reread the entire journal, first page to last, and put one of three marks against every observation:

- **✓ Confirmed by repetition.** The effect showed up three or more separate times, in separate entries, not copied from each other. Chamomile and your evenings. The nettle jar and the next morning. Whatever *your* pages actually repeat.
- **○ Observed once.** Real, but alone. Not discarded — just not yet load-bearing. One observation is a hypothesis with good manners.
- **— Expected but never felt.** You read it, you believed it, you waited for it, and your own pages never delivered it. Write these down without flinching.

The ✓ list is your **materia medica** — ten plants deep, maybe fewer, and honestly *yours*: not what the tradition says these plants do, but what they demonstrably do in your kitchen, in your body, for your child. It's a short list. Every herbalist's real list is short. And the — list is worth almost as much, because it's the list of things you no longer have to believe: from tonight, that claim goes back to being someone else's claim, carried lightly until your own pages say otherwise.

This is the discipline the martial artist and consciousness teacher Peter Ralston spent a lifetime on: never mistake hearsay, concepts or expectation for knowing — and be exact about which one you're holding. Your journal has been quietly training you in it since day one. The three marks just make it visible.

### Reading the 80%: five questions for any source

This course taught the vital 20% — the ten plants, the methods, the safety architecture. The remaining depth lives in books, and the books are a mixed field: centuries of real observation growing right next to marketing in a linen apron. You don't need a reading list as much as a **filter**. Five questions, ten minutes with any book, blog or course — including, please note, this one:

| Question to ask | Good sign | Warning sign |
|---|---|---|
| Does the author separate tradition, trial and personal observation? | "traditionally used for… / studies suggest… / in my own practice…" — three registers, kept apart | everything served as settled fact in one voice |
| Does every plant chapter name cautions? | interactions, pregnancy, children, doses appear *per plant*, not in a disclaimer at the back | only gifts, no shadows — a plant with no cautions is a plant nobody has looked at closely |
| Is anyone selling you something? | the book stands on its own | every chapter funnels toward the author's shop, line of tinctures, or premium course |
| How is dosing handled? | ranges, ages, "start low," honest "we don't know" | heroic doses and promised cures |
| Where did the knowledge come from? | named teachers, named sources, a visible lineage | "ancient secret," "they don't want you to know" |

Two kinds of books deserve permanent shelf space. A **classic materia medica** — plant-by-plant reference with actions, preparations and cautions, the genre built for looking things up in the middle of life. And a **good flora or field key for your own region** — because the next stage of your path grows outdoors, and identification is a book-and-eyes discipline, not a book-only one.

And Buhner — named honestly, because this course owes him its soul. The conviction that a plant is a whole, living, communicating chemistry rather than a delivery vehicle for one molecule: that's his, and chapters 1 and 2 are built on it. Read him. *And* read him the way he'd be read by his own best student: his claims about direct perception are invitations to test in your own plant hours, not facts to swallow — run them through the same three marks as everything else. The journal stays the judge, even of the teachers. Especially of the teachers.

### Ten plants of your place

The kitchen ten were chosen because they're everywhere — pharmacy, market, roadside, windowsill. Your next ten are chosen by the opposite rule: **they grow within a kilometer of your door.** The plantain you now harvest was the first of them — the plant you'd walked past a thousand times until chapter 4 introduced you and chapter 8 taught you to gather it. There are at least nine more strangers on your street doing exactly what plantain did: waiting.

You already own every tool this project needs. The **plant hour** (chapter 2) is the method of acquaintance — it worked on mint, it works on whatever grows by your gate, and it is the tool forever. The **three-source rule** (chapter 8) is the gatekeeper — from memory, before reading on: what were the three sources, and what's the rule when they disagree? *(Two good books or keys, plus one live confirmation — a person who knows, or a full season of your own repeated observation. When in doubt — don't.)* That rule is not training wheels to outgrow; the herbalists who never poison anyone are the ones who never stopped using it.

One plant a season is a serious pace — four new acquaintances a year, each with a real plant hour, a real ID file, a real journal trail. In three years you'd know your ground the way chapter 1 said ten plants should be known: not a catalog, a neighborhood. The seasons will set the rhythm, and the year wheel from chapter 8 tells you what to look for when. This is also where the road leads that no course can walk for you — *your* soil, *your* winter, *your* particular weeds. Nobody can write that chapter but you.

## Worked example

The final inspection — the walk to the shelf this whole course has been pointing at. Do it exactly like this, journal open.

**Reread the promise.** Literally — open [chapter 1](/compass/herbalism-01-the-whole-plant) and read what it said this course would be: a serious apprenticeship in feeding and gently supporting a family with plants, taught rigorously or not at all, with hard boundaries where medicine begins. That was the contract. Tonight you're the inspector.

**Walk the shelf against the journal, jar by jar.** The chamomile that started everything — ✓ or not, by your own pages? The nettle jar, the thyme oxymel, the tincture, the calendula oil, the blend from chapter 10. For each jar, one question with a written answer: *what does the journal actually show this earned?*

**Find the dead weight — and take it off.** There will be some; there always is. The jar bought in enthusiasm and never opened. The preparation that got a — mark tonight. Take it off the shelf, without drama and without guilt: compost it, gift it, finish it as ordinary tea. This is not failure management — this is the same non-attachment that runs through this whole site. You are not your jars. A shelf is a working tool, not a museum of intentions, and *less, but better* applies to it exactly as it applied to the catalog of two hundred plants in chapter 1. What remains after an honest cut is not a smaller apothecary. It's a truer one.

**Write the verdict.** Five to ten lines, dated, in the journal: did the shelf keep chapter 1's promise? What does your family actually reach for without prompting? What did you expect this course to give you that it didn't? What did it give that you didn't expect? An honest mixed verdict is worth infinitely more than a glowing one — you've known that since the first review you ever read on this site.

## Practice

The closing session — about an hour, away from the screen, shelf and journal within reach:

1. **Run the review protocol:** the whole journal, first entry to last, three marks — ✓ / ○ / —. No skimming; this is the harvest of two months of writing.
2. **Write your materia medica page:** one page, your handwriting. Each ✓ plant gets one line — what it reliably does *in your house*, in your words, not the tradition's.
3. **Write the — list** on the facing page, titled honestly: *expected, never felt.* No excuses attached.
4. **Edit the shelf:** every jar defends itself against the journal, or comes off. Count what remains and write the number down.
5. **Write the verdict:** 5–10 dated lines against chapter 1's promise, mixed and honest.
6. **Open the next path:** name your first bioregion plant — the one you already suspect from your own street. Write the three sources you'll use to confirm its ID, and put its plant hour on the calendar.

**How to know it worked:** a materia medica page in your own hand; a shelf that either got smaller or defended every jar; a dated verdict in the journal — and one named stranger growing within a kilometer of your door, with an appointment.
