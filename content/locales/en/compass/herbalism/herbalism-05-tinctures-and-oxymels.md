## The core

### Four keys, not four strengths

Here is the mental model, and it's worth getting exactly right: solvents are not a ladder from weak to strong. They are **different keys cutting different doors**.

- **Water** — the everyday key, and still the widest: aromatics (partially — they also flee with steam), bitters, mucilage, and, with long patient hours, minerals. That was nettle's whole story: calcium and magnesium come out slowly, but for them water *works*.
- **Alcohol** — the key to the locked rooms: **resins** (calendula's stickiness — now you know what it was waiting for) and **many alkaloids**, plus much of what water starts, preserved for years instead of days. What alcohol is bad at is exactly nettle's treasure: minerals barely ride it. A nettle tincture is an expensive way to waste good nettle.
- **Vinegar** — the modest key with one famous specialty: **minerals**. Sour, food-grade, kitchen-native — the mineral logic of the overnight jar, transferred to a liquid that keeps for months and pours over salad.
- **Honey** — barely a solvent at all, honestly, but a matchless *carrier*: it takes up aromatics from plants like thyme, soothes as it goes down, and makes willing patients out of reluctant ones.

So a tincture is not "herbalism, but serious." Remember *plant, not pill* from chapter 1 — try to recall what it said about the preparation deciding what reaches the cup. This is that principle, third verse: the same calendula gives one medicine to water and a different one to alcohol. Neither is the plant "at full power"; each is one door opened. The craft is choosing the door on purpose.

### The folk tincture — started tonight

The folk method needs no scale, no percentages beyond what's printed on the bottle, and no equipment your shelf doesn't already hold:

1. A clean jar. Fill it **about half full, loosely,** with dried herb (chopped fresh herb: fill nearly full).
2. Cover completely with **plain 40% vodka** — two or three fingers above the plant. Everything must stay submerged; anything poking above the surface can mold.
3. Lid on. **Label, same discipline as chapter 3 but two lines longer:** plant, *menstruum and strength* (vodka 40%), date set — and the **strain date**, four to six weeks out. Write that date on the family calendar too; the jar can't remind you.
4. Dark cupboard. Shake it when you pass — daily-ish the first week, then whenever you remember. The herb drinks, swells, settles; the liquid takes on color and intent.
5. At four to six weeks: strain through cloth, squeeze the last (strongest) part out of the herb, bottle in dark glass, label again. A well-made tincture keeps for **years** — this is the long-game shelf.

Now the honest clock, because this course keeps its own: if you're pacing a chapter a week, tonight's jar comes of age somewhere around **chapter 8 or 9**. That's not a flaw in the plan — it *is* the plan. A tincture is the first preparation you make for the person you'll be in two months. Herbalism keeps two calendars, the kettle's and the season's, and today you enroll in the second.

And here is the first hard house line, stated without softening: **alcohol tinctures are an adult tool.** In this house, children get water preparations, oxymels, and glycerites (glycerin-based extracts — we name them now, chapter 7 touches dosing rules); tincture drops are dosed in an adult register, and for babies the answer isn't a smaller dose of anything — it's the pediatrician, first and always. The tincture matures on the *top* shelf, in both senses.

### Vinegar — the mineral key

Infused vinegar is the least glamorous preparation in this course and one of the most quietly useful. The classic is **nettle vinegar**: fill a jar a third with dried nettle, top up with apple cider vinegar, and here's the one piece of hard-won craft everyone learns the messy way — **vinegar eats metal lids**. A square of parchment under the lid, or a plastic lid, or you'll unscrew a ring of rust in a month. Two to four weeks in the cupboard, shake now and then, strain, label.

What you get is the overnight jar's mineral logic in a bottle that keeps about **six months**: sour, green, faintly savory. It lives in the *food* register — a spoonful in salad dressing is the daily mineral cup wearing dinner clothes, and nobody at the table needs to call it herbalism. The same bottle is the traditional hair-and-skin rinse (diluted well) — a thread chapter 6 picks up.

Taste it against its source, and let chapter 2's *taste as information* referee: the sourness is the vinegar's own, but the green depth behind it — that's the nettle, carried into a new medium.

### Honey and the oxymel

**Infused honey:** dried thyme, stirred into honey, jar closed, turned over every day or two for a couple of weeks — or hurried gently in a warm water bath (warm, never hot: cooking honey defeats the point of honey). The result is a spoonable aromatic preserve that keeps for months: thyme's warmth in the most child-friendly vehicle the kitchen owns — with one absolute exception coming a paragraph down.

**Oxymel** — the old name means "sour-honey," and the recipe is its own translation: herb, plus honey and vinegar, classically about half and half. It sounds like a mistake and tastes like a discovery: the vinegar pulls its share and brightens, the honey pulls its share and soothes, and the two together make thyme — a plant children's cups usually find "too much" — genuinely pleasant. The oxymel is the traditional cough-season spoon of European kitchens, and it's this chapter's worked example below.

Now the line this chapter exists to state, and it is absolute: **honey — plain, infused, or in an oxymel — is never given to a child under one year old.** The reason is infant botulism: honey can carry spores that are harmless to older children and adults but dangerous to infants, and **no heating, brewing, or mixing removes that risk**. This is not a strength question and not a quality-of-honey question; there is no safe version. In this house the rule goes on the jar itself — one extra label line: *"not under 1 year."* The jar teaches whoever opens it.

### Which jar for which job

The four mediums, side by side — the reference row for every preparation decision from here to the end of the course:

| Medium | Pulls best | Shelf life | Child note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Water** (infusion / decoction) | aromatics, bitters, mucilage; minerals with long hours | same day; 24–48 h in the fridge | the children's medium — everyday strength |
| **Alcohol 40%** (tincture) | resins, many alkaloids, broad spectrum preserved | years, dark glass | **adult tool** — children get water, oxymels, glycerites; babies → pediatrician |
| **Vinegar** (infused) | minerals; bright food-grade notes | ~6 months, cool and dark | food register — fine diluted in dressings |
| **Honey** (infused / oxymel) | aromatics; carries and soothes | months, closed jar (oxymel: fridge) | **never under 1 year — no exceptions** |

Notice what happened to your labels: they've grown from three lines to five — plant, form or menstruum, source, date set, strain-or-use-by date. That's the labeling discipline chapter 3 promised would stop being a joke: from tonight your shelf holds jars that look nearly identical and behave nothing alike, in a house with children. The label *is* the safety system.

And file this chapter's one forward question, because your shelf just made it urgent: of all these jars — tincture, vinegar, honey, oxymel — **which ones can be trusted to a child's skin?** Which touch is safe, which needs diluting, which is an adult-only bottle? That's **chapter 6**, and it opens exactly there.

## Worked example

Two builds tonight, walked honestly — including the parts that go wrong.

**Build one: the calendula tincture.** The plant chooses itself: calendula is the one whose resin water refused, so alcohol is her key. Half a jar of dried heads, loosely — they're bulky and will fight you a little. Vodka over the top, three fingers clear. First annoyance: the heads *float*. All of them. A submerged-plant rule and a plant that won't submerge — welcome to real craft. The fix is patience plus agitation: shake, and by day three or four the heads drink enough to start sinking. Label, five lines, strain date on the calendar: four to six weeks out. Into the dark cupboard. Done — thirty minutes, most of it label-writing, and the shelf now contains a preparation *addressed to September*.

**Build two: the thyme oxymel.** A third of a small jar of dried thyme. Now the honey argument: cold honey doesn't pour, it sulks. Stand the honey jar in warm water for ten minutes first — this is the entire secret. Mix honey and apple cider vinegar roughly half and half, pour over the thyme, stir out the air pockets (second annoyance: the bubbles are stubborn — a chopstick beats a spoon). Parchment under the lid, vinegar rule. Label — including the *"not under 1 year"* line. Two weeks in the fridge, shaken when you remember, then strain.

Before you taste anything: pull **thyme's portrait** from chapter 4 out of memory — compass corner, taste families, the caution line. Written down? Now taste the fresh mix off the stirring spoon and log the prediction:

> **Journal, 12 July — oxymel day one, prediction.** Fresh mix, spoon-tip: sour hits first, honey rounds it, thyme is already there underneath — warm, aromatic, that dry edge from the portrait. Prediction for day 14: thyme louder, edges fused, something a child would actually accept. Calendula jar set 21:30, heads floating (expected, still annoying). Strain date on the calendar: 23 August. First shake done.

That entry is doing quiet work: prediction now, verdict in two weeks — chapter 2's *expected vs sensed*, stretched across time for the first time. Get used to that stretch. From here on, the shelf always holds something that isn't ready yet.

## Practice

Kitchen, away from the screen — 45 minutes tonight, plus ten seconds a day of shaking for a while:

1. **Set the calendula tincture:** half a jar of dried calendula, loosely; 40% vodka to three fingers above; lid. Fight the floating heads with a first shake.
2. **Label it in five lines:** plant, menstruum and strength, source, date set, strain date (+4–6 weeks) — and write the strain date on the family calendar, today, or September-you will curse July-you.
3. **Build the thyme oxymel:** warm the honey first; honey and apple cider vinegar half and half over a third-jar of thyme; stir out bubbles; parchment under the lid; label — including *"not under 1 year"*; fridge, two weeks.
4. **Optional third jar,** if chapter 3's nettle bag still has depth: nettle vinegar — a third of a jar of nettle, cider vinegar to the top, parchment, cupboard, two to four weeks.
5. **Journal all of it:** one entry per jar, plus the oxymel prediction from thyme's portrait — written *before* the two weeks argue with it.

**How to know it worked:** the shelf has grown a slow corner — two or three jars whose five-line labels match dates on the family calendar, a honey jar that carries the under-1 warning in ink, and a written prediction in the journal waiting patiently to be wrong.
