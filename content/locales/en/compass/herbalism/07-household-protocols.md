## A familiar scene

The cold arrives on a Tuesday and takes the whole family by Thursday — except it isn't the same cold in any two bodies. One adult is burning: dry throat, hot skin, restless, kicking the blanket off. The child is the opposite: pale, shivery, glued to a lap, nose running like a tap. Same virus, presumably. Same word, "cold." And on the counter sits one pharmacy box of "herbal cold tea" — one answer for two people who are having two different experiences.

Chapter 6's bath evening ended with exactly this question: *when everyone is sick differently, how do you choose?* And chapter 2 sent an older question ahead to this chapter — try to answer it yourself before reading on, from memory: **why does mint help one cold and get in the way of another?** You've owned the instrument for five chapters now. This is the chapter where it earns its keep — and where the course's safety promise from chapter 1 gets paid in full, in writing, taped to your shelf.

## The core

### The red-flag list — formal, and posted

Chapter 1 promised that the red flags would one day become a formal list. Today is that day. These are the situations where the shelf closes and the doctor's step begins — not eventually, but **today**:

- **high fever, or any fever that persists** or keeps returning;
- **difficulty breathing** — of any kind, in anyone;
- **signs of dehydration** — dry mouth, no tears, little urine, unusual drowsiness;
- **allergic reaction** — swelling, hives, wheezing after any food, sting or preparation;
- **worsening after 48 hours** — whatever it is, it should be getting better by then;
- **blood where blood shouldn't be**;
- **severe pain**, or pain that wakes a person from sleep;
- **anything at all concerning a baby under one** — for infants the list has one line: pediatrician first, always.

Notice what this list is *not*: it's not fine print, and it's not a lawyer's disclaimer. It is the load-bearing wall of everything else in this chapter — the reason the rest of the matching craft is allowed to be relaxed and homely. A plant never buys delay; the list is what "never" looks like in practice. And because memory is exactly what fails at 2 a.m. with a hot child on your shoulder, this list doesn't live in your head. It lives on a card, in your handwriting, taped to the green shelf — that's this chapter's artifact, and you'll make it in tonight's practice.

### The state, not the name

Here is chapter 2's mint question, finally answered in full. A "cold" is a name; your household never contains a name — it contains a **state**. The burning adult from our opening scene is in a **hot and dry** state; the shivering child is in a **cold and damp** one. Mint is cooling — you tasted that yourself in the plant hour, felt the menthol chill arrive after the swallow. Hand mint to the hot, restless, dry-throated person and it meets the state moving toward it: relief. Hand the same mint to the pale, shivery child and you've sent a cooling plant into a body already losing the battle for warmth — at best useless, at worst one more push in the wrong direction. **Mint helps one cold and hinders another because there is no such thing as "a cold" — there are hot colds and cold colds,** and the plant doesn't read the diagnosis, it pushes its own direction regardless.

This is also the full answer to chapter 6's family question. When everyone is sick differently, you are not choosing one herb for one illness — you are reading **one state per person** and matching each separately. Same virus, three bodies, possibly three different cups. The energetic compass from chapter 2 was never a party trick; it was always this chapter waiting to happen. ### The matching chain

Now the whole craft in one line — six links, always in this order:

**The situation, named plainly → red flags? → which step → the state → the herb → the preparation and strength.**

- **Name the situation in ordinary words** — "heavy stomach after a rich dinner," not a diagnosis. If you're reaching for medical vocabulary, you're probably on step three already.
- **Red flags first, every time,** even when the answer is obviously no. The check costs five seconds; skipping it as a habit costs the one time it mattered.
- **Which step:** is this food territory (rest, broth, the everyday cup, patience) or genuinely support territory? Most household moments live on step one, and the honest matcher admits it — chapter 3's nourishing register handles more than beginners expect.
- **Read the state:** hot or cold, dry or damp — by looking and touching, not guessing: flushed or pale? dry lips or streaming? restless or heavy?
- **Pick the herb from the ten** — by its portrait, not by a search engine. Retrieval nudge: recall the daily-cup rotation from chapter 4 — every one of the ten passed through your own cup precisely so this moment wouldn't be a first date.
- **Pick the preparation and strength:** chapters 3, 5 and 6 are your menu — cup, bath, compress, oxymel, salve — and for children the strength rule below is senior to everything.

### Dosing, honestly: adult versus child

The folk tradition's dosing wisdom compresses into three words: **weakest first, always.** For adults the registers you know from chapter 3 stand as they are. For children, everything shifts down — in strength, in amount, and in which herbs are invited at all:

| Who | The cup | The house rule |
|---|---|---|
| **Adult** | full cup, everyday or strong setting | support register stays short-term; if it's becoming daily, it's a step-one question |
| **School child (~7–12)** | about half a cup, everyday strength | weakest-first; the child's taste verdict is real data, not disobedience |
| **Small child (~3–7)** | a quarter cup, brewed weak | gentle herbs only — chamomile first; mint stays out of small cups |
| **Toddler (1–3)** | spoonfuls of a weak brew, at most | prefer the bath and the compress over the cup; no menthol near face or chest |
| **Baby under 1** | — | **pediatrician only.** Bath and fresh air are the entire home toolkit; nothing by mouth — and honey, never, in anything |

Three lines from earlier chapters get repeated here on purpose, because this table is where people get hurt by enthusiasm: **honey never touches anything for a child under one** (chapter 5's rule — infant botulism); **no mint or menthol near an infant's or toddler's face or chest** (chapter 6's rule); and alcohol tinctures remain an adult tool — children get water preparations, baths and, past their first birthday, honey-based ones. When a child refuses a preparation, that's the end of the negotiation: taste is information, and forcing herbs teaches a lesson nobody wants taught.

### Herbs and medicines don't share a shelf silently

The most understated sentence in popular herbalism is this one: **interactions are real.** Herbs are chemistry — that was chapter 1's whole point — and chemistry doesn't stop being chemistry because it grew in a garden. From your own kitchen ten:

- **Garlic, ginger and yarrow all lean on blood clotting.** For anyone on anticoagulants or blood thinners, these three move from "food" to "conversation with the doctor first" — and that includes generous therapeutic amounts of garlic, not just concentrated preparations.
- **Chamomile stacks with sedatives.** Gentle is not the same as inert; alongside sleep medication, that gentleness adds up.
- **Mint can worsen reflux** — the same relaxing quality that settles one stomach loosens the wrong valve in another.
- **Pregnancy shortens the list.** **Yarrow is off it entirely.** The wider rule: during pregnancy and nursing, kitchen-strength food herbs are the register, and anything beyond it — including that maturing tincture — waits for a professional's yes.
- **The Asteraceae family — chamomile, calendula, yarrow** — shares its allergy. If ragweed season wrecks someone in your house, their patch tests and first cups deserve double patience (you met this caution in chapter 4's portraits; here is where it becomes protocol).

The blanket rule, and it is blanket: **pregnant, nursing, or on regular medication → herbs only after checking with a professional who knows the full picture.** And the household corollary that costs nothing and prevents the worst of it: your doctor should know what's in your cups — herbs and medicines from the same first-aid kit must never mix silently.

## Worked example

Four protocols, run through the chain honestly — including the messy parts.

**Protocol 1 — the cold evening.** The Thursday family from our opening scene. Red flags: checked one person at a time — fevers mild, breathing clear, everyone drinking; no flags. The burning adult: hot-dry state → cooling and moistening wants → **elderflower** from the ten, classic hot infusion for exactly this feverish-dry picture — plus the unglamorous step-one floor: rest, broth, water. The shivery child: cold-damp state → gentle warmth wants → weak **ginger** decoction, spoonful-register, sweetened with honey *because this child is four, not four months* — or thyme's territory if the cough takes over, where chapter 5's oxymel earns its shelf space. Same evening, same "cold," two opposite cups — and mint, note, in neither of them: nobody in this house is in a hot-stomach-restless state tonight. That's chapter 2's question closed for good.

**Protocol 2 — the heavy stomach.** Adult, after a celebratory dinner that won. No flags. Step: support, briefly. State: heavy, full, sluggish — not burning. The bitter path is the answer: **chamomile**, strong cup — you've known its late bitterness since the first evening of this course — or **mint** for an adult, since here (heat and heaviness, no reflux) is exactly where mint's cooling, settling direction belongs. One cup, slow, and the honest note that tomorrow's protocol is lighter dinners.

**Protocol 3 — the restless evening.** A child who can't wind down: wired, warm, the day still buzzing in them. No flags — this is barely even step two; it's mostly rhythm. The protocol is a **ritual, not a dose**: the chamomile bath from chapter 6, warm and unhurried, then a quarter-cup of weak chamomile with a story. The plant helps; the sameness helps more — the bath, the cup, the story in the same order every time is Steiner's insight doing the heavy lifting, with chamomile as its fragrant assistant. Write in the journal which nights it works and which it doesn't; that's your data, nobody else's.

**Protocol 4 — the scraped knee.** The yard delivers. First and always: **clean it** — running water, patiently, gravel out; no herb outranks washing. Then the plant layer: **plantain**, the one growing right there — a clean crushed leaf as the field poultice, the folk move that earns its reputation — or chapter 6's **calendula salve** once home, on cleaned, unbroken-around-the-edges skin. Then the boundary, from the doctor's column: deep or gaping, won't stop bleeding, from an animal or a rusty thing, or any redness that spreads in the days after — step three, today. A scraped knee is also an attunement moment, chapter 6's lesson in miniature: the washing hands matter as much as the leaf.

## Practice

Tonight, at the shelf, no screen — 30–45 minutes:

1. **Write the safety card by hand:** the eight red flags, your own handwriting, big enough to read while holding a child. Hand-writing is the point — it's the first rehearsal.
2. **Tape it to the green shelf**, eye level, where a worried 2 a.m. version of you will look first.
3. **Map your household:** one journal line per person — age, pregnancy or nursing, regular medications, known allergies (ragweed included). Check the ten against the map using this chapter's interactions section; circle any herb that needs a doctor's yes for anyone, and write that on their line.
4. **One dry run:** take a real minor situation from the past month and walk it through the chain on paper, link by link, ending at a named preparation and strength — or at "doctor," if that's where it honestly leads.
5. **Read the card aloud once.** Yes, out loud. The night you need it, you'll be reciting, not reading.

**How to know it worked:** the card is on the shelf in your handwriting; the household map exists with at least one honest circle on it if one applies; and your dry run ends in a specific preparation at a specific strength — or a specific "this was never ours to treat."
