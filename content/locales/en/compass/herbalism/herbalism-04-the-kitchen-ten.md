## The core

### The herb portrait — one grid, six lines

An encyclopedia entry tries to say everything about a plant and ends up saying nothing you'll remember. A **portrait** does the opposite: six fixed lines, always the same six, so that every plant on your shelf can be compared, recalled, and — crucially — *checked against your own senses*:

1. **Part used** — flowers, leaf, root, bulb: the part decides the method (chapter 3's rule: delicate steeps, tough simmers).
2. **Method and strength** — infusion or decoction, and which of the three dial settings is home ground: everyday cup, strong cup, nourishing jar.
3. **Energetics and taste** — the compass coordinates from chapter 2, plus the taste families that announce them.
4. **Kitchen-strength role** — what the tradition actually reaches for this plant for, at food and everyday strength. Not disease claims; household roles.
5. **The child note** — how this plant meets the youngest people in the house, if at all.
6. **One caution** — every honest portrait has one. A plant with "no cautions" is a plant nobody has looked at properly.

The portrait is not a fact sheet you copy — it's a *relationship record*. Tradition fills the frame in pencil; your tongue, your journal, your daily cup ink it in. Where you haven't verified a line yet, leave it visibly blank. Blanks are honest; borrowed certainty isn't.

**Chamomile** — you've already met her properly; she was chapter 1. Flowers; infusion at any strength from everyday to strong. Mildly cooling, aromatic with that late bitter finish you caught in your first real mug. The household role: evenings, bellies, frayed nerves, and — from chapter 6 on — skin. She is the classic first plant of childhood, the one gentle enough to be most children's introduction to the whole craft. Her one caution belongs to her family, and it's written a few portraits below.

**Nettle** — the plant that taught you ratios. Leaf; home ground is the nourishing overnight jar, 30 g to a liter, the brew that pours out like green broth. Mineral, deep, almost savory. Role: the food step, squarely — the mineral cup, soups, the family's green reserve. Child note: as food — in soup, in weak family brews — nettle is a generous plant. Caution: **dried or cooked only**. Fresh nettle stings; drying and heat disarm it completely.

**Mint** — the plant that taught you energetics, and the one whose portrait carries the sharpest child line. Leaf; everyday infusion, always covered (you know why — the aromatics ride the steam). Cooling, unmistakably; aromatic with a light astringent tail. Role: the heavy stomach, the too-hot afternoon. Now the caution, plainly: **menthol does not belong near the face or chest of babies and toddlers** — in the very young it can trigger a reflex spasm of the airways. No mint rubs, no mint steam near an infant. And for anyone with reflux, mint can loosen exactly the door that needs to stay shut. A kind plant, but not a baby's plant.

**Ginger** — the small stove from chapter 2's winter scene. Root; decoction, 15 minutes at a gentle simmer, because steeping barely dents it (that was your retrieval — check it). Warming and drying, pungent on the tongue within seconds. Role: shivery evenings, the queasy stomach — nausea is ginger's oldest reputation. Child note: in food, freely; as tea, dilute it — a child's ginger cup is a pale, honey-soft version of yours. Cautions: in large amounts it can push heartburn, and its blood-thinning tendency means anyone on anticoagulants should treat strong daily ginger as a real conversation with their doctor, not a garnish.

**Garlic** — the only member of the ten that never needs a teacup. Bulb; its method *is* food: crushed into dressing, folded into broth, roasted whole. Warming, drying, pungent — the kitchen's oldest cold-season ally. Child note: in food, yes; on skin, never — **raw garlic burns**, and a child's skin blisters where an adult's merely reddens. No garlic compresses, no folk chest-rubs on children. Second caution, same register as ginger's: garlic thins blood; on anticoagulants, big medicinal doses need a doctor's yes.

**Thyme** — the plant that out-works its soup reputation, exactly as promised. Flowering tops; everyday infusion, covered — its aromatic oil is the whole point. Warming, aromatic, lightly astringent. Role: the cough-season plant of European kitchens, the steam over a winter pot, and — next chapter — the star of your first oxymel. Child note: gentle in food and weak tea; the strong cup is adult register. One caution: like all strongly aromatic plants, more is not better — the everyday setting is thyme's honest home.

**Calendula** — the stranger on the list for most kitchens, and the one whose portrait you should write in pencil this week. Flowers — actually the whole resinous flower head. Infusion, everyday strength… and here your fingers will teach you something the books underline: rub a dried calendula head and it's *sticky*. Resin. Water takes calendula's color and some of its virtue, but that resin mostly stays behind in the strainer — file that observation; it matters very soon. Role: the skin plant in waiting — chapter 6 makes her the queen of child skin, oil and salve and bath. Child note: that whole chapter. Caution: family note below.

**Elder** — the tree of fever-season folklore, and the portrait with the strictest line in it. Two parts, two rules. **Flowers:** infusion — the honeyed, cooling cup of the European cold-season tradition, the family classic. **Berries: cooked, only, always.** Simmered into syrup or decoction they're a beloved dark-purple tradition; raw or unripe they cause nausea and vomiting, and the leaves and bark do the same — the plant defends itself with cyanogenic compounds that heat dismantles. There is no gray zone here and no folk exception: elder gives you her flowers freely and her berries through the pot.

**Yarrow** — the sternest character of the ten. Flowering tops; infusion, and your tongue will report bitter, aromatic, and astringent all at once — a plant that tastes like it means business. Drying. Role: the wound-and-fever plant of field tradition, plantain's older colleague; mostly an adult's plant, because children rarely accept the taste. Two cautions, both firm: **avoid in pregnancy**, full stop; and the family note below applies.

**Plantain** — the one underfoot, the plant you've walked past a thousand times: ribbed leaves, parallel veins, growing flat in every yard, path edge and meadow on the continent. Leaf; infusion — and, outdoors, the washed fresh leaf is the folk first-aid classic for scrapes and stings. Cooling, gently astringent with a mucilaginous softness — both soothing directions in one modest weed. Child note: this is the scraped-knee plant of every childhood, and chapter 6 will formalize it. Caution — and this is really a rule about *place*, not plant: pick only from clean ground. Never roadsides, never sprayed lawns, never the verges every dog in the neighborhood knows. Chapter 8 will turn that instinct into a proper discipline.

One family note before the table, stated once and properly: **chamomile, calendula and yarrow are all Asteraceae** — the daisy family, the same family as ragweed. For most people this means nothing. For the minority with ragweed or daisy-family allergy it means these three deserve a careful first meeting: a weak cup, a small amount, attention on. If your family carries that allergy, introduce all three the way you'd introduce any new food to a baby — slowly, one at a time, watching. Chapter 7 returns to this in the allergy section.

### The rotation — how strangers become acquaintances

Ten jars in one evening is easy — an hour's work. Ten *acquaintances* in one evening is impossible, and pretending otherwise would just rebuild the two-hundred-strangers problem this course exists to avoid. So the shelf gets a queue.

The tool is one you already hold: the **daily cup** from chapter 3, pointed down a list. Post the queue on the shelf — strangers first: calendula, yarrow, elder flower, thyme, plantain; then the half-known: nettle, ginger, mint, chamomile. Garlic is excused from the teacup — meet him in food, deliberately, one dish where he's the point. One plant per day, everyday strength, one honest journal line each. Ten days, ten first meetings — that's the honest price of acquaintance, and it's the cheapest bargain in this course.

For the true strangers, spend more than a cup: the **plant hour** from chapter 2 — try to recall its steps from memory before peeking: dry meeting, brew, slow tasting by taste family, body scan, *expected vs sensed*. Calendula and yarrow deserve the full hour; both will surprise you in different directions.

And one question to carry, not to answer yet. Ten good soloists are not an orchestra. Somewhere ahead you'll wonder how ten one-at-a-time herbs become one *smart blend* — why some plants multiply each other and some just make mud. That craft has its own chapter: **chapter 10**. Carry the question through every cup between here and there.

## Worked example

Let's build the shelf's biggest single day — and sit one plant for its portrait, start to finish.

**The gathering.** One errand: a pharmacy with loose herbs covers chamomile, nettle, mint, calendula, yarrow, elder flower and plantain leaf for surprisingly little — these are among the cheapest genuinely useful things a pharmacy sells. The grocery covers ginger, garlic, and thyme (whole dried, not powdered). Real things cost real money, so put the total in the journal; it will be one of the smallest numbers in this course, and it buys the whole cast.

**The jarring.** Ten glass jars, real lids. Into each: the plant, whole as possible. Onto each, the chapter 3 label — plant, form, source, date — because ten unlabeled jars of crumbled green leaves look *identical* by November, and guessing is exactly what this course trains you never to do.

**The portrait sitting: thyme.** Six lines, filled honestly:

1. *Part:* flowering tops.
2. *Method and strength:* infusion, everyday setting, covered — aromatic, so the lid rule from chapter 1 applies in full.
3. *Energetics and taste:* to be verified tonight — the compass says warming-aromatic; your tongue gets the final word.
4. *Kitchen-strength role:* the cough-season cup, kitchen steam, and next chapter's oxymel.
5. *Child note:* food and weak tea, gladly; strong cups stay adult.
6. *One caution:* aromatic plants don't reward "more" — everyday strength is home.

Then the cup that inks the pencil in — here's how it should land in the journal:

> **Journal, 10 July — thyme, first rotation cup.** Everyday strength, 1 tsp, covered, 12 min. Smell under the lid: warmer than the spice jar ever suggested, almost medicinal. Taste: aromatic first, then a dry, tightening edge — astringent, didn't expect that from a soup herb. Warmth noticeable in the chest a few minutes in — warming confirmed, my own data this time. Portrait line 3 inked. Queue tomorrow: calendula, full plant hour.

That's one portrait done properly. Nine to go — and the queue on the shelf already knows the order.

## Practice

One errand this week, then about 60 minutes at the kitchen table, away from the screen:

1. **Gather the ten.** Pharmacy loose herbs: chamomile, nettle, mint, calendula, yarrow, elder flower, plantain. Grocery: ginger root, garlic, dried thyme. Note the total cost in the journal.
2. **Jar and label everything** — ten jars, chapter 3 labels: plant, form, source, date. Line them up; enjoy the sight for a second, honestly — yesterday this was a cupboard of odds and ends.
3. **Write ten portrait skeletons** in the journal — one page per plant, the six lines as headers. Fill from this chapter *in pencil*; leave every unverified line visibly blank. The blanks are the plan.
4. **Post the rotation queue** on the shelf — strangers first — and mark which two get the full plant hour (calendula and yarrow are our nomination).
5. **Brew cup one of the rotation tonight**, everyday strength, and write its line. Day two is tomorrow; the queue does the remembering for you.

**How to know it worked:** ten labeled jars stand in a row where yesterday there were none; a written queue is taped beside them; the journal holds ten portrait pages — most lines still blank — and tonight's first rotation entry, in ink.
