## A familiar scene

Two jars of mint on the table. The first came from a supermarket box: greyish crumbs, a smell mostly of paper, no harvest year printed anywhere — it could be two summers old, or five. The second was dried last July: the leaves are still recognizably leaves, dark green with their edges intact, and when you open the lid the smell arrives before you've leaned in. Brew both at everyday strength and your own tongue — trained since chapter 2 — gives the verdict in one sip: the first is a memory of mint; the second is mint.

Same species. Same shelf price, nearly. The difference is everything that happened between plant and jar: who picked it, when in the year, how it dried, how long it sat in the light. Chapter 7 sent you here with an open question — *where do you get herbs you can trust?* This chapter answers it twice: first with a ladder for judging what you buy, then with the older and better answer — a July morning, a clean meadow, and your own hands. The green shelf has been waiting for this since chapter 1's timeline promised "your own dried harvest by chapter 8."

One honest line before we start: this is the chapter where herbalism steps out of the kitchen, and the one place in this course where a careless mistake can genuinely hurt you — a misidentified plant doesn't care how sincere you were. So the chapter's first concept is a discipline, and we will hold it strictly.

## The promise

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- apply the **three-source rule** before touching any plant you haven't formally met;
- rank any herb source on the **trust ladder** and judge a bought herb by color, smell, and harvest year;
- gather with **reciprocity** — taking little, leaving the stand stronger, and noticing what that changes in you;
- read the **year wheel**: what each season offers, and when in the day to gather;
- dry and store a harvest so it keeps its color, smell, and strength — labeled properly.

## The core

### The three-source rule

Here it is, the named concept this course promised back when it was an outline: **never harvest a plant unless three independent sources agree on its identity.** In practice: two good written sources — a proper regional plant key, a photographed field guide, a botanical description you can check feature by feature — plus **one live confirmation**: a person who genuinely knows the plant standing next to you, or your own repeated observation of that exact stand across a season. And the rule's second half carries equal weight: **when in doubt — don't.** There is no herb in this course worth a gamble; every one of the ten can be bought.

Is it frightening to think you might confuse one plant for another? Good — that fear is the right instrument; the three-source rule is simply fear turned into procedure. Most of the kitchen ten are hard to mistake: nettle stings hello, mint announces itself to your fingers, plantain's parallel veins and rosette are close to unmistakable. But yarrow grows in the same meadows as white-flowered umbellifers — a family that contains some of the most poisonous plants of the temperate world — and "feathery leaves, white flat flower head" describes both to a beginner in a hurry. The rule exists exactly for that beginner, and it works because the three sources fail independently: a bad photo match, a wrong assumption, an over-confident friend rarely all line up on the same plant.

Remember the **plant hour** from chapter 2 — and before reading on, try to recall *why* this course put senses before books. Here is that answer, promoted to a safety tool: a stand you have visited across a season — spring rosette, summer flower, the smell of a crushed leaf in your fingers — is a plant you *know*, the way you know a neighbor's face. Repeated seasonal observation is a legitimate third source precisely because it is slow. Identification is not a lookup; it is an acquaintance with a paper trail.

### The trust ladder — where herbs worth trusting come from

Now the bought half of chapter 7's question. Sources are not equal, and the ladder runs:

1. **Your own harvest** — you know the plant, the place, the day, the drying. Nothing else offers that chain of custody.
2. **A named local grower or herb farm** — a person with a face and a reputation, who can tell you the harvest year without checking.
3. **Pharmacy loose herbs** — controlled species identity (that part is genuinely valuable), variable freshness; read the packing date.
4. **Anonymous dusty teabags** — the bottom rung. Species usually right, everything else unknowable.

Whatever the rung, the same three checks — and your chapter 2 training does all the work. **Color:** alive, close to the living plant's; grey-brown means the plant left long ago. **Smell:** an aromatic herb should announce itself when the jar opens; a scentless thyme is a spent thyme. **Harvest year:** printed or asked for; this year's or last year's for leaves and flowers, and if nobody can tell you — that silence is the answer. One honest cost to name: herbs that pass all three checks cost more than teabag dust. That is not a markup on romance; color and smell are the active matter, and you are paying for the presence of the thing itself.

### Reciprocity — take less than you're tempted to

Gathering has rules older than any field guide, and Stephen Harrod Buhner spent his life arguing they are not etiquette but the actual relationship the whole craft rests on. The working set:

- **Never take the first plant you meet.** Walk on; find the stand, not the individual.
- **Take a third at most** from any stand — a visitor's share, never a harvester's clear-cut.
- **Never take rare, struggling, or lonely plants.** Abundance is the invitation; scarcity is a closed door.
- **Leave the stand stronger:** scatter ripe seed, clear what chokes, break nothing you don't take.
- **And say thank you.** Out loud, if you like — there is nobody in the meadow to be embarrassed in front of, and the words cost nothing.

We won't tell you a plant hears you; that claim belongs to Buhner, not to us, and this site doesn't retail beliefs. We'll tell you what is checkable instead: gathering this way *changes the gatherer*. A person moving slowly enough to choose a stand, count thirds, and thank a plant is a person who is actually looking — and actually looking is nine-tenths of identification and nine-tenths of quality. Run the comparison yourself this month and let the journal judge: one hurried gathering, one reciprocal one, and note the difference in what you noticed, what you took, and what your jars look like. Check for yourself — the course's oldest rule, applied to its oldest tradition.

### The year wheel — and when in the day

The shelf lives on a calendar older than shops. Around the wheel:

- **Spring** — leaves and shoots, gathered young: nettle above all, before it flowers, when the leaf is tender and the minerals you met in chapter 3's overnight jar are at their peak.
- **Early summer** — flowers: elderflower in its brief creamy fortnight, chamomile head by head.
- **High summer** — flowering tops, the peak of the aromatic year: yarrow, thyme, mint, calendula in full flower; and plantain's leaves, generous all season long.
- **Autumn** — roots and berries: rosehips after the first cold nights and elderberries — with the portrait warning from chapter 4 now in full force: **elderberries only cooked, always.** Raw or unripe berries — and the plant's leaves and bark at any time — cause real nausea and vomiting (cyanogenic glycosides); cooking dismantles the problem entirely. Flowers fine, cooked berries fine, everything else is not food. (Ginger, the ten's one import, stays bought — this wheel is for what grows around you.)
- **Winter** — jars, tea, and the journal. Not an off-season: the season where the shelf pays out and the notes get read. The wheel turns whether you gather or not.

Within the day, the tradition and the drying rack agree: **a dry morning, after the dew has burned off and before the heat** — wet plants mold, and afternoon sun has already driven off part of the aromatic fraction you're coming for. The tradition goes further and gathers by the moon — leaves on the waxing, roots on the waning. We won't hand you that as fact; we'll hand it to you the way this site hands over everything of its kind: as a rhythm to *test*. [The almanac on our front page](/) computes the real phase from a real ephemeris — no horoscope-column fluff, actual celestial mechanics. Note the phase in every harvest entry this year, and let a season of your own jars tell you whether it earns a place in your practice. Serious, or not at all — that rule holds here too.

### Drying and storing — where harvests are won and lost

Everything to this point can be done perfectly and still lost in a week. Drying is where the real cost of impatience lives: **damp plant material in a closed jar molds, and a moldy jar is the whole harvest gone** — a month of the year wheel, unrecoverable until next season. The craft is plain:

| Harvest | Dry it | Ready when | Jar and keep |
|---|---|---|---|
| Leaves (nettle, mint, plantain) | thin layer on paper, shade, moving air | crumbles between fingers | glass, dark place, kept whole-ish; ~1 year |
| Flowers (chamomile, calendula, elderflower) | thin layer, never in sun | petals papery, base of the head fully dry | glass, dark; ~1 year |
| Flowering tops (yarrow, thyme, mint) | small loose bundles, hung in shade | stems snap instead of bending | glass, dark; ~1 year |
| Roots and berries (autumn) | washed, sliced; days longer than you'd think | pieces snap clean, no soft center | glass, dark; roots 2–3 years, elderberries cooked at use |

The test is always the same: **crisp.** A leaf that bends is not done; a stem that folds is not done. Shade and air do the work — the sun that fed the plant standing bleaches it lying down.

Storage keeps the chapter 3 discipline and grows it by one line. Remember the two label lines that were never optional — try to recall them before reading on. Plant and date, strength when brewed; the harvest label carries **plant + place + month and year**: "Plantain — upper meadow — July 2026." Place matters because next year you'll want to return to the good stand and skip the bad one; the label is a letter to your future self. Keep dried herbs as whole as storage allows and crumble at use — every broken edge is a door for the smell to leave through. The working rule for the shelf: **one year for leaves and flowers.** When the wheel comes back around, the old jar goes to the compost and the new harvest takes the shelf — that's not waste, that's the wheel.

## Worked example

A July gathering hour, walked through the way your journal should catch it. July is generous to a beginner: plantain, yarrow, and mint are all standing in flower, and one of them — plantain — you were promised back in chapter 3, the plant you'd walked past a thousand times.

The site first: a meadow edge up and away from the road, no sprayed field uphill, above dog height, not a verge. (Chapter 4's portrait warned you about roadside plantain; here that note becomes the rule for *every* plant: a clean plant from a clean place, or no plant.) Then the three sources, in writing:

> **Journal, 12 July, 09:40 — plantain, first own harvest.** ID check: (1) field guide — rosette, broad oval leaves, parallel veins running the length, tough strings when the stalk snaps: all confirmed in hand. (2) Regional key — flower spike leafless, seed head a narrow brown column: confirmed, three spikes checked. (3) Live — this is the stand by the walnut tree I've watched since May; same rosettes, same place, photographed then and now. Three of three. Moon (from the almanac): waxing, four days to full — noting per the experiment, verdict owed in October.
> Took maybe a fifth of the big stand, leaf by leaf, skipped the first cluster and everything small. Said thank you out loud. Felt foolish for exactly three seconds; gathered slower and noticed more for the rest of the hour — two leaves with rust stayed where they were, which last month I'd have bagged without looking.
> Drying: single layer on paper on top of the bookshelf — shade, air, out of the baby's reach. Label written in advance and waiting: "Plantain — meadow by the walnut — July 2026."

Four days later, if the weather holds, the leaves crumble instead of bending, the jar closes on the year's first own harvest, and the label goes on — and the green shelf crosses a line it can't cross any other way: from a shelf of things you bought to a shelf with a place and a season in it.

Notice what the entry did *not* do: it didn't rush, it didn't take much, and it didn't skip the written ID check even for a plant that seems obvious. The discipline is cheap on the day you don't need it — that's what makes it there on the day you do.

## Practice

One gathering hour outdoors this week, plus ten minutes on jarring day — 60 minutes total of actual work:

1. **Choose one July plant** from the ten: plantain, yarrow, or mint. (Yarrow only if your live source is strong — it's the one with the treacherous look-alikes.)
2. **Run the three-source check in writing:** field guide, second written source, live confirmation — a knowledgeable person or your own documented observation of the stand over time. Any disagreement, any hesitation → don't harvest. That's the rule working, not failing.
3. **City fallback, honestly equal:** if no clean stand or no third source exists where you live, buy pharmacy loose herb instead and run the *trust-ladder audit* on it — color, smell, harvest year, journal verdict. The skill this chapter builds is judgment, and it works on bought herbs too.
4. **Check the site:** away from roads and verges, no sprayed land uphill, above dog height.
5. **Gather with the reciprocity rules:** not the first plant, a third at most, nothing rare, scatter ripe seed if you find it — and the thank-you is part of the protocol. Note in the journal what gathering slowly changed about what you saw.
6. **Set the drying:** thin layer on paper or small hung bundles, shade, moving air. Write the full label now — plant + place + month/year — and leave it waiting by the tray.
7. **On crisp day** (leaves crumble, stems snap — days later, be patient): jar, label, shelf. Add the moon phase from [the almanac](/) to the harvest entry — your October self will want the data.

**How to know it worked:** within the week, a labeled jar of your own dried plant stands on the green shelf (or a bought jar that passed a written trust-ladder audit) — and your journal holds a three-source record you'd be willing to show the strictest person you know.

## Now you can

- Run the three-source rule on an unfamiliar plant — and refuse the harvest when the sources disagree.
- Rank a source on the trust ladder and audit any bought herb by color, smell, and harvest year.
- Gather by the reciprocity rules and say what they change in the gatherer, not just the meadow.
- Place each of the ten on the year wheel and pick the right morning to gather.
- Dry to crisp, jar in glass and darkness, and label with plant, place, and month — a letter to next July.
