## What cup I had in mind

The plan in one or two lines — which bean, which brewing method, what taste
you expected to get and at what moment of the day.

## What actually came out in the cup

The real version. Specifics instead of adjectives: water temperature, grind,
extraction time, that particular flavor detail you still remember. If
something didn't go according to the recipe — and it usually doesn't —
say so: that deviation is most often the real protagonist of the note.

## Where the coffee didn't work out

The moment it got hard or disappointing — over-roasted, too much water poured,
the taste was off. Name out loud exactly what didn't work — a named failure
makes the rest true. And what you tried to fix.

## What saved the cup (or what you'd change in the brewing)

The way out — or what you'd do differently next time: different grind,
different water, different time. One honest conclusion is enough — and
"haven't figured it out yet" counts too.

## What's worth remembering about this coffee

The detail that proved itself, a small brewing trick, a phrase about the
taste — something the reader can actually use in their own kitchen. Leave
them with something — as if sending a friend home with a bag of beans.

<!-- ————— Form 2: this is how we live ————— -->

## How I brew coffee every day

The practice in simple words — which method, which equipment — and what it
replaced. One honest line about why many people do it differently wouldn't
hurt; no preaching needed — the cup itself is the argument.

## How this coffee story started

A habit has a birthday too: a moment, disappointment with an old method,
a person, an article, or a recipe that started it all. Begin there — that
particular story is what makes the rest come alive.

## An ordinary morning with this cup

Specifics instead of theory: the hour, the sequence of actions, the actual
equipment, what's happening around at that time. Show how it works on an
ordinary weekday, not on a perfect day off.

## What this coffee habit costs

Everything real has a price — money for beans and equipment, time for
brewing, what you gave up for it. Name it: a named cost is what makes
the advice true.

## If you'd like to brew this way too

The smallest honest first step — and one beginner's mistake I'd want to
spare a friend from. Leave the reader with something to start their cup with.
