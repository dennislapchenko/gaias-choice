<!-- ————— Shape 1: a story ————— -->

## What we set out to do

The plan, in a line or two — what the workday was supposed to look like: where
you meant to park, what signal you were counting on, what was on the calendar
that day.

## What actually happened

The real version. Concrete beats adjectives: the hour the call started, the
spot you actually parked in, the moment the signal dropped or held. If the
baby's rhythm rewrote the schedule — it usually does — say so plainly; that
rhythm is often the real protagonist, not the meeting.

## The hard part

The moment it got difficult — the call that cut out, the deadline against a
dying battery, the spot with no signal and no backup plan. Name it out loud
first; naming the fear is what makes the rest believable. Then what you tried.

## What worked (or what we'd change)

The fix, or the thing you'd do differently next time — a different app, a
different hour, a different kind of spot. One honest lesson is enough — and
it's allowed to be "we still haven't solved this."

## One thing worth keeping

A piece of gear, a hotspot trick, a rule about where you'll park to work —
anything a reader could actually use tomorrow. Leave them something, the way
you'd send a friend home with a jar.

<!-- ————— Shape 2: a way we live ————— -->

## What we do

The setup in plain words — desk, signal, hours, how work and driving and the
kids actually share the same square meters. What it replaced (an office, a
commute, a fixed schedule), and one honest line on why most people wouldn't
do it this way; no preaching needed, the doing is the argument.

## How it came into our life

Every setup like this has an origin story: the job that made it possible, the
day you decided the office wasn't worth it, the first terrible call from a
parking lot that taught you something. Start there — the story is what makes
the rest land.

## An ordinary day with it

Concrete beats theory: the hour you open the laptop, where the van is parked,
what the connection actually looks like, what the baby does while you're on
mute. Show a normal Tuesday's workday, not the postcard version.

## What it costs

Everything real has a price — the signal you don't have, the meetings you
can't take, the money spent on data or power, the hours lost hunting for a
spot with both quiet and bars. Name it; the named cost is what makes the
recommendation believable.

## If you want to try it

The smallest honest first step — one call taken from the van before you
commit to more — and the one mistake we'd warn a friend away from. Leave the
reader somewhere to start.
