## What we planned

A one-to-two-line plan — what food was being made, what we expected from the
table, what this lunch or dinner was supposed to be.

## How it actually turned out

The real version. Specifics instead of adjectives: what actually ended up on
the plate, who refused to eat, who asked for seconds, that one detail of
taste or smell you still remember. If hunger or a toddler's whim rewrote the
plan — and it usually does — say so: that hunger is most often the real
main character.

## The hardest part

The moment things got hard. Name it out loud — no one ate, someone was
picky, nothing worked out — it's exactly the named failure that makes the
rest true. And what was tried to fix it.

## What helped (or what we'd change)

The way out — or what we'd do differently next time with the recipe, the
serving, the timing. One honest conclusion is enough — and "we still don't
know how to get him to eat vegetables" counts too.

## What's worth taking away

A recipe, a hack, a phrase said at the table — something the reader can
actually use in their own kitchen. Leave them with something — the way
you'd send a friend home with a jar of jam.

<!-- ————— Form 2: how we live ————— -->

## What we do

How we eat well day to day — and what it replaced: pre-made food, snacks on
the run, cooking out of obligation. One honest line about why most people
eat differently wouldn't hurt; no need to preach — the food on the table
itself is the argument.

## How it came into our life

Approaches to food have an origin story too: a day, a problem with someone's
appetite, a person or a recipe where it all started. Start there — it's the
story that brings the rest to life.

## An ordinary day with this

Specifics instead of theory: what's made for breakfast, lunch, dinner, how
long it takes, what the toddler is doing at the table meanwhile. Show how it
works on an ordinary Tuesday, not on a perfect holiday.

## What it costs

Everything real has a cost — time at the stove, money on groceries, nerves
if someone won't eat what was made. Name it: it's the named cost that makes
the advice true.

## If you'd like to try it

The smallest honest first step in the kitchen — and one mistake we'd spare
a friend from. Leave the reader with something to start with.
