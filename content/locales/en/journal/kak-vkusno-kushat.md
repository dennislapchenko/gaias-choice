<!-- ————— Form 1: story ————— -->

## What we planned

The plan in one or two lines — what food was being cooked, what we expected
from the table, what this lunch or dinner was supposed to be.

## What actually happened

The real version. Specifics instead of adjectives: what ended up on the
plate, who refused to eat, and who asked for seconds, that particular detail
of taste or smell you still remember. If hunger or a toddler's whim rewrote
the plan — and it usually does — say so: that hunger is most often the main
character of the story.

## The hardest part

The moment it got hard. Say it out loud — didn't eat, was picky, nothing
worked out — it's the named failure that makes the rest true. And what was
tried to fix it.

## What helped (or what we'd change)

The way out — or what we'd do differently next time with the recipe, the
serving, the timing. One honest conclusion is enough — and "we still don't
know how to get him to eat vegetables" counts too.

## What's worth taking away

A recipe, a hack, a phrase said at the table — something the reader can
really use in their own kitchen. Leave them with something — the way you'd
send a friend home with a jar of jam.

<!-- ————— Form 2: how we live ————— -->

## What we do

How we eat well day to day — and what it replaced: convenience food,
on-the-go snacking, cooking out of obligation. One honest line about why
most people eat differently wouldn't hurt; no need to preach — the food on
the table is argument enough.

## How it came into our life

Approaches to food have an origin story too: a day, an appetite problem, a
person or recipe it all started with. Start there — it's the story that
makes the rest come alive.

## An ordinary day with this

Specifics instead of theory: what we make for breakfast, lunch, dinner, how
long it takes, what the toddler is doing at the table meanwhile. Show how it
works on an ordinary Tuesday, not on a perfect holiday.

## What it costs

Everything real has a price — time at the stove, money on groceries, nerves,
if someone won't eat what's been made. Name it: it's the named price that
makes the advice true.

## If you want to try it

The smallest honest first step in the kitchen — and one mistake we'd spare a
friend from. Leave the reader with a place to start.
