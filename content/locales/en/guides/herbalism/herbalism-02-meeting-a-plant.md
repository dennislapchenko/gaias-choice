## A familiar scene

A cold winter evening, and someone kindly makes you a big mug of mint tea. Warm mug, warm steam — and twenty minutes later you're reaching for a blanket, vaguely *chillier* than before. Meanwhile your grandmother's remedy for the same shivery evening was ginger, and that one worked from the inside out, like a small stove.

Both are "nice herbal teas." So why do they push in opposite directions? Because plants have *temperaments* — directions they move a body in — and mint's is cold while ginger's is hot, regardless of the water's temperature. Herbalism has carried this grammar for a few thousand years across every major tradition (Greek, Chinese, Ayurvedic — each with its own dialect of the same observation). Miss it, and herbs seem random. Learn it, and you can predict half of a plant's behavior before you've looked anything up.

Quick retrieval from chapter 1 before we go on — from memory: why did covering the chamomile mug matter, and what does *plant, not pill* say about where a plant's action lives? Ten seconds. That principle is about to become a *felt* skill.

## The promise

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- describe any preparation on two **energetic** axes: warming/cooling, drying/moistening;
- read the five key **tastes** as predictions of action — bitter, aromatic, astringent, mucilaginous (demulcent), pungent;
- run the **plant hour** — a full-senses first meeting with one plant, recorded properly;
- separate what you sensed from what you expected — the journal discipline, now applied to your own tongue.

## The core

### Energetics: warming/cooling

The first axis every tradition agrees on. **Warming** plants — ginger, garlic, thyme — increase circulation and heat sensation: flushed skin, warmth spreading from the center, that inner-stove effect. **Cooling** plants — mint, chamomile (mildly) — do the opposite; mint's menthol literally triggers the skin's cold receptors, which is why a hot mint tea can leave you chillier on a winter night. Neither direction is "better": a hot, red, agitated state calls for cooling; a pale, shivery, sluggish one calls for warming. The skill is matching the plant's direction to the state in front of you — and getting that wrong is exactly the mint-on-a-winter-evening mistake, now explained.

### Energetics: drying/moistening

The second axis. **Drying** plants — sage famously, and every astringent — tighten tissues and reduce secretions. **Moistening** plants — the mucilage carriers like marshmallow root, and to a gentler degree oats and violet — coat, soothe and lubricate. A dry hacking cough wants moistening, not more drying; a wet, streaming state wants the astringents. Two axes make a compass: ginger is warming-and-drying, marshmallow cooling-and-moistening. Rough coordinates, honestly gathered, beat precise-sounding claims copied from the internet — you'll gather your own tonight.

### Taste as information

Here is the part that turns your tongue into an instrument. Taste isn't a side effect of a plant — in herbal tradition it's a *readout* of chemistry, and each major taste predicts a family of actions:

- **Bitter** (chamomile's late finish, dandelion leaf) — wakes up digestion: saliva, stomach juices, bile. The bitterness *is* the digestive action announcing itself — you met it in chapter 1's mug without knowing its name.
- **Aromatic** (mint, thyme, chamomile's honeyed sharpness) — volatile oils: antimicrobial tendencies, digestion-settling, mood-touching. Anything you can smell across the room is aromatic — and remember from chapter 1 *why* the mug gets covered: those oils leave with the steam.
- **Astringent** (strong black tea's mouth-pucker, oak, raspberry leaf) — tannins tightening tissue: the drying direction made tangible.
- **Mucilaginous / demulcent** (marshmallow, chia-water sliminess) — slippery coating: the moistening direction made tangible.
- **Pungent** (ginger, garlic) — heat on the tongue: the warming direction made tangible.

Notice what just happened: three of the five tastes *are* the energetic axes, felt directly. The compass isn't book knowledge — it's in your mouth.

### The plant hour

Buhner taught that the deepest herbal knowledge comes from **direct, unhurried attention to one plant at a time** — senses first, books second. He'd say the perception runs deeper than the senses; we'll make you no such claim and simply hand you the practice, because as a *discipline of attention* it demonstrably works: one plant, one hour, all senses, notes.

A small honest note, in keeping with this site's rules: Buhner's "heart perception" has no scientific framework behind it, and we're not going to pretend otherwise. We offer the plant hour as what it verifiably is — the fastest way we know to move a plant from *category* to *acquaintance* — and your journal, not any doctrine, remains the judge of what it yields. Try it before you file it anywhere. («Проверьте сами», as the Russian side of this site says — check for yourself.)

What an apprentice notices in a real plant hour reliably outstrips what the same person retained from an hour of reading. Why does this work? Partly it's just bandwidth — smell, taste, texture, temperature-feel all encoding memory at once. Partly it's the difference between information *about* and experience *of* — the same line this site draws everywhere.

## Worked example

Let's run a compressed plant hour on **mint** — the plant from our opening scene — the way your journal should catch it:

> **Journal, 7 July — mint, plant hour (40 min of it).** Dried leaves, rubbed between fingers: smell jumps out before the kettle's even on — aromatic, confirmed, strongly so. Infusion: 1 tsp, covered, 10 min. First sip held long on the tongue: cool arrives *after* the swallow, spreading, faint menthol chill at the back of the throat — cooling, felt directly, not read about. Slight tongue-tightening at the end — mild astringent, hadn't expected that. Belly: something eases in the upper stomach within minutes — the aromatic-digestive effect, presumably. Warming/cooling verdict: cooling, no doubt. Drying/moistening: mildly drying (that tightening). Where the winter-evening mistake comes from: now obvious — I handed a cooling plant to an already-chilled person.
> *Expected vs sensed:* expected "fresh"; did not expect the astringency or how *physical* the cooling is.

Look at the last line especially. *Expected vs sensed* is chapter 1's observer discipline turned inward — the tongue fools easily when a book has told it what to find, so we write down both and let them argue. And notice the compass working: two axes plus tastes gave us a working profile of mint from one mug, before any reference was opened. Chapter 4 will hand you ten plants; you now own the instrument that will make them ten *acquaintances* rather than ten catalog entries.

One thing this chapter deliberately leaves open: knowing mint is cooling still doesn't tell you what to *reach for* when a specific cold, a specific stomach, a specific sleepless child is in front of you — matching plant to situation is a craft of its own, and it's **chapter 7**. Carry the question; the compass comes first.

## Practice

The plant hour — one hour, away from every screen, this week:

1. Choose **one** plant: mint or chamomile (you already own the chamomile — meeting it *again*, properly, is allowed and even elegant: you'll see how much chapter 1's mug missed).
2. **Dry meeting (10 min):** loose plant in your palm. Look — actual flower heads, leaf shapes. Rub and smell. Note texture, color, dust, anything.
3. **Brew (per chapter 1):** covered, generous, patient. Use the wait to write the dry-meeting notes.
4. **Tasting (20 min):** smell under the lid first. Then slow sips — hold each on the tongue for a count of ten before swallowing. Hunt each taste family from this chapter by name: any bitterness? Where? When? Astringent tightening? Aromatic bloom? Pungent heat? Cooling spread?
5. **Body scan (15 min):** sit quietly with the empty mug. Temperature shifts, belly, breath, mood. Boring is fine — write "nothing noticed," it's honest data, same rule as always.
6. **Journal:** the full entry, ending with *expected vs sensed* — one line for each.

**How to know it worked:** your entry contains at least one sensation you did **not** expect — and you can place your plant on both energetic axes from your own evidence, not from anything you read.
