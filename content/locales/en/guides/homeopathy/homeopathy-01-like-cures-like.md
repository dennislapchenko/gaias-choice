## The core

### Like cures like

In 1790, a German physician named Samuel Hahnemann was translating a medical textbook and doubted its explanation of how cinchona bark (the source of quinine) cured malaria. He did something unusual for his time: he took repeated doses of it himself, while healthy — and developed, by his account, fever-like symptoms resembling the very disease it treated. From this and years of further experiments he formulated the principle he published in 1796: *similia similibus curentur* — let like be cured by like. A substance that produces a set of symptoms in a healthy person may, in small doses, help a sick person whose illness shows a **similar** set of symptoms.

The everyday example is the onion. Slice one and you get streaming eyes and a watery, dripping nose. Homeopathically prepared onion — *Allium cepa* — is a classic remedy for exactly that kind of cold: the one with bland, watery eyes and a nose that runs like a tap. Coffee keeps a healthy person's mind racing at midnight; *Coffea* is a traditional remedy picture for sleeplessness from an over-active, excited mind.

Note what the principle is **not**: it is not "herbs" (herbalism works with material doses of whole plants — that's our other course), and it is not "natural = safe = works". It is one specific idea: match the remedy's known symptom picture to the sick person's symptom picture.

### The picture, not the diagnosis

Modern medicine names the disease: influenza, otitis, gastritis. Homeopathy asks a stranger question: **how exactly are *you* ill?** Two people with the same flu can be ill in opposite ways. One lies motionless, dry-lipped, irritable, wanting only to be left alone in a dark room; every movement hurts. The other is weepy, clingy, wants windows open and company close, and feels better for gentle fuss. Same virus, same diagnosis — two completely different **pictures**, and the tradition matches remedies to pictures, not to diagnoses.

| Same flu, side by side | One person | The other |
|---|---|---|
| Mood | grim, irritable | weepy, clingy |
| Company | wants everyone gone | wants company close |
| The room | dark, curtains closed | windows open |
| Movement & touch | every movement hurts | better for gentle fuss |
| **The picture** | **stillness and solitude** | **air and closeness** |

This is why there is no "the homeopathic remedy for flu" — and why the whole discipline stands or falls on the quality of your observation. Which is why your journal, not your remedy kit, is this course's first purchase.

### The small dose

Hahnemann's second pillar: use the **minimum dose** that acts — the least intervention needed. In practice he went much further, developing serial dilution with vigorous shaking ("potentization"), which produced remedies diluted past the point where chemistry expects any molecules of the source substance to remain. Chapter 3 unpacks the whole system — what the C and D numbers on a label mean, why 30C became the home standard, and how the tradition's "less is more" logic actually gets applied at the bedside.

For now, keep the principle: in this tradition, *more* is never the answer. One remedy at a time, the gentlest dose that responds, stop when the response comes.

**The honest note — read this part slowly.** Precisely because of those extreme dilutions, mainstream science rejects homeopathy: large modern reviews conclude that its effects are not distinguishable from placebo, and at 12C and beyond, not a single molecule of the original substance is expected in the vial. The tradition disputes how those studies handle individualization; the debate is old and unresolved, and we will not pretend to settle it. Our position is the one this whole site is built on: **don't believe — test.** Practice only within the safe scope defined below, write down every case honestly in your journal — including the failures — and let your own recorded experience, not our enthusiasm and not anyone's scorn, tell you what this method is worth in your home. That discipline of honest self-observation is worth having even if you end the course a skeptic.

### Red flags — the boundary that makes everything else possible

Homeopathy at home has exactly one legitimate territory: **minor, self-limiting complaints** — the bruises, teething nights, mild colds and upset stomachs that resolve on their own, where the question is comfort and attentiveness. It has zero legitimate territory in emergencies, infections that need treatment, chronic disease, or anything involving a very young baby.

Burn this list in now; chapter 6 will formalize it. A doctor — immediately, not "after we try something first" — for: any fever in an infant under three months; difficulty breathing; signs of dehydration; a stiff neck with fever; unusual drowsiness or unresponsiveness; seizures; severe or worsening pain; anything that is getting worse despite care; and anything that simply frightens you in your gut. **A remedy must never buy delay.** The tradition's own best practitioners insist on this; so do we, without exception. For babies, the rule is even simpler: the pediatrician comes first, always.

## Worked example

Meet the two things you will build across all 11 chapters — the course's spine:

1. **The observation journal** — a paper notebook where every observation, every remedy given, and every outcome (including "nothing happened") gets a dated entry.
2. **The family kit** — a small labeled box that will hold about ten remedies by chapter 4. Tonight it can be any sturdy box with a lid; its first vial arrives in chapter 3.

Here is what a first journal entry can look like — say, for yourself, on an ordinary evening while a cold is starting:

> **5 July, 22:15 — self.** Throat felt scratchy since ~17:00. Chilly, wanted a sweater though the room is warm. Thirst: almost none. Mood: irritable, don't want conversation. Better: hot tea, lying still. Worse: getting up, cold air from the window. Slept badly last night (worth noting — this started after that).

Nothing clever. No conclusions, no remedy names, no self-diagnosis — just what you would testify to. Look at how much is already there, though: temperature reactions, thirst, mood, what helps and what worsens. In chapter 2 you'll learn that these plain details are the exact raw material the entire method runs on, and that most of them are the ones everyone forgets to notice.

## Practice

Tonight, away from any screen — 20–30 minutes:

1. Find a **paper** notebook you like enough to keep on the shelf for months, and a pen that lives with it. Write the date on page one and the words "Observation journal".
2. Sit for ten quiet minutes and observe the person you know best: yourself. Then write your first entry covering, at minimum: sleep last night, appetite today, warmth/chilliness, thirst, mood, and one thing that made you feel better or worse today.
3. Choose the future kit box — sturdy, with a lid, big enough for ~10 small vials plus an index card. Put the journal next to it. That shelf corner is now the course's home.
4. Name the cost honestly, in writing, on the journal's first page: this course will ask roughly the price of ten remedy vials by chapter 4 (a modest sum — check your local pharmacy's prices for 30C tubes and write the number down), plus 1–2 hours per chapter. Real things have real prices; we start by seeing them.

**How to know it worked:** your journal has a dated first entry that you can read aloud and every sentence of it is something you *observed*, not something you *concluded*.
