> **Founder guide.** This is one of five internal guides we wrote for ourselves
> while bootstrapping this site. We're building in public — once we've actually
> done these things, these guides get replaced by real reader-facing content.

## What we are actually building

Gaia's Choice is a **niche affiliate content site**. The business model, stripped
of romance:

1. We publish **honest, first-hand reviews and guides** about natural,
   plastic-free gear for traveling with a baby.
2. People searching for exactly that find us (search, Pinterest, word of mouth).
3. Some of them buy through our links, and the shop pays us a small commission —
   at no cost to the buyer.

That's it. There are only two assets in this business, and everything we do
either builds them or wastes time:

- **Trust** — readers believe us because we actually used the gear and say so,
  including what disappointed us.
- **Traffic** — enough of the right people find the site.

Trust compounds slowly and dies instantly. Traffic follows trust with a 6–12
month lag. There is no shortcut where either arrives first.

## The uncomfortable truth about our current content

Everything on this site today — the reviews, the old guides, the About story —
was **AI-generated placeholder text** describing a fictional family's seven
months on the road. It reads fine. It is also exactly the content Google's
review-system updates are built to bury, and exactly what a real parent smells
from a paragraph away.

The rule from here: **nothing stays on this site that isn't literally true.**
Every claim of experience must be ours. Every photo must be ours. Every product
reviewed must be one we bought (ideally with our own money) and used. The
placeholder content's only job was to make the layout real — its replacement is
the actual work.

## Order of operations

Do these in order. Each one is pointless without the one before it.

1. **Decide who "we" are.** Whose name and face is on the About page? What is
   our *true* story — even if it's "we're two parents who just started caring
   about this"? An honest beginner beats a fictional expert. Rewrite
   `about.md` to be true.
2. **Own 5–10 products for real.** Buy the gear we'd genuinely research for our
   own kid. Use each item for at least a couple of weeks. Take photos with our
   own hands in the frame.
3. **Write the first 10 real reviews** using the process in
   [Writing Reviews That Earn Trust](/guides/writing-reviews-that-earn-trust).
   Delete or rewrite every placeholder review as we go.
4. **Launch properly** — domain, real email, disclosure and privacy pages,
   analytics, Search Console. The full list is in the
   [Launch Checklist](/guides/launch-checklist).
5. **Publish on a rhythm and build traffic** — see
   [Getting Traffic](/guides/getting-traffic-seo-and-pinterest). One good piece
   a week beats ten in January and silence until June.
6. **Apply for affiliate programs only once there's real content** — see
   [How This Site Will Make Money](/guides/how-this-site-will-make-money).
   Applying with placeholder content gets us rejected or banned.

The phased version with milestones lives on the public [Roadmap](/roadmap).

## The weekly rhythm

A content site is a habit, not a project. A sustainable starting rhythm:

- **One review or guide per week**, properly done: used the product, took the
  photos, wrote the verdict we'd give a friend.
- **One older piece improved** (better photos, an update note, a fixed claim).
- **A few pins** to Pinterest from the week's content.
- **Fifteen minutes in Search Console** looking at what queries we're showing
  up for — that's next month's content list.

## What failure looks like (so we can recognize it)

- Publishing reviews of products we haven't touched, because it's faster.
- Chasing commission rates instead of reader questions.
- Redesigning the site for the third time instead of writing review #7.
- Quitting at month four because traffic is flat — **traffic is always flat at
  month four.** The sites that win are the ones still publishing at month ten.
