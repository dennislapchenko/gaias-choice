## A familiar scene

You're standing at the fridge at 6 p.m., tired, a little hungry, and now there's a *rulebook* in your head: protein not with starch, fruit not after, melon alone, acids off the starch, sugars on their own... and the easy thing — just throw it all on a plate — starts to look very appealing again. This is the moment good rules go to die: not because they're wrong, but because six of them loose in your head at dinnertime is too much to carry.

So this chapter doesn't add a seventh rule. It does the opposite: it folds everything from chapters 2 and 3 into **one picture** you can glance at, and hands you a single simplifying principle that makes most of the rulebook unnecessary. By the end you'll have a Table Map you can actually cook from — and permission to stop here if you want to.

## The core

### The food categories

Before the list — try to recall from chapter 2: which foods counted as *neither* concentrated protein nor concentrated starch? Hold your answer a second. Those low-in-both foods are about to become the most useful category of all.

Every rule so far has really been about a handful of **categories**. Name them once and the rules stop being a list and become a shape:

- **Concentrated proteins** — meat, fish, eggs, cheese, nuts.
- **Concentrated starches** — bread, potato, rice, pasta, grains.
- **Non-starchy vegetables** — leafy greens, cucumber, celery, broccoli, courgette, peppers, onion. Low in both protein and starch.
- **Fats** — oil, butter, avocado.
- **Acid fruits** — citrus, pineapple, most berries, tomato.
- **Sweet fruits** — banana, dates, dried fruit.
- **Melons** — their own category, by the chapter-3 rule.
- **Milk** — its own awkward case, below.

Here's the whole thing as one chart for the fridge. It isn't a chemistry table — it's the shape of a good plate:

| With → | Protein | Starch | Non-starchy veg | Fat | Acid fruit | Sweet fruit |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Protein** | one kind at a time | **avoid** | **yes** | fair | avoid | avoid |
| **Starch** | **avoid** | yes | **yes** | yes | avoid | avoid |
| **Non-starchy veg** | **yes** | **yes** | yes | yes | fair | fair |
| **Fat** | fair | yes | yes | — | fair | fair |
| **Melon** | \*alone\* | \*alone\* | \*alone\* | \*alone\* | melon only | melon only |

Read across any row and the pattern jumps out: the two "avoid" cells that matter most are protein-with-starch (chapter 2) and anything sweet-or-acid stacked on a main meal (chapter 3). And one row is almost all "yes."

### The green bridge

That near-all-"yes" row is the most useful idea in the chapter: **raw non-starchy vegetables combine with almost everything.** Because they're low in both protein and starch, they don't demand acid *or* an uninterrupted alkaline window — they carry active enzymes and fibrous bulk and simply help the meal along. They are the **green bridge**: the thing you add to *any* meal to make it sit better.

This turns the whole practice into something you can do without thinking: **build the meal around one concentrated food — a protein or a starch, not both — and fill the rest of the plate with raw and lightly-cooked non-starchy vegetables.** That one habit satisfies the master rule automatically and makes almost any meal a well-combined one.

### Simpler digests easier

Under all the rules sits a single principle, and it's the one to keep if you keep nothing else: **the simpler the meal, the easier it digests.** The tradition's ideal is almost austere — one main food at a sitting, plenty of green around it. A plate with a protein, a starch, a sugar, an acid and a fat all fighting is the hard case; a plate with *one* concentrated food and a heap of vegetables is the easy one.

This is where trophology meets the whole spirit of this site — *less, but better* — at the most literal level: fewer things on the plate, better digested. Not a diet of denial, but of clarity. You're not eating less food; you're asking your stomach to do one job at a time instead of five at once. Most people find they can drop from "a bit of everything at every meal" to "one clear thing per meal" and feel the difference within days — which, of course, you'll check in your journal rather than take from us.

### Milk, honestly

Milk earns its own paragraph because the tradition makes strong claims about it and the truth is genuinely mixed. The combining rule itself is simple and uncontroversial-in-spirit: **milk combines poorly with almost everything** — it's a protein *and* a fat, and it curdles on contact with stomach acid, coating whatever else is there. If you drink milk, the food-combining guidance is "on its own, or not at all" — not washing down a meal.

Now the honest part. Reid goes much further — raw-versus-pasteurized claims, the Pottenger-cats study, assertions about calcium and disease. **We are not repeating those as established.** Some (like adult lactase decline being common) are real; others (sweeping human-health conclusions drawn from mid-century cat experiments) are not things you should take on faith, and the raw-milk debate carries genuine safety questions that belong to you and, for a child, a pediatrician — not to a food-combining course. So take the narrow, testable claim — *milk sits better alone* — into your journal, and leave the polemic where you can examine it yourself.

## Worked example

Time to finish the Table Map. You've been adding to it: protein/starch columns (chapter 2), a timing box (chapter 3). Now assemble the whole sheet so the family can cook from it:

1. **The combining chart** from above, small, in a corner.
2. **The green-bridge line**, big, across the top: *one concentrated food + lots of vegetables.* This is the rule you'll actually use.
3. **Your real meals**, sorted — the rebuilt protein meals and starch meals your family will actually eat, from chapters 2's work, now a proper list.
4. **The timing box** — fruit and melon and sweets, from chapter 3.

Then the honest test, comparing a "one clear thing" week to the mixed baseline:

> **22 July — week review.** Ran "one concentrated food + greens" most dinners. Journal shows lighter evenings 5 of 7 nights vs. the early-July mixed-plate baseline. The two heavy nights were both eating-out (couldn't control it) — noted for chapter 5. Fruit-in-the-morning stuck easily. Verdict so far: the green-bridge habit is the keeper; the finer acid/sugar rules matter less to me than protein-starch.

That last line is exactly what the journal is for: *you* deciding which rules earn their place in *your* body, not us deciding for you.

## Practice

This week:

1. **Finish the Table Map** as above — chart, green-bridge line, real meals, timing box — one clean sheet on the fridge.
2. **Cook three "green-bridge" dinners:** one concentrated food, half the plate vegetables, each night. Journal each at 60–90 minutes.
3. **Do one milk experiment** if milk is part of your life: have it alone one day, with a meal another, and journal the difference — your evidence, not Reid's.
4. **Write your interim verdict** in the journal: which rules clearly help you, which you can't tell yet, which you'll drop. Honesty includes "no difference."

**How to know it worked:** the Table Map is complete enough that someone else in the household could cook a well-combined meal from it without asking you — and your journal holds your own interim verdict, in your words.
