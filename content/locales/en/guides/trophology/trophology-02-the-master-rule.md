## A familiar scene

Look at the meals a whole culture calls normal: meat and potatoes. Burger and bun. Fish and chips. Eggs and toast. Chicken and rice. Sandwich. Pasta with meatballs. Every one of them is the same pairing — a concentrated protein sitting right on top of a concentrated starch. It's so universal it feels less like a choice than like what food *is*.

Remember from chapter 1 what the stomach does with that plate? Before reading on, try to recall it: which juice does the protein call for, which does the starch call for — and what happens when both arrive at once? Hold your answer a second before you keep reading.

Right: acid and pepsin for the protein, alkaline ptyalin for the starch, and the two cancelling into a weak, watery juice that handles neither well — the setup for fermentation and putrefaction. Of all the "different juices" clashes, this is the big one. If you change nothing else in this whole course, change this.

## The promise

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- state the master rule in one sentence and explain the mechanism behind it;
- tell a **concentrated** protein or starch from a food that only contains a little;
- explain why a whole food that naturally contains both (like a lentil or an oat) is *not* the same problem as a mixture;
- rebuild one of your family's staple meals so it no longer breaks the rule — the first tile of your Table Map.

## The core

### The master rule

Here it is, the spine of the whole practice:

> **Eat concentrated proteins and concentrated starches at separate meals.**

That's it. Meat, fish, eggs, cheese on one side; bread, potato, rice, pasta on the other; and — as a working rule — not the two together in one sitting. This single rule, Shelton and Reid both argue, prevents more digestive trouble than all the others combined, because the protein-plus-starch plate is the one nearly everyone eats at nearly every meal.

How to actually do it, without cooking two dinners:

- **Give the protein a vegetable partner instead of a starch.** Steak with a big salad and greens, not steak with fries. Fish with roasted non-starchy vegetables, not fish with chips.
- **Give the starch a vegetable partner instead of a protein.** Potatoes with butter and greens; pasta with vegetables and olive oil; rice with stir-fried vegetables — a fine meal that just doesn't also carry a slab of concentrated protein.
- **Split the classic combos across the day.** Reid's own example: toast or eggs at breakfast, the burger patty *or* the bun at lunch, meat *or* potatoes at dinner. Same foods, different sittings.

### Concentrated food

The rule hangs on one word: **concentrated**. It matters because almost no food is pure protein or pure starch — the point is which one *dominates*.

The rough working thresholds the tradition uses: a food counts as a **protein food** if it's about 15% protein or more (meat, fish, eggs, cheese, nuts), and a **starch food** if it's about 20% carbohydrate or more (bread, potato, rice, pasta, most grains). Non-starchy vegetables — leafy greens, cucumber, celery, broccoli, courgette — are low in both, which (as chapter 4 will make central) is exactly why they get along with everything.

So the rule isn't "never let a protein molecule near a starch molecule" — that's impossible. It's "don't put a *concentrated* protein and a *concentrated* starch in the same meal." A little cheese in a vegetable dish, a few croutons on a salad — minor. A chicken breast on a bed of rice — that's the pairing to break.

### A whole food is not a mixture

Here's the objection everyone raises, and it's a good one: beans, lentils, chickpeas and whole grains *naturally* contain both protein and starch. If the rule were iron, nature itself would be breaking it. So why do these sit fine?

Shelton's answer is the sentence to remember: **there's a difference between digesting one food, however complex, and digesting a mixture of foods.** To a single food that happens to be part-protein, part-starch, the body can time and tune its juices to that one thing's particular needs. It knows the package. But when you eat two *different* foods with two *different* demands — a steak and a potato, which want opposite conditions on opposite schedules — that precise tuning becomes impossible. The body can't run acid-for-hours and alkaline-now at the same time.

So: a lentil is a whole food, and the body meets it as one. Lentils *plus* a lamb chop is a mixture of two concentrated foods with opposite needs. The first is nature's design; the second is the culinary crime. Keep that line and most of trophology follows.

## Worked example

Let's rebuild an actual staple. Take the family's weeknight standby from chapter 1's journal: **chicken thighs, roast potatoes, salad** — a textbook protein-on-starch plate, the one that left the "heavy at 90 minutes" baseline.

Two honest ways to fix it, pick whichever you'll actually do:

- **Protein night:** chicken thighs, roast the potatoes' *place* full of non-starchy vegetables instead — courgette, peppers, onion, a big salad. The potatoes move to another day.
- **Starch night:** roast potatoes with butter, herbs, greens and that same salad — a genuinely satisfying plate — and the chicken moves to tomorrow's protein night with vegetables.

Notice what you did *not* do: you didn't eat less, you didn't count a calorie, you didn't buy anything. You took one meal apart along a single seam. Now the test — because we don't believe, we check:

> **12 July, 19:30 — dinner (rebuilt).** Chicken thighs, roasted courgette + peppers, big salad. No potato. Ate until full.
> **~21:00 (90 min):** Lighter than the 5 July baseline. Less bloated, didn't get that early crash. Still comfortably full.

One entry proves nothing — bodies vary, days vary. But now you have two lines to compare instead of a belief. Run the swap three or four times over a week or two and let the *pattern* in your journal, not this paragraph, tell you whether the master rule earns its place at your table.

## Practice

This week, in the kitchen — not on a screen:

1. **Pick one staple meal** your family eats often that breaks the rule (almost any "meat + [potato/rice/bread/pasta]" will do).
2. **Rebuild it two ways** on paper: a protein-plus-vegetables version and a starch-plus-vegetables version. Cook whichever you like this week.
3. **Journal the before and after** exactly as in chapter 1 — the meal, and two or three lines at 60–90 minutes. If you still have the 5 July baseline for a similar meal, note how they compare.
4. **Start the Table Map.** On the fridge sheet, draw two columns — "Protein meals" and "Starch meals" — and write your rebuilt meal into the right one. This is the first tile; chapters 3 and 4 fill in the rest.

**How to know it worked:** your Table Map has its first real meal on it, and your journal has a rebuilt-meal entry you can hold next to a baseline — whichever way it came out.
