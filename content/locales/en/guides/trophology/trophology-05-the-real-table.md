## A familiar scene

It's easy at your own fridge. Then you're at a friend's table and they've made lasagna with garlic bread and a fruit tart, and it's an act of love, and you're going to eat it. Or you're in a restaurant where every main is protein-on-starch. Or it's a holiday and the whole point is the feast. Four chapters of careful rules, and life just serves you the Great American Meal on a nice plate.

Scared this is where the whole thing falls apart? Honestly — a little. Rules that only work alone in your kitchen aren't a practice, they're a hobby. So this last chapter adds nothing new to memorize. It does the harder thing: it makes trophology *portable* — usable at a stranger's table, forgiving of a broken night — and it faces the question the whole course has been circling: how do you actually *know* this works for you, and not just believe it?

## The promise

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

- apply the rules in the messy real world — restaurants, guests, travel — with priorities, not perfection;
- name the Taoist table manners that matter as much as any combining rule;
- run your journal as a genuine test of the whole practice, and read its verdict honestly;
- decide, from your own recorded evidence, what to keep — and know where to learn more.

## The core

### Priorities, not perfection

You can't combine perfectly at other people's tables, so stop trying — and instead carry a **ranked** version of what you learned, so you spend your limited control where it matters most. In order:

1. **Protein and starch** — the master rule. If you fix only one thing at a hard table, fix this: take the fish *or* the bread, lean the plate toward one concentrated food. This alone catches most of the trouble.
2. **Nothing sweet or acid on top.** Skip the fruit tart *now*; it's the after-dinner-fermenting position from chapter 3. Have it tomorrow, alone, if you want it.
3. **The green bridge.** Ask for or reach for the salad and vegetables; make them half the plate whatever else is there. Green forgives a lot.
4. **Eat less of the clash.** When you genuinely can't uncombine — grandmother's one dish — eat a smaller portion of it, more of the vegetables, and let it go.

Let's deconstruct the hard case so you can see the priorities working. Here's the "Great American Meal" — cheeseburger, fries, milkshake — read through everything you now know:

| What's on the tray | Category | Rule it breaks |
|---|---|---|
| Beef patty + cheese | two concentrated proteins | one protein at a time |
| The bun | concentrated starch | protein-with-starch (the master rule) |
| Fries | more starch, deep-fried | starch + fat load on the protein |
| Milkshake | milk + sugar | milk alone; sugar on a full stomach |
| (no vegetables) | — | no green bridge at all |

Five or six clashes stacked in one meal — which is exactly why it sits like a stone. But watch how far *one* priority gets you: take the burger patty and cheese with the salad, skip the bun, the fries and the shake, and you've turned a six-clash meal into a clean one, at any counter, without a lecture to anybody.

### The manners under the rules

Reid spends as much time on *how* you eat as on *what* — and these Taoist table manners may matter more than any combining chart, because they help almost any meal, even a badly combined one:

- **Eat to 70–80% full.** The oldest rule in the book. The stomach can't cleanly handle what an eager, sedentary appetite piles in. Stop a little before "full."
- **Chew until it's liquid.** Starch digestion *starts* in the mouth (remember ptyalin from chapter 1) — chewing is not optional for it. "Drink your food and chew your beverages," as Gandhi put it. This one costs nothing and helps immediately.
- **No ice-cold drinks with meals**, and don't drown the meal in liquid — cold and volume both blunt the stomach's work. A little warm tea is kinder than a big cold soda.
- **Eat closer to raw and less processed** where you sensibly can — Reid's raw-food emphasis lands here, and it's the one place the Health lens (Asprey's toxin load, Chek's real food) and trophology fully agree: the less mangled the food, the more its own enzymes help digest it.

None of these is about combining at all — which is the point. On a night when the combining rules are hopeless, the manners still work.

### You are the judge

Here is the deepest thing the course has to teach, and it isn't a food rule at all. Four chapters gave you a method; this gives you the *authority* to judge it — and that authority is yours, not ours, not Reid's.

You've been keeping a digestion journal since chapter 1. That journal is now weeks long, and it is the only evidence that actually matters, because it's evidence about **your** body. Not a study on average people, not a Taoist text, not this website — *your* recorded before-and-after. The method for the rest of your life is a loop:

This is the site's founding stance made concrete: **don't believe — test.** Remember chapter 1's honest note — mainstream science doesn't endorse strict food combining, and the big historical claims don't hold. None of that is your problem now, because you're not being asked to *believe* trophology. You're asked to run it and read your own results. If your journal says the master rule makes your evenings lighter — that's true *for you*, and no meta-analysis overrules your own honest month. If your journal says a rule makes no difference to you — that's also true, and you drop it without guilt. Bio-individuality (Chek) and n=1 experiment (Asprey) aren't hedges here; they're the whole method. The journal is the judge, and you are the one who reads it honestly — including the failures.

## Worked example

The Summit's work is to grade the spine artifact against chapter 1's promise. Go back to your very first baseline entry — the "heavy at 90 minutes" mixed-plate dinner from chapter 1 — and read it next to your latest week.

> **29 July — course review.** Baseline (5 July): meat + potatoes, heavy and sleepy at 90 min, most nights.
> Now: "one concentrated food + green bridge" dinners, fruit moved to mornings, eating to ~80% full and actually chewing. Journal shows lighter evenings maybe 5–6 nights in 7. The chewing-and-portion change surprised me — bigger effect than some combining rules.
> **Verdict:** keeping — master rule, green bridge, fruit-timing, 80%-full, chewing. Unsure/dropping — the finer acid rules (no clear effect for me). Two hard nights this month were both social; priorities-not-perfection handled them fine.

That paragraph is the whole course delivered: not a belief you adopted, but a set of habits you *tested*, kept the ones that earned it, and can defend from your own evidence. That's trophology owned, not borrowed.

## Practice

This is the Summit — the practice is the final exam, so there's no separate task:

1. **Read your journal end to end.** First baseline to latest week. Mark the honest pattern — where meals got lighter, where nothing changed.
2. **Write your verdict**, as above: keep / unsure / drop, for each rule, from *your* evidence.
3. **Run one hard table on purpose** — eat out or at a friend's — and apply priorities-not-perfection. Journal how the ranked approach held up.
4. **Post the keepers** on your Table Map and let go of the rest. The finished artifact is the rules that survived contact with your own body.

**How to know it worked:** you can hold your first journal entry next to your last and say, in your own words and from your own evidence, what food combining is worth at your table — keeping what earned its place and dropping what didn't, with no need to believe anyone.
