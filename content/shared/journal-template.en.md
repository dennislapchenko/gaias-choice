<!-- ————— Shape 1: a story ————— -->

## What we set out to do

The plan, in a line or two — where we were going, what we expected, what the
day was supposed to look like.

## What actually happened

The real version. Concrete beats adjectives: the hour, the place, the one
detail you still remember. If the baby's rhythm rewrote the plan — it usually
does — say so plainly; that rhythm is often the real protagonist.

## The hard part

The moment it got difficult. Name it out loud — scared, tired, over budget —
naming the fear first is what makes the rest believable. Then what you tried.

## What worked (or what we'd change)

The way out, or the thing you'd do differently next time. One honest lesson
is enough — and it's allowed to be "we still don't know".

## One thing worth keeping

A gear item that earned its place, a small trick, a phrase — anything a
reader could actually use. Leave them something, the way you'd send a friend
home with a jar.

<!-- ————— Shape 2: a way we live ————— -->

## What we do

The practice in plain words — and what it replaced. One honest line on why
most people do it differently is welcome; no preaching needed, the doing is
the argument.

## How it came into our life

Approaches have origin stories too: the day, the problem, the person or the
book it started with. Start there — the story is what makes the rest land.

## An ordinary day with it

Concrete beats theory: the hour, the routine, the actual objects, what the
baby does meanwhile. Show it working on a normal Tuesday, not an ideal one.

## What it costs

Everything real has a price — money, time, nerves, the things we gave up for
it. Name it; the named cost is what makes the recommendation believable.

## If you want to try it

The smallest honest first step, and the one mistake we'd warn a friend away
from. Leave the reader somewhere to start.
